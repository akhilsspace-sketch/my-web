Period 1 Notes

Contact & Colonization

1491–1607 ~5% of exam 6 topics + comparison

Big Picture

Period 1 is the shortest period on the exam, but it lays the groundwork for everything that follows. When European, Native American, and African peoples were brought into sustained contact after 1492, the result was a massive exchange — of plants, animals, diseases, labor systems, and ideas about land and society — known as the Columbian Exchange. The patterns of conquest, labor, and cultural interaction established here by Spain, France, and England previewed the very different colonial societies Period 2 will explore.

1.1 Native American Societies Before European Contact

  • By 1491, North America was home to an enormous diversity of Indigenous societies, each adapted to its own environment — there was no single "Native American" way of life.
  • In the arid Southwest, Pueblo peoples built permanent adobe settlements and relied on irrigation-based agriculture to farm in a dry climate.
  • The Mississippian culture of the Eastern Woodlands built large, mound-centered cities (such as Cahokia) supported by maize agriculture and extensive trade networks — though many of these societies were already in decline by the time of European arrival.
  • On the Great Plains, groups combined hunting (especially bison) with seasonal farming; the horse-centered Plains culture often associated with these groups only developed after Europeans introduced horses.
  • In the Eastern Woodlands, the Iroquois Confederacy united multiple nations under a shared political structure with matrilineal kinship — an example of sophisticated diplomacy among Indigenous nations long before Europeans arrived.
  • A common thread across these diverse societies: land was generally treated as a shared, communal resource rather than private property — a fundamentally different concept from European land ownership that would become a major source of conflict.

1.2 European Exploration: Motives & Context

  • Renaissance-era advances — improved navigation tools (compass, astrolabe), new ship designs (the caravel), and better mapmaking — made long ocean voyages newly feasible for European sailors.
  • Motivations are often summarized as "God, Gold, and Glory": the desire to spread Christianity (intensified by Catholic–Protestant rivalry after the Reformation), the search for new trade routes and precious metals, and competition for national prestige among European monarchies.
  • Spain and Portugal led early exploration. The Treaty of Tordesillas (1494) divided the as-yet-unexplored "New World" between the two powers along a line of longitude — without any consultation of the peoples already living there.
  • Christopher Columbus's voyages, beginning in 1492 under Spanish sponsorship, opened the way for Spain's early dominance of the Americas during this period.

1.3 The Columbian Exchange

  • Sustained contact between the Eastern and Western Hemispheres after 1492 triggered a massive transfer of plants, animals, people, diseases, and ideas — the Columbian Exchange.
  • Old World → New World: horses, cattle, and pigs reshaped Native economies and landscapes; but devastating diseases — smallpox, measles, influenza — to which Indigenous peoples had no immunity caused catastrophic population collapse across the Americas in the decades after contact.
  • New World → Old World: crops like maize, potatoes, and tobacco transformed European diets, fueled population growth, and became major cash crops.
  • The introduction of horses was especially transformative for Great Plains societies, who adopted horse culture and fundamentally reshaped their hunting and warfare practices.
  • Silver mined in the Spanish Americas entered global trade networks — including transpacific trade with Asia — making the Columbian Exchange a truly worldwide phenomenon, not just an Atlantic one.

1.4 Spanish Exploration & Colonization

  • Spanish conquistadors — most famously Hernán Cortés against the Aztec Empire (1519–1521) — toppled powerful Indigenous empires using superior weaponry, alliances with rival Indigenous groups, and the demographic devastation caused by disease.
  • Within the territory that would become the United States, Spanish presence centered on Florida and the Southwest.
  • St. Augustine (founded 1565, in present-day Florida) is the oldest continuously occupied European-established settlement in the present-day U.S. — founded primarily for strategic and military reasons, to protect Spanish shipping routes.
  • Spanish missions spread through Florida and later the Southwest, aiming both to convert Indigenous peoples to Catholicism and to extend Spanish territorial claims.
  • Overall, Spanish colonization combined three goals: extracting mineral wealth, converting Indigenous populations to Catholicism, and securing strategic territorial control through forts (presidios) and missions.

1.5 Labor & Society in Spanish Colonial America

  • The encomienda system granted Spanish colonists the right to demand labor and tribute from Indigenous communities in exchange for (largely nominal) protection and religious instruction — in practice, it was frequently brutal and exploitative.
  • As Indigenous populations collapsed from disease and forced labor, Spanish colonists increasingly turned to enslaved Africans to meet labor demands, especially in mining and plantation agriculture.
  • A racial and social hierarchy known as the casta system developed across Spanish America, ranking people by ancestry — Spanish-born, American-born of Spanish descent, Indigenous, African, and various mixed-ancestry categories — and shaping legal rights, social status, and economic opportunity.
  • These labor and social patterns, established earliest in Spanish territories, previewed the racial hierarchies and forced-labor systems that would come to define large parts of colonial North America.

1.6 Other European Powers: Early Approaches

  • While Spain dominated this period, France and England were also probing the Americas — with very different goals and methods that foreshadow the colonial societies of Period 2.
  • France's earliest interest centered on the fur trade. Because French traders depended on Indigenous hunters and existing trade networks, early French contact tended to involve relatively cooperative, alliance-based relationships, and French settlement remained small in scale during this period.
  • England's early efforts were tentative and often failed. The Roanoke colony (established 1585 in present-day North Carolina) collapsed entirely, its fate unresolved — earning it the nickname the "Lost Colony."
  • English ventures during this period were primarily driven by commercial motives and a search for resources or a passage to Asia — the joint-stock company financing that would fund Jamestown (1607) was just beginning to take shape.

1.7 Comparison: Early European Colonization Approaches

The different starting approaches of Spain, France, and England in this period set patterns — toward Native peoples, labor, and land — that recur throughout the entire course.

Power Primary Goal Relationship with Native Peoples Labor System
Spain Extract mineral wealth, convert to Catholicism, secure territory Conquest and subjugation; missions sought religious conversion Encomienda, transitioning toward African slavery
France Fur trade and commercial profit Cooperative, alliance-based — dependent on Native trading partners Minimal formal labor system; trade-based economy
England Commercial ventures, resource extraction, search for a passage to Asia Initially limited and unstable (e.g., failed Roanoke colony) Not yet established — would develop rapidly after 1607

Exam Tip

This early comparison matters because Period 2 builds directly on it. Spain's conquest-and-conversion model, France's trade-alliance model, and England's still-undeveloped commercial model each set a trajectory for how Native relations and labor systems would evolve once permanent English colonization began in 1607.

Key Vocabulary

Columbian Exchange
The transfer of plants, animals, people, diseases, and ideas between the Eastern and Western Hemispheres after 1492.
Treaty of Tordesillas
1494 agreement dividing New World territorial claims between Spain and Portugal.
Conquistador
A Spanish conqueror, such as Cortés or Pizarro, who toppled Indigenous empires in the Americas.
Encomienda System
Spanish system granting colonists the right to forced labor and tribute from Indigenous communities.
Casta System
Spanish colonial social hierarchy that ranked people by ancestry and mixed heritage.
Mission System
Spanish religious outposts aimed at converting Indigenous peoples to Catholicism while extending territorial claims.
Mississippian Culture
Mound-building Indigenous societies of the Eastern Woodlands centered on maize agriculture and trade.
Iroquois Confederacy
A political alliance of multiple Native nations in the Eastern Woodlands with matrilineal kinship structures.
Roanoke
England's failed 1585 colony in present-day North Carolina, known as the "Lost Colony."
