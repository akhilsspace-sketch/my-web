Period 6 Notes

Industrialization & the Gilded Age

1877–1898 ~13% of exam 6 topics + comparison

Big Picture

The "Gilded Age" takes its name from a satirical Mark Twain novel — a thin layer of gold over something less valuable. Explosive industrial growth, mass immigration, rapid urbanization, and a transformed West reshaped American life, while stark inequality grew alongside it. Government largely stood back under a laissez-faire philosophy — until labor unions and the Populist movement began demanding a different relationship between government and the economy.

6.1 The Rise of Big Business & New Technologies

  • A "Second Industrial Revolution" introduced new industries at massive scale: steel (Andrew Carnegie, using the Bessemer process), oil (John D. Rockefeller's Standard Oil), and railroads, which formed the backbone of a truly national market.
  • New business structures — corporations, trusts, and holding companies — combined with strategies of vertical and horizontal integration to let a small number of firms dominate entire industries.
  • Contemporaries debated these figures fiercely: were they "captains of industry" who built America's industrial strength and practiced philanthropy (Carnegie's "Gospel of Wealth"), or "robber barons" who exploited workers and crushed competition through unethical practices? Both framings appear regularly on the exam.
  • Social Darwinism — applying "survival of the fittest" to economic competition — was used by some to justify minimal government regulation of business and the era's stark inequality.

6.2 Labor in the Gilded Age

  • Industrial work was often dangerous, low-paying, and involved long hours — including widespread use of child labor.
  • Workers organized into unions with different strategies: the Knights of Labor welcomed a broad range of workers, while the American Federation of Labor (AFL), led by Samuel Gompers, focused on skilled workers and concrete bargaining goals like wages and hours.
  • Major strikes — the Great Railroad Strike (1877), the Haymarket Affair (1886), the Homestead Strike (1892), and the Pullman Strike (1894) — often turned violent and were frequently suppressed with the help of state or federal troops.
  • These conflicts reflected a fundamental tension of the era: government generally sided with business against organized labor, raising questions about whether workers had a meaningful right to bargain collectively.

6.3 Immigration & Urbanization

  • A massive wave of "new immigration" arrived from Southern and Eastern Europe (Italy, Poland, Russia) as well as China and Japan — groups that differed in origin, religion, and culture from earlier immigrant waves.
  • Immigrants settled disproportionately in rapidly growing cities, often in crowded tenement housing with poor sanitation.
  • Nativist reactions included the Chinese Exclusion Act (1882) — the first major federal law restricting immigration based on nationality — alongside broader anti-immigrant sentiment, especially toward Catholics and Jews.
  • Urban political machines (e.g., Tammany Hall in New York) provided jobs and services to immigrant communities in exchange for political support — a system critics called corrupt, but which also met real needs that formal government left unaddressed.

6.4 The New South & the Trans-Mississippi West

  • Boosters promoted a "New South" built on industrialization and diversified agriculture — but in practice, the Southern economy remained heavily dependent on cotton, often through sharecropping and tenant farming systems that kept many Black and white farmers in debt.
  • Settlement of the trans-Mississippi West accelerated, encouraged by the Homestead Act, railroad construction, and the conclusion of armed conflicts with Native American nations on the Plains.
  • Federal Native American policy shifted toward forced assimilation: the Dawes Act (1887) broke up tribal landholdings into individual allotments, undermining traditional tribal structures and leading to massive loss of Native-held land.
  • The 1890 census declared the frontier "closed" — a symbolic marker, popularized by historian Frederick Jackson Turner, of the end of one phase of westward expansion.

6.5 Populism & Political Response

  • Farmers faced falling crop prices, high railroad shipping rates, and crushing debt — organizing first through the Grange and then the Farmers' Alliances.
  • The Populist (People's) Party emerged in the 1890s, advocating for free coinage of silver (to inflate currency and ease debt burdens), railroad regulation, a graduated income tax, and direct election of senators.
  • The Populists found some electoral success but struggled to build a lasting multiracial, cross-regional coalition — though many of their ideas (income tax, direct election of senators, railroad regulation) would later be adopted during the Progressive Era (Period 7).
  • The federal government's overall posture remained largely laissez-faire, though early regulatory steps appeared: the Interstate Commerce Act (1887, regulating railroads) and the Sherman Antitrust Act (1890, targeting monopolies) — both weakly enforced at first.

6.6 The Path to Overseas Expansion

  • By the 1890s, several factors pushed the U.S. toward overseas expansion: the search for new markets for industrial overproduction, ideological justifications drawing on Social Darwinism, and a renewed sense of national mission echoing Manifest Destiny.
  • Naval theorist Alfred Thayer Mahan's arguments about the strategic importance of sea power encouraged the U.S. to build a stronger navy and consider acquiring overseas bases.
  • The Spanish-American War (1898) — fought ostensibly over Cuban independence from Spain and inflamed by sensationalist "yellow journalism" — ended in quick U.S. victory and the acquisition of Puerto Rico, Guam, and the Philippines, plus a protectorate-like relationship with Cuba.
  • This war marks the close of Period 6 and the opening of Period 7's themes — the U.S. emerging as a global imperial power.

6.7 Comparison: Responses to Industrialization

Big business, labor, and farmers all responded to the same underlying transformation — industrial capitalism and the concentration of wealth — but proposed very different remedies.

Group Diagnosis of the Problem Proposed Solution Degree of Success (by 1898)
Big Business / Government Regulation would hinder economic growth and progress Laissez-faire; minimal government interference Largely successful through this period
Labor (Unions) Workers lack bargaining power and safe conditions Collective bargaining, strikes, union organization Limited — major strikes often suppressed
Farmers (Populists) Currency, railroads, and banks favor industrial elites over farmers Free silver, railroad regulation, income tax, direct election of senators Limited immediately, but many ideas adopted later

Exam Tip

A strong continuity-and-change essay notes that almost none of these Gilded Age proposals disappear — they resurface, often successfully, as Progressive Era reforms in Period 7. Period 6 is best understood as the problem-setting stage for Period 7's reform agenda.

Key Vocabulary

Bessemer Process
Steel manufacturing process that enabled mass production of cheap, strong steel.
Trust
A combination of companies organized to reduce competition and dominate an industry.
Social Darwinism
Application of "survival of the fittest" to justify economic inequality and minimal regulation.
Knights of Labor
Broad-based labor union open to most workers regardless of skill.
Chinese Exclusion Act
1882 law banning Chinese immigration, the first major federal restriction by nationality.
Dawes Act
1887 law breaking up tribal lands into individual allotments to promote assimilation.
Populist Party
1890s political party representing farmers, advocating free silver and railroad regulation.
Sherman Antitrust Act
1890 law aimed at breaking up monopolistic trusts, though weakly enforced at first.
Yellow Journalism
Sensationalist news coverage that helped build support for the Spanish-American War.
