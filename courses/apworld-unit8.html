Unit 8 Notes

Cold War & Decolonization

c. 1900–present ~8–10% of exam 6 topics + comparison

Big Picture

Two huge stories run in parallel after 1945: the Cold War — an ideological standoff between the US and USSR fought through proxies rather than direct war — and decolonization, as European empires collapsed and dozens of new nations had to figure out how to govern themselves, often while being pulled into the Cold War's orbit.

8.1 Origins of the Cold War

  • After WWII, the United States and Soviet Union emerged as the world's two superpowers with fundamentally opposed systems: capitalist democracy versus one-party communism.
  • Europe split into a Western bloc (aligned with the US, supported by Marshall Plan economic aid) and an Eastern bloc (Soviet-controlled communist governments) — what Churchill called the Iron Curtain. Germany itself was divided, with the Berlin Wall (built 1961) becoming the era's most visible symbol of division.
  • U.S. policy centered on containment — preventing the further spread of communism — formalized through military alliances: NATO (1949, Western bloc) and the Warsaw Pact (1955, Soviet bloc).
  • The conflict stayed "cold" between the superpowers themselves — direct war was avoided largely due to nuclear deterrence (mutually assured destruction) — but it played out through proxy conflicts elsewhere in the world.

8.2 The Cold War: Conflict & Competition

  • Proxy wars let the superpowers fight by supporting opposing sides without direct confrontation: the Korean War (1950–53) ended in stalemate, dividing Korea near the 38th parallel; the Vietnam War saw the U.S. support South Vietnam against the communist North, ending in 1975 with a communist victory and reunification.
  • The arms race drove rapid nuclear weapons development on both sides; the Cuban Missile Crisis (1962) brought the world to the brink of nuclear war before a negotiated resolution.
  • The Space Race — from Sputnik (1957) to the Apollo moon landing (1969) — was a symbolic competition for technological and ideological supremacy.
  • Beyond direct conflict, the rivalry extended into espionage, propaganda, and competition in culture, sports, and science.

8.3 Decolonization in Asia

  • WWII weakened European colonial powers and exposed the contradiction of fighting for "freedom" abroad while maintaining colonies — fueling independence movements across Asia and Africa.
  • India, under leaders including Mohandas Gandhi (who championed nonviolent civil disobedience), achieved independence in 1947 — but Partition into India and Pakistan along religious lines triggered massive violence and displacement between Hindu and Muslim populations.
  • Other major movements: Indonesia gained independence from the Netherlands, and Vietnam's struggle against France (the First Indochina War) set the stage for the later Vietnam War as a Cold War proxy conflict.
  • Many movements combined nationalist goals with a search for a development model — some aligned with the capitalist West, others with the communist bloc, and others sought a "third way" (see 8.5).

8.4 Decolonization in Africa

  • African independence accelerated through the 1950s–60s — 1960 alone is often called the "Year of Africa" for the sheer number of nations gaining independence.
  • Methods varied widely: Ghana (under Kwame Nkrumah) achieved independence in 1957 through relatively peaceful negotiation, becoming the first sub-Saharan African colony to do so — while Algeria's independence from France (1954–1962) came only after a long, violent war involving significant atrocities on both sides.
  • Pan-Africanism — an ideology promoting solidarity and unity among African peoples and former colonies — influenced cooperation between newly independent African states.
  • Many new states struggled with arbitrary colonial-era borders that had grouped together diverse ethnic groups with no shared history of self-governance — a major contributor to post-independence conflict in some regions.

8.5 Newly Independent Nations: Challenges & Strategies

  • New nations faced the dual challenge of building stable political institutions and developing economies often still tied to former colonial trade patterns — exporting raw materials while importing manufactured goods, a continuation sometimes called neocolonialism.
  • The Non-Aligned Movement — formalized at the Bandung Conference (1955) and the Belgrade Conference (1961) — let many newly independent "Third World" nations avoid choosing a Cold War side, instead pursuing cooperation among themselves.
  • Economic strategies varied: some pursued import substitution industrialization (building domestic industries to reduce reliance on imports), while others accepted aid and investment tied to alignment with either the U.S. or USSR.

8.6 The Cold War's End

  • By the 1980s, the Soviet economy strained under heavy military spending and systemic inefficiency. Soviet leader Mikhail Gorbachev introduced glasnost ("openness," allowing more political expression) and perestroika ("restructuring," economic reform).
  • These reforms — combined with growing nationalist movements within Soviet republics and Eastern Bloc countries — led to the collapse of communist governments across Eastern Europe in 1989 (including the fall of the Berlin Wall) and the dissolution of the Soviet Union itself in 1991.
  • The Cold War's end left the United States as the world's sole superpower (at least for a time) and produced a new wave of independent states from former Soviet republics.

8.7 Comparison: Paths to Independence

How a country won independence often shaped its politics for decades afterward — compare the method of independence to the post-independence challenge.

Country Path to Independence Cold War Alignment Major Post-Independence Challenge
India Nonviolent civil disobedience (Gandhi) Non-aligned (leading figure in the movement) Partition violence and communal conflict
Ghana Negotiated independence (1957) Pan-Africanist, sought non-alignment Political instability after independence
Algeria Prolonged armed revolutionary war vs. France Aligned with socialist/non-aligned bloc Post-independence authoritarian rule
Vietnam Armed struggle vs. France, then vs. U.S.-backed South Aligned with communist bloc (USSR/China) Reunification & reconstruction after prolonged war

Exam Tip

A strong essay links the method of independence to its aftermath — countries that won independence through prolonged armed struggle often emerged with powerful militaries and a tendency toward authoritarian, one-party rule, while negotiated transitions left more of the colonial administrative structure intact (for better or worse).

Key Vocabulary

Containment
U.S. Cold War policy aimed at preventing the spread of communism.
Iron Curtain
Term for the ideological and physical division between Soviet-controlled Eastern Europe and the West.
Proxy War
A conflict where major powers support opposing sides without fighting each other directly.
Non-Aligned Movement
Coalition of nations that avoided formal alignment with either Cold War superpower.
Pan-Africanism
Ideology promoting unity and solidarity among African peoples and former colonies.
Neocolonialism
Continued economic dependence of former colonies on developed nations after political independence.
Partition (India)
1947 division of British India into India and Pakistan along religious lines, causing mass violence and displacement.
Glasnost
Gorbachev-era policy of increased political openness in the Soviet Union.
Perestroika
Gorbachev-era policy of restructuring the Soviet economy.
