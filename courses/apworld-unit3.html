Unit 3 Notes

Land-Based Empires

c. 1450–1750 CE ~12–15% of exam 6 topics + comparison

Big Picture

After 1450, a handful of large land-based empires consolidated huge, religiously and ethnically diverse populations. Many relied on gunpowder weapons to expand quickly — earning the label "gunpowder empires" for the Ottomans, Safavids, and Mughals. The central question for every empire in this unit: once you've conquered a diverse population, how do you actually govern it?

3.1 Empires Expand

  • Beginning around 1450, several empires rapidly expanded using gunpowder weapons — cannons capable of breaching city walls and firearms that changed infantry warfare.
  • The term "gunpowder empires" is often applied to the Ottoman, Safavid, and Mughal Empires specifically, because gunpowder technology was central to their military success and rapid territorial growth.
  • Expansion brought each empire huge, diverse populations — different ethnicities, languages, and especially religions — under one ruler. How each empire handled this diversity becomes the central comparison of this unit.
  • Access to and control of trade routes (Mediterranean, Indian Ocean, overland Asian routes) and productive agricultural land were major drivers of where these empires expanded.

3.2 The Ottoman Empire

  • The Ottomans, a Turkic Muslim dynasty, captured Constantinople in 1453 — ending the Byzantine Empire and renaming the city Istanbul, their new capital.
  • The janissaries were an elite military corps recruited through the devshirme system — Christian boys from the Balkans were taken, converted to Islam, and trained for military or administrative careers, often rising to high-ranking positions.
  • The millet system let religious minorities (Christians, Jews) govern their own internal community affairs — marriage, inheritance, local disputes — under their own religious leaders, while the community as a whole paid the jizya tax to the Ottoman state.
  • The Ottomans controlled critical chokepoints between Europe and Asia (the Bosphorus, eastern Mediterranean), profiting from trade passing through their territory.
  • Under Suleiman the Magnificent (r. 1520–1566), the empire reached its territorial and cultural peak — expanding into the Balkans (sieging Vienna in 1529) and codifying Ottoman law.

3.3 The Safavid Empire

  • Founded by Ismail I in 1501, the Safavid Empire was centered in Persia (modern Iran).
  • Its defining feature: the Safavids made Shia Islam the empire's official state religion — a sharp contrast to the Sunni Ottoman and Mughal empires, and a major source of ongoing religious and political rivalry with the (Sunni) Ottomans.
  • Shah Abbas I brought the empire to its height — reorganizing the military with gunpowder weapons and a corps of slave-soldiers (similar in concept to the Ottoman janissaries), moving the capital to Isfahan, and promoting silk trade and monumental architecture.
  • Sitting geographically between the Ottoman and Mughal Empires, the Safavids were frequently drawn into conflict with both neighbors.

3.4 The Mughal Empire

  • Founded in 1526 by Babur (a descendant of Timur, with claimed lineage back to Genghis Khan), the Mughal Empire ruled over a Hindu-majority South Asia from a Muslim ruling class.
  • Akbar (r. 1556–1605) pursued religious tolerance: he abolished the jizya tax on non-Muslims, appointed Hindus to high administrative posts, and promoted Din-i-Ilahi, a syncretic court philosophy blending elements of multiple religious traditions.
  • Later emperors — most notably Aurangzeb — reversed this tolerance, reinstating the jizya and promoting Islamic orthodoxy, which increased tension with Hindu subjects and contributed to unrest.
  • Mughal architecture blended Persian, Islamic, and Indian styles — most famously in the Taj Mahal, built under Shah Jahan.
  • The economy rested on agricultural taxation (a land revenue system) and a thriving textile industry, especially cotton.

3.5 Ming & Qing China

  • The Ming Dynasty (1368–1644) restored Han Chinese rule after the Mongol Yuan Dynasty, reviving the Confucian scholar-bureaucracy and civil service examination system.
  • Ming China was initially outward-looking (Zheng He's voyages, Unit 2) but later turned more inward — restricting most direct foreign trade, though exchange continued through the tribute system, in which foreign states acknowledged Chinese superiority in exchange for trade privileges.
  • The Qing Dynasty (1644–1912) was established by the Manchus, a non-Han people from the northeast, who conquered Ming China.
  • The Qing ruled through a dual administration: they preserved the existing Han Confucian bureaucracy to govern the majority population, while maintaining a separate banner system that kept Manchu military and ethnic identity distinct from (and privileged above) the Han population.

3.6 Other Land-Based Empires

  • Songhai Empire (West Africa) succeeded Mali as the dominant power controlling trans-Saharan trade; Timbuktu and Gao remained centers of Islamic scholarship. Songhai fell in 1591 to Moroccan forces equipped with gunpowder weapons — illustrating how this military technology reshaped power balances even outside the major "gunpowder empires."
  • Russia expanded rapidly under rulers like Ivan IV ("the Terrible"), who took the title Tsar and pushed Russian territory eastward into Siberia. Russian Orthodox Christianity was closely tied to political authority, with the tsar holding significant influence over the church.
  • Tokugawa Japan: after the Tokugawa Shogunate unified Japan in 1603 (ending a long period of civil war), it enforced a rigid social hierarchy (samurai, peasants, artisans, merchants — in that order of status) and adopted sakoku, a policy that sharply restricted foreign contact to a few controlled ports (notably Nagasaki), limiting trade mainly to the Dutch and Chinese.

3.7 Comparison: Governing Diverse Empires

The throughline of Unit 3: every empire here had to decide how to treat religious/ethnic groups that differed from the ruling elite. Compare their approaches directly.

Empire Policy Toward Religious/Ethnic Minorities Administrative Structure Source of Ruler's Legitimacy
Ottoman Millet system: religious autonomy for non-Muslims in exchange for jizya tax Centralized bureaucracy + janissary corps (recruited via devshirme) Sultan claimed the title of caliph — political and religious authority
Safavid Imposed Shia Islam as state religion; less accommodating to Sunni subjects Centralized monarchy with a slave-soldier military corps Shah's authority tied to Shia religious leadership
Mughal Varied by ruler: Akbar's tolerance (abolished jizya, syncretic Din-i-Ilahi) vs. Aurangzeb's orthodoxy (reinstated jizya) Centralized bureaucracy; land revenue (mansabdari) system Mix of Islamic kingship and (under Akbar) syncretic religious authority
Qing China Dual system: Confucian governance for Han majority, separate Manchu banner system for ruling ethnic group Confucian scholar-bureaucracy + Manchu military banners Mandate of Heaven, while preserving distinct Manchu identity

Exam Tip

Don't just say an empire was "tolerant" or "intolerant" — AP graders want specific mechanisms. "The Ottomans allowed religious diversity" is vague; "the millet system let non-Muslim communities maintain their own courts for internal legal matters, in exchange for the jizya tax" is the kind of detail that earns points.

Key Vocabulary

Gunpowder Empires
The Ottoman, Safavid, and Mughal Empires — so named because firearms and cannons were central to their rapid expansion.
Janissaries
Elite Ottoman military corps recruited through the devshirme system.
Devshirme
Ottoman practice of recruiting Christian boys from the Balkans, converting them to Islam, and training them for military/administrative service.
Millet System
Ottoman policy granting religious minorities self-governance over internal community affairs in exchange for the jizya tax.
Shah
Title used by Safavid (and earlier Persian) rulers.
Din-i-Ilahi
Syncretic court philosophy promoted by Mughal emperor Akbar, blending elements of multiple religions.
Mansabdari System
Mughal system ranking officials and assigning them land revenue rights in exchange for military/administrative service.
Tribute System
Diplomatic/trade framework in which foreign states acknowledged Chinese superiority in exchange for trade access.
Banner System
Qing Dynasty military/social organization that kept Manchu identity and privilege distinct from the Han Chinese majority.
Sakoku
Tokugawa Japan's policy of restricting foreign trade and contact to a few controlled ports.
Tsar
Title adopted by Russian rulers beginning with Ivan IV, asserting supreme political authority.
