Unit 5 Notes

Political Participation

Linkage Institutions ~25% of exam 8 topics + comparison

Big Picture

Unit 5 covers the linkage institutions that connect citizens' preferences to government action: political parties, elections, campaign finance, interest groups, and the media. The American two-party system is shaped heavily by structural rules — particularly winner-take-all elections — and these institutions interact constantly: how parties are funded, how districts are drawn, how groups organize, and how the media frames it all shape who runs, who wins, and what policies result.

5.1 Political Parties: Functions & the Two-Party System

  • Political parties perform several core functions: recruiting and nominating candidates, mobilizing voters, structuring electoral choice around competing sets of policy positions, and organizing government once in office (committee assignments, leadership).
  • The U.S. has long had a two-party system, reinforced structurally by winner-take-all, single-member district elections — systems where only one candidate per district can win tend to produce two dominant parties, since votes for smaller parties are often seen as "wasted."
  • Proportional representation systems, by contrast, allocate seats according to vote share and tend to produce multiparty systems — a contrast that helps explain why third parties struggle in the U.S. despite occasionally raising issues that major parties later adopt.
  • Third parties also face structural disadvantages beyond the electoral system itself — ballot access requirements and exclusion from candidate debates further limit their competitiveness.

5.2 Party Realignment & Ideology

  • Realignment is a long-term shift in the demographic and ideological coalitions that make up each party's base, often triggered by major events — the Great Depression produced the "New Deal coalition" that anchored the Democratic Party for decades, while the Civil Rights era saw many Southern white voters gradually shift toward the Republican Party.
  • Party platforms broadly reflect the liberal/conservative ideological spectrum from Unit 4, though both major parties contain significant internal ideological diversity.
  • Polarization (see Unit 2) has been accompanied by increased "sorting" — voters' party identification increasingly aligns with their ideology, shrinking the pool of ideologically moderate voters within either party.

5.3 Elections: Primaries, Caucuses & the Electoral College

  • Primaries are elections within a party to select its nominee — "open" primaries allow any voter to participate, while "closed" primaries restrict participation to registered party members.
  • Caucuses are local gatherings where party members discuss and vote for candidates directly — once common, now used by only a handful of states.
  • The general presidential election uses the Electoral College: each state receives electoral votes equal to its congressional delegation (House seats plus two senators), and nearly all states award their electoral votes winner-take-all (Maine and Nebraska are exceptions).
  • Because of winner-take-all allocation, the Electoral College can produce a president who did not win the national popular vote (as in 2000 and 2016) — a recurring source of debate about electoral reform.

5.4 Campaign Finance

  • Campaign finance is regulated primarily by the Federal Election Campaign Act (FECA, 1971) and later reforms, which established contribution limits and disclosure requirements.
  • PACs (Political Action Committees) raise and spend money to support candidates, subject to contribution limits set by federal law.
  • Citizens United v. FEC (2010) ruled that corporations and unions have a First Amendment right to make unlimited independent expenditures on political speech — spending not coordinated directly with a candidate's campaign.
  • This ruling led to the rise of Super PACs, which can raise and spend unlimited sums on elections as long as they do not coordinate directly with candidates — fueling ongoing debate over the tension between free speech ("money as speech") and concerns about wealth's influence on elections.

5.5 Interest Groups

  • An interest group is an organization that seeks to influence public policy without itself running candidates for office — the key distinction from a political party.
  • Interest groups influence policy through lobbying (direct contact with policymakers), grassroots mobilization, litigation, and providing specialized information to legislators and agencies.
  • Groups vary widely in type — economic groups (business associations, labor unions), public interest groups (environmental or civil rights organizations), and single-issue groups.
  • Two competing theories describe their overall effect: pluralist theory holds that many competing groups balance each other out, while elitist theory argues that a small number of wealthy, well-organized groups exert disproportionate influence — both connect to the "iron triangle" concept from Unit 2.

5.6 Voter Behavior & Turnout

  • Turnout is shaped by socioeconomic status (education and income correlate positively with turnout), age (older voters turn out at higher rates), registration requirements, and how competitive a race is perceived to be.
  • Vote choice is shaped most strongly by party identification, along with candidate characteristics, retrospective voting (evaluating an incumbent's past performance), and specific issues.
  • Redistricting and gerrymandering (see Unit 2's discussion of Shaw v. Reno) can reduce the competitiveness of individual races, which in turn can affect turnout incentives in those districts.
  • U.S. voter turnout is lower than in many other democracies, a gap often attributed to the country's "opt-in" voter registration system, which places the administrative burden of registering on individual citizens rather than the government.

5.7 The Role of Media in Politics

  • The media functions as a linkage institution by informing citizens about candidates and issues, setting the political agenda — influencing which issues receive public attention — and providing a forum for political debate.
  • Media also serves a "watchdog" function: investigative journalism that holds government accountable, as with the Pentagon Papers reporting connected to New York Times v. United States (Unit 3).
  • Media has evolved dramatically — from print newspapers, to broadcast radio and television (which created shared national narratives), to 24-hour cable news (more partisan, continuous), to internet and social media (highly fragmented and personalized).
  • This evolution has reshaped how candidates communicate directly with voters — connecting to the "going public" strategy discussed in Unit 2 — and how citizens encounter political information day to day.

5.8 Media's Influence, Bias & the Changing Media Landscape

  • Media bias refers to concerns that outlets favor particular political perspectives through story selection, framing, or commentary.
  • "Horse-race journalism" describes coverage that focuses on who is winning or losing — polls and campaign strategy — rather than substantive policy differences.
  • In a fragmented media environment, individuals increasingly consume content that reinforces their existing views — sometimes called "echo chambers" or filter bubbles — a dynamic that can deepen the polarization discussed in Unit 2 and Topic 5.2 above.
  • Debates over the media's role connect back to First Amendment principles from Unit 3 — balancing a free press's watchdog function against concerns about its influence on democratic discourse.

5.9 Comparison: Political Parties vs. Interest Groups

Both are "linkage institutions" connecting citizens to government, but they operate very differently.

Political Parties Interest Groups
Primary Goal Win elections and control government Influence policy on specific issues
Candidates Nominate and run candidates under their label Do not run their own candidates (may endorse)
Scope of Issues Broad platforms covering many issues Typically narrow, issue-specific focus
Example Democratic Party, Republican Party NRA, Sierra Club, AARP

Exam Tip

A common exam trap is confusing PACs or interest groups with parties. Remember: interest groups try to influence policy from outside government and do not field their own candidates, while parties seek to win control of government directly through their own nominees.

Key Concepts & Milestones

Two-Party System
Reinforced structurally by winner-take-all, single-member district elections.
New Deal Coalition (1930s)
Major realignment that reshaped the Democratic Party's base for decades.
Southern Realignment (1960s–1980s)
The Civil Rights era gradually shifted many Southern white voters toward the Republican Party.
FECA (1971)
Established the modern framework for regulating campaign contributions and spending.
Citizens United v. FEC (2010)
Ruled that corporations and unions have a First Amendment right to make unlimited independent political expenditures.
Rise of Super PACs (post-2010)
Groups that may raise and spend unlimited funds on elections but cannot coordinate directly with campaigns.
Agenda Setting

Echo Chambers
Fragmented media environments that reinforce existing views.

Key Vocabulary

Two-Party System
A political system dominated by two major parties, reinforced by winner-take-all elections.
Realignment
A long-term shift in the demographic and ideological coalitions of a party's base.
Electoral College
The body of electors, allocated by state, that formally selects the president.
Winner-Take-All
An electoral system where the candidate with the most votes receives all of a state's electoral votes.
PAC / Super PAC
Organizations that raise and spend money to support candidates; Super PACs face no spending limit but cannot coordinate with campaigns.
Interest Group
An organization seeking to influence policy without running its own candidates.
Linkage Institution
A channel connecting citizens' preferences to government policy, such as parties or media.
Media Bias
A tendency of media coverage to favor particular political perspectives.
Agenda Setting
The media's role in influencing which issues the public considers important.
Horse-Race Journalism
Coverage emphasizing campaign strategy and polling over policy substance.
Echo Chamber
A media environment where people mainly encounter views that reinforce their own.
