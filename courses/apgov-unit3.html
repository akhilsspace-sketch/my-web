Unit 3 Notes

Civil Liberties & Civil Rights

Bill of Rights & 14th Amdt. ~15% of exam 7 topics + comparison

Big Picture

Unit 3 covers the rights and protections individuals hold against government power (civil liberties) and the protections ensuring government treats people equally (civil rights). Both categories are defined less by the bare constitutional text than by over two centuries of Supreme Court interpretation — making this unit especially case-heavy, and a major source of Concept Application and SCOTUS Comparison questions on the exam.

3.1 The Bill of Rights & Selective Incorporation

  • The Bill of Rights originally restricted only the federal government — it did not limit what state governments could do.
  • The 14th Amendment's Due Process Clause (1868) became the vehicle for selective incorporation — the gradual, case-by-case process by which the Supreme Court applied most Bill of Rights protections to state governments as well.
  • McDonald v. Chicago (2010) incorporated the 2nd Amendment right to keep and bear arms against the states — a case explored further in Topic 3.4.
  • Nearly all major Bill of Rights protections have now been incorporated, though a few minor provisions (such as the right to a grand jury indictment) technically have not.

3.2 Freedom of Religion: Establishment & Free Exercise

  • The First Amendment's Establishment Clause prohibits government from establishing an official religion or favoring one religion (or religion generally) over others.
  • Engel v. Vitale (1962) ruled that state-sponsored prayer in public schools violates the Establishment Clause, even if students are not required to participate.
  • The Free Exercise Clause protects individuals' right to practice their religion — though this right is not absolute, as government can restrict religious practice for sufficiently compelling reasons.
  • Wisconsin v. Yoder (1972) ruled that Amish parents could not be compelled to send their children to school past 8th grade, citing Free Exercise rights over the state's interest in compulsory education.
  • These two clauses can be in tension: accommodating religious practice (Free Exercise) can sometimes look like favoring religion (raising Establishment concerns).

3.3 Freedom of Speech & Press

  • The First Amendment protects freedom of speech and the press, but this protection is not unlimited — government can regulate speech under certain narrow circumstances.
  • Schenck v. United States (1919) established the "clear and present danger" test, upholding limits on speech that could pose a serious threat (though later cases narrowed this standard considerably).
  • Tinker v. Des Moines (1969) established that students retain free speech rights at school, including symbolic speech (wearing armbands to protest the Vietnam War) — though schools may restrict speech that substantially disrupts the educational environment.
  • New York Times Co. v. United States (1971), the "Pentagon Papers" case, set a very high bar for "prior restraint" — government generally cannot block publication before it happens, even when the material may be damaging.
  • Together, these cases show the Court balancing free expression against other interests — national security, school order — while maintaining a strong presumption against government censorship.

3.4 The Second Amendment: Right to Bear Arms

  • The Second Amendment states that "the right of the people to keep and bear Arms, shall not be infringed" — for most of American history, courts generally treated this right as connected to militia service rather than as a freestanding individual right.
  • District of Columbia v. Heller (2008) marked a major shift: the Court held for the first time that the Second Amendment protects an individual right to possess a firearm for traditionally lawful purposes, such as self-defense in the home, independent of militia service.
  • McDonald v. Chicago (2010) — introduced in Topic 3.1 — incorporated this individual right against state and local governments via the 14th Amendment, meaning state and local gun laws (not just federal ones) must satisfy the standard set in Heller.
  • The Court has continued to define the limits of permissible regulation: New York State Rifle & Pistol Assn. v. Bruen (2022) struck down a state law requiring applicants to show "proper cause" for a concealed-carry permit, holding that gun regulations must be consistent with the nation's "historical tradition of firearm regulation" — fueling ongoing debate over which modern restrictions (background checks, assault weapons bans, magazine limits) can survive this test.

3.5 Due Process & Rights of the Accused

  • The 4th, 5th, 6th, and 8th Amendments protect individuals accused of crimes — covering unreasonable searches and seizures, self-incrimination, double jeopardy, the right to counsel, the right to a speedy and public jury trial, and protection from cruel and unusual punishment.
  • Gideon v. Wainwright (1963) established that states must provide an attorney to criminal defendants who cannot afford one, incorporating the 6th Amendment's right to counsel via the 14th Amendment.
  • The "exclusionary rule" prevents illegally obtained evidence from being used in court, enforcing 4th Amendment protections against unreasonable searches.
  • These protections reflect a fundamental tension in the justice system between effective law enforcement and safeguarding individuals against government power — a tension that recurs in debates over civil rights below.

3.6 The Right to Privacy & Its Controversies

  • Though not explicitly named in the Constitution, the Supreme Court has recognized an implied right to privacy, drawn from several amendments (3rd, 4th, 5th, 9th, and 14th).
  • This implied right became the basis for some of the Court's most controversial rulings — Roe v. Wade (1973) initially established a constitutional right to abortion grounded in this privacy right.
  • Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization (2022) later overturned Roe, holding that the Constitution does not confer a right to abortion and returning the issue to state regulation.
  • This debate exemplifies the broader "originalism vs. living Constitution" disagreement covered in Unit 2 — should constitutional rights be limited to those explicitly enumerated, or can courts recognize new rights implied by the document's broader principles?

3.7 Civil Rights: Equal Protection & the Struggle for Equality

  • The 14th Amendment's Equal Protection Clause prohibits states from denying any person "equal protection of the laws" — the constitutional basis for civil rights litigation.
  • Brown v. Board of Education (1954) declared school segregation unconstitutional, overturning the "separate but equal" doctrine and becoming a landmark application of the Equal Protection Clause.
  • The civil rights movement combined litigation (the NAACP's legal strategy) with nonviolent direct action; Martin Luther King Jr.'s "Letter from Birmingham Jail" — a required foundational document — argued that unjust laws must be challenged, even through civil disobedience, when legal channels fail to deliver justice.
  • Later debates over civil rights have included affirmative action — policies aimed at remedying past discrimination — which the Court has subjected to strict scrutiny and limited in scope over time.

3.8 Comparison: Civil Liberties vs. Civil Rights

These two terms are often confused, but they describe different relationships between individuals and government.

Civil Liberties Civil Rights
Definition Protections from government action — things government cannot do to you Protections ensured by government — guarantees of equal treatment
Constitutional Basis Primarily the Bill of Rights (1st–8th Amendments) Primarily the 14th Amendment's Equal Protection Clause
Example Freedom of speech, freedom of religion, right to bear arms Protection from discrimination based on race or sex
Key Case Tinker v. Des Moines Brown v. Board of Education

Exam Tip

A useful framing: civil liberties are about what government cannot do to you; civil rights are about ensuring government treats everyone equally — and sometimes require government action to remedy historical inequality rather than government restraint.

Key Vocabulary

Civil Liberties
Individual freedoms protected from government interference, mainly via the Bill of Rights.
Civil Rights
Protections ensuring equal treatment under the law, mainly via the 14th Amendment.
Selective Incorporation
Process of applying Bill of Rights protections to state governments case by case.
Establishment Clause
First Amendment provision barring government from establishing or favoring a religion.
Free Exercise Clause
First Amendment provision protecting individuals' right to practice their religion.
Second Amendment
Protects an individual right to keep and bear arms, as interpreted in District of Columbia v. Heller (2008).
Due Process
Constitutional guarantee of fair legal procedures before government deprives someone of life, liberty, or property.
Equal Protection Clause
14th Amendment provision requiring states to treat people equally under the law.
Symbolic Speech
Nonverbal expression (such as protest signs or armbands) protected as free speech.
Affirmative Action
Policies intended to remedy past discrimination in education and employment.
