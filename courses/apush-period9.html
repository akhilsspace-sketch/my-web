Period 9 Notes

The Recent Past

1980–present ~5% of exam 6 topics + comparison

Big Picture

Period 9 is the shortest period on the exam, covering roughly the last four-plus decades. Its central theme is a renewed national debate over a question that runs through the entire course: what should the role of government be? A conservative resurgence beginning with Reagan pushed back against the liberal consensus of Periods 7–8, even as the country grew more diverse, more globally interconnected, and — especially after 2001 — newly focused on national security.

9.1 The Conservative Resurgence: Reagan & "Reaganomics"

  • Ronald Reagan's election (1980) reflected a broader conservative movement built since the 1960s–70s, combining economic conservatism (lower taxes, deregulation, reduced spending on social programs), social conservatism often tied to evangelical Christian voters, and a more assertive anti-Soviet foreign policy.
  • "Reaganomics" — tax cuts, deregulation, and increased military spending — aimed to stimulate growth through "supply-side economics," but also produced large federal budget deficits.
  • Reagan's foreign policy combined a harder line toward the Soviet Union (military buildup, support for anti-communist movements abroad) with eventual diplomacy — arms control agreements with Soviet leader Mikhail Gorbachev in the later 1980s.
  • The conservative movement reshaped the Republican Party and national political debate for decades, even as liberal advocacy on civil rights and environmental policy continued.

9.2 The End of the Cold War & 1990s Foreign Policy

  • The Soviet Union's collapse (1991) — following Gorbachev's reforms and the fall of communist governments across Eastern Europe in 1989 — ended the Cold War and left the U.S. as the world's sole superpower, at least for a time.
  • The U.S. led a multinational coalition in the Gulf War (1991) against Iraq after its invasion of Kuwait — a quick military victory some saw as establishing a "New World Order" of U.S.-led international cooperation.
  • Economic globalization accelerated through agreements like NAFTA (1994) and the World Trade Organization (1995), deepening economic integration — though its benefits were unevenly distributed, with manufacturing job losses concentrated in certain regions.
  • Domestically, the 1990s ended with a federal budget surplus and continuing debate over the size and role of government, including welfare reform in 1996.

9.3 Demographic & Economic Change

  • Following the 1965 immigration reform (Period 8), immigration patterns shifted significantly — in recent decades, immigrants have increasingly come from Latin America and Asia, reshaping the demographic composition of the U.S.
  • The "Digital Revolution" — personal computers, the internet, and later smartphones — transformed communication, the economy (the rise of major technology companies), and daily life.
  • Economic inequality grew significantly from the 1980s onward, with income gains increasingly concentrated among higher earners — a trend that became a major point of political debate.
  • Deindustrialization continued to affect traditional manufacturing regions, even as new tech-centered economic hubs grew rapidly elsewhere.

9.4 Post-9/11: The War on Terror & Domestic Politics

  • The September 11, 2001 terrorist attacks led to a major reorientation of U.S. policy: the War on Terror, the invasion of Afghanistan (2001) to remove the Taliban government that had harbored al-Qaeda, and the more controversial invasion of Iraq (2003).
  • Domestically, the USA PATRIOT Act expanded government surveillance powers, and the Department of Homeland Security was created — reflecting a new emphasis on national security that raised civil liberties concerns for some.
  • These conflicts proved long and costly, reviving Vietnam-era debates about the proper scope and limits of U.S. military intervention abroad.

9.5 The 2008 Financial Crisis & Its Aftermath

  • The 2008 financial crisis — triggered by the collapse of a housing market bubble fueled by risky mortgage lending — caused the worst economic downturn since the Great Depression, with major job losses and a severe credit crunch.
  • The federal government responded with significant intervention: the Troubled Asset Relief Program (TARP), bailing out major financial institutions, and, under the Obama administration, a stimulus package and new financial regulations (the Dodd-Frank Act, 2010).
  • The crisis also led to the Affordable Care Act (2010) — a major expansion of health insurance coverage that echoed earlier debates from the Great Society (Period 8) about government's role in citizens' welfare.
  • The slow recovery and continued economic anxiety fed political movements on both the left (Occupy Wall Street, concerns about inequality) and the right (Tea Party, concerns about government spending and debt).

9.6 Continuing Debates: Polarization & Social Change

  • Political polarization increased significantly in recent decades, with growing ideological and geographic divides between the parties and increasingly contentious debates over immigration, healthcare, and climate change.
  • Social change continued: same-sex marriage was legalized nationwide (Obergefell v. Hodges, 2015), part of a broader, ongoing expansion of civil rights debates that have run throughout the course.
  • Climate change emerged as a major and increasingly urgent policy debate, extending the environmental concerns of Period 8 alongside new scientific understanding of global environmental change.
  • As in earlier periods, these debates over rights, government's role, and national identity connect to questions that have recurred since the nation's founding — the AP US History course's big questions remain unresolved and ongoing.

9.7 Comparison: Visions of Government's Role Since 1980

The debate over the proper role of government is the throughline of the entire course — from Federalists vs. Anti-Federalists to the New Deal's critics. Since 1980, that debate has played out along these lines.

Conservative Position Liberal Position
Economic Policy Lower taxes, deregulation, smaller government role Greater regulation, social safety net expansion (e.g., ACA)
Social Policy Often emphasizes traditional values, limits on federal mandates Often emphasizes expanding civil rights protections
Foreign Policy Strong military posture, assertive use of force Mixed — varies between intervention and restraint depending on era

Exam Tip

Many "evaluate the extent of change" prompts hinge on recognizing this recurring debate — Hamilton vs. Jefferson, Whigs vs. Democrats, Progressive reform vs. laissez-faire, the New Deal vs. its critics, and now the post-1980 conservative/liberal divide are all variations on the same fundamental question about the size and scope of government. Framing an essay around this throughline is one of the strongest moves available in APUSH.

Key Vocabulary

Reaganomics
Economic policy combining tax cuts, deregulation, and increased military spending.
Supply-Side Economics
Theory that tax cuts for businesses and high earners spur broad economic growth.
NAFTA
1994 agreement creating a free trade zone among the U.S., Canada, and Mexico.
Globalization
Increasing economic and cultural interconnection across national borders.
War on Terror
U.S. military and policy response to terrorism following the September 11 attacks.
USA PATRIOT Act
2001 law expanding government surveillance powers after 9/11.
2008 Financial Crisis
Severe economic downturn caused by the collapse of the housing market bubble.
Affordable Care Act
2010 law significantly expanding access to health insurance.
Polarization
Growing ideological divide between political parties in recent decades.
