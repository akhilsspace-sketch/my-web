Period 5 Notes

Civil War & Reconstruction

1848–1877 ~13% of exam 7 topics + comparison

Big Picture

Period 5 covers the bloodiest and most transformative chapter in American history: the collapse of the sectional compromises that had held the Union together, four years of civil war that ended slavery, and over a decade of Reconstruction that attempted — and ultimately failed — to remake Southern society on the basis of racial equality.

5.1 The Sectional Crisis Intensifies

  • The Compromise of 1850 — admitting California as a free state, enacting a stronger Fugitive Slave Act, and allowing popular sovereignty in new territories — attempted to settle disputes over the Mexican-American War's territorial gains, but satisfied no one fully.
  • The stronger Fugitive Slave Act outraged many Northerners by requiring their cooperation in returning escaped enslaved people; Harriet Beecher Stowe's Uncle Tom's Cabin (1852) galvanized anti-slavery sentiment in response.
  • The Kansas-Nebraska Act (1854) allowed popular sovereignty in territories north of the Missouri Compromise line — effectively repealing that 1820 compromise — and triggered violent conflict between pro- and anti-slavery settlers known as "Bleeding Kansas."
  • The party system reorganized around slavery: the Whig Party collapsed, and the new Republican Party formed explicitly opposing slavery's expansion into the territories.
  • The Dred Scott decision (1857) — ruling that Congress could not ban slavery in the territories and that African Americans were not citizens — further inflamed sectional tensions.

5.2 The Road to War: Election of 1860 & Secession

  • John Brown's raid on Harpers Ferry (1859) — an attempted slave uprising — terrified the South and was celebrated by some abolitionists, deepening mutual distrust between the sections.
  • The election of 1860 split sharply along sectional lines; Abraham Lincoln won without a single electoral vote from the South, on a platform opposing the expansion of slavery.
  • Southern states began seceding even before Lincoln took office, explicitly citing the protection of slavery as a central justification in their declarations of secession.
  • The Confederate States of America formed, and fighting began at Fort Sumter in April 1861.

5.3 The Civil War: Strategy & Turning Points

  • The Union's strategy combined a naval blockade of Southern ports, control of the Mississippi River, and — by the war's later stages — total war tactics (e.g., Sherman's March to the Sea) aimed at destroying the South's capacity and will to fight.
  • Early Confederate military success under skilled generals like Robert E. Lee gradually gave way to Union advantages in manpower, industrial capacity, and resources as the war dragged on.
  • The Battle of Antietam (1862) — a tactical draw but strategic Union victory — gave Lincoln the political opening to issue the Emancipation Proclamation.
  • Gettysburg and Vicksburg (both 1863) marked the war's turning point, after which the Confederacy remained largely on the defensive until Lee's surrender at Appomattox in April 1865.

5.4 The Civil War: Emancipation & Expanded Government Power

  • The Emancipation Proclamation (1863) — though limited in immediate practical effect since it applied only to Confederate-controlled territory — reframed the war's purpose as a fight against slavery and opened the door to formal abolition.
  • Roughly 180,000 African American soldiers served in the Union Army (United States Colored Troops), demonstrating both a major military contribution and a claim to citizenship rights.
  • The war drove an enormous expansion of federal government power: the first federal income tax, a national paper currency ("greenbacks"), the Homestead Act and Pacific Railway Acts encouraging westward settlement and infrastructure, and the first military draft.
  • These wartime expansions of federal authority set important precedents for the federal government's role in American life going forward.

5.5 Reconstruction: Presidential vs. Congressional Approaches

  • Reconstruction debates centered on a fundamental question: on what terms would former Confederate states rejoin the Union, and what rights would newly-freed African Americans have?
  • Lincoln's, and later Andrew Johnson's, more lenient approach favored quick reunification with relatively easy terms for former Confederates.
  • Congressional ("Radical") Republicans favored a more thorough transformation of Southern society, including stronger protections for freedpeople's rights — this approach prevailed after Johnson's repeated clashes with Congress, including his impeachment (though he was not removed from office).
  • The Reconstruction Amendments — the 13th (abolishing slavery), 14th (citizenship and equal protection), and 15th (voting rights regardless of race) — represent the era's most significant and lasting legal achievements.

5.6 Reconstruction in the South: Successes & Limits

  • The Freedmen's Bureau provided education, legal assistance, and other aid to formerly enslaved people, helping establish schools throughout the South.
  • African Americans participated actively in Southern politics during Reconstruction — voting, holding office, and organizing communities — representing a dramatic, if temporary, transformation of Southern society.
  • Economically, the promise of land redistribution to freedpeople largely went unfulfilled. Many former slaves ended up in sharecropping arrangements that left them economically dependent on, and often in debt to, landowners.
  • White Southern resistance took organized form through groups like the Ku Klux Klan, which used violence and intimidation to undermine Black political participation and Republican state governments.

5.7 The End of Reconstruction

  • Northern commitment to Reconstruction waned over time due to war-weariness, economic concerns (the Panic of 1873), and a growing desire for sectional reconciliation.
  • The Compromise of 1877, which resolved the disputed 1876 presidential election, resulted in the withdrawal of federal troops from the South — effectively ending Reconstruction.
  • In the following decades, Southern states implemented "Jim Crow" laws and other measures — poll taxes, literacy tests, violence — that effectively disenfranchised Black voters and enforced racial segregation, despite the Reconstruction Amendments remaining technically in effect.
  • Reconstruction's legacy is often described as an "unfinished revolution" — it ended slavery and established a constitutional framework for racial equality, but failed to secure lasting protection of African Americans' civil and political rights.

5.8 Comparison: Reconstruction Plans

How lenient or strict should terms for the former Confederacy be? The competing plans reflect very different visions of what Reconstruction was meant to accomplish.

Lincoln's 10% Plan Johnson's Plan Congressional (Radical) Reconstruction
Readmission Terms 10% of voters swear loyalty oath Similar leniency; quick restoration of state governments Stricter — required ratification of 14th Amendment, new state constitutions
Protections for Freedpeople Minimal, not fully specified Minimal; allowed Southern "Black Codes" to persist Strongest — Freedmen's Bureau, Reconstruction Amendments, federal oversight
Treatment of Former Confederates Relatively lenient Lenient — many pardoned quickly Temporarily barred many from holding office

Exam Tip

The relatively lenient terms that ultimately prevailed after Congressional Reconstruction's retreat in the 1870s help explain why Reconstruction's gains proved so reversible — strong essays connect the terms of Reconstruction directly to the rise of Jim Crow that followed.

Key Vocabulary

Compromise of 1850
Legislative package admitting California as a free state and strengthening fugitive slave laws.
Kansas-Nebraska Act
1854 law allowing popular sovereignty over slavery, repealing the Missouri Compromise line.
Dred Scott v. Sandford
1857 Supreme Court ruling denying citizenship to African Americans and Congress's power to ban slavery in territories.
Emancipation Proclamation
1863 order declaring enslaved people in Confederate territory to be free.
Reconstruction Amendments
The 13th, 14th, and 15th Amendments, ending slavery and establishing citizenship and voting rights.
Freedmen's Bureau
Federal agency providing education and aid to formerly enslaved people after the Civil War.
Sharecropping
Labor system where farmers worked land for a share of the crop, often trapping them in debt.
Ku Klux Klan
White supremacist group that used violence to suppress Black political participation.
Compromise of 1877
Agreement that resolved the 1876 election and ended Reconstruction by withdrawing federal troops.
