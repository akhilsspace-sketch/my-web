Period 4 Notes

Expansion & Sectionalism

1800–1848 ~10% of exam 6 topics + comparison

Big Picture

Period 4 traces the young republic's growth from a small Atlantic nation into a continental power, driven by democratic expansion, economic transformation, and a wave of social reform. But economic growth followed two very different paths in the North and South — a divergence that, by 1848, had hardened into the question of whether slavery would expand into the West, a question the nation could no longer avoid.

4.1 The Jeffersonian Republic & the Louisiana Purchase

  • Jefferson's election in 1800 marked the first peaceful transfer of power between rival political parties in U.S. history — though once in office, Jefferson kept much of Hamilton's financial system intact.
  • The Louisiana Purchase (1803) — bought from France — roughly doubled the size of the United States. It raised a constitutional puzzle: Jefferson's own strict-constructionist principles seemed to conflict with the broad implied power needed to acquire new territory, but he proceeded anyway.
  • The Lewis and Clark Expedition (1804–06) mapped the new territory and established relationships — sometimes cooperative, sometimes not — with the Native nations already living there.
  • Jefferson's Embargo Act (1807), intended to keep the U.S. out of the Napoleonic Wars by halting trade with Europe, instead badly damaged the American economy and proved deeply unpopular — foreshadowing the tensions that led to the War of 1812.

4.2 The War of 1812 & the Era of Nationalism

  • Causes of the War of 1812: British impressment of American sailors, British interference with American trade during the Napoleonic Wars, and British support for Native American resistance on the frontier (notably Tecumseh's confederacy).
  • The war itself was largely inconclusive militarily — the Treaty of Ghent (1814) restored prewar boundaries — but its political effects were significant.
  • The war effectively destroyed the Federalist Party (which had opposed it) and ushered in the "Era of Good Feelings" — a period of one-party dominance and rising nationalism.
  • It also crushed organized Native American resistance in the Old Northwest and South, including the defeat of Tecumseh's coalition, opening more land to American settlement.

4.3 The Market Revolution & Economic Transformation

  • The Market Revolution — a transformation in transportation, communication, and production — tied the country together economically and shifted many Americans from subsistence farming toward commercial production.
  • Transportation improvements — canals (most famously the Erie Canal, 1825), turnpikes, and eventually railroads — dramatically cut the cost and time of moving goods across the country.
  • Henry Clay's American System — protective tariffs, a national bank, and federally funded internal improvements — aimed to bind the national economy together, though states'-rights advocates objected to federal funding of internal improvements.
  • A factory system began to emerge, especially in New England textile manufacturing (e.g., the Lowell mills, which employed young women) — an early step toward the industrialization that Period 6 will pick up at a much larger scale.

4.4 Westward Expansion & Manifest Destiny

  • Manifest Destiny — the belief that American expansion across the continent was inevitable and divinely sanctioned — became a powerful ideological justification for territorial growth.
  • The Indian Removal Act (1830) led to the forced relocation of Native nations from the Southeast to Indian Territory — most infamously the Trail of Tears, in which the Cherokee and other nations suffered massive loss of life during forced removal.
  • American settlers moved into Mexican-held Texas; Texas declared independence in 1836 and was annexed by the U.S. in 1845 — contributing to tensions with Mexico that would lead to the Mexican-American War (1846–48), at the very edge of this period.
  • Each new wave of territorial expansion reignited the question of whether slavery would be permitted in new territories — a tension that builds steadily across this period.

4.5 Jacksonian Democracy

  • Andrew Jackson's presidency (1829–1837) is associated with the expansion of political participation among white men, as many states eliminated property requirements for voting — dramatically widening the electorate.
  • Jackson's policies reflected a broader suspicion of concentrated economic power: his veto of the re-charter of the Second Bank of the United States (the "Bank War") reflected this populist stance, though critics argued it destabilized the economy.
  • The spoils system — rewarding political supporters with government jobs — became a hallmark of the new mass-party political system.
  • The Nullification Crisis (1832–33), in which South Carolina attempted to nullify a federal tariff, showed Jackson asserting strong federal authority even as he positioned himself as a champion of "the people" against entrenched elites.

4.6 Reform Movements & the Second Great Awakening

  • The Second Great Awakening (early 1800s) — another wave of religious revivalism — emphasized individual capacity for salvation and moral improvement, inspiring a broad range of social reform movements.
  • The temperance movement campaigned against alcohol consumption, which reformers saw as a root cause of poverty and family breakdown.
  • The abolitionist movement grew increasingly vocal — figures like William Lloyd Garrison (publisher of The Liberator) and Frederick Douglass argued for the immediate end of slavery, a more radical stance than earlier gradual-emancipation approaches.
  • The women's rights movement, which often grew out of women's participation in abolitionism, held the Seneca Falls Convention (1848), producing the Declaration of Sentiments and calling for expanded women's rights, including suffrage.
  • Utopian communities (e.g., Brook Farm, Oneida) experimented with alternative social and economic arrangements, reflecting the era's broader reform impulse.

4.7 Comparison: Northern & Southern Society by 1848

The regional differences from Period 2 didn't disappear — the Market Revolution widened them. By 1848, North and South had genuinely different economies, labor systems, and stakes in westward expansion.

North South
Economy Increasingly industrial and commercial; factory-based manufacturing Agricultural, centered on cash crops (especially cotton)
Labor System Free wage labor Enslaved labor central to the plantation economy
Population Trends Rapid urban growth, fueled partly by immigration Slower urbanization; population growth tied to plantation expansion
Stake in Western Expansion Wanted new territories open to free labor and settlement Wanted new territories open to slavery to maintain political balance

Exam Tip

Don't treat the North/South divide as something that suddenly appeared in the 1850s — it traces back to Period 2's regional patterns and was sharpened by the Market Revolution's uneven effects. By 1848, the core disagreement wasn't just about slavery's morality, but about whose economic and political system would define the nation's future in the West.
