Unit 6 Notes

Consequences of Industrialization

c. 1750–1900 CE ~12–15% of exam 7 topics + comparison

Big Picture

If Unit 5 explained why the Industrial Revolution began, Unit 6 covers everything it caused: new economic ideologies, reshaped societies, massive migration, and a fresh wave of imperialism as industrialized powers sought raw materials and markets across the globe.

6.1 Economic Developments & Innovations

  • By the late 1800s, a "Second Industrial Revolution" introduced new industries — steel (via the Bessemer process), chemicals, electricity, and oil-based products.
  • Railroads transformed transportation, opening interior regions to trade and rapid troop movement; steamships sped up ocean transport; the telegraph enabled near-instant long-distance communication for the first time in history.
  • New business structures emerged: corporations pooled massive amounts of investor capital with limited liability, while monopolies and trusts (e.g., Standard Oil) concentrated economic power in the hands of a few firms.
  • The global economy became more tightly integrated: raw materials flowed from colonized and peripheral regions to industrial "core" countries, while finished manufactured goods flowed back — a pattern that reinforced global economic inequality.

6.2 Reactions to Industrial Capitalism: Socialism

  • Industrialization created stark new class divisions: the bourgeoisie (factory- and capital-owning class) versus the proletariat (wage-earning industrial workers).
  • Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels, in The Communist Manifesto (1848), argued that capitalism was inherently exploitative and predicted that the proletariat would eventually rise up to create a classless, communist society.
  • Socialism — in various forms — advocated collective or state ownership of the means of production as an alternative to unregulated capitalism.
  • These ideas had limited immediate political impact in this period, but they would become enormously influential in the 20th century (the Russian Revolution, Unit 7/8).

6.3 Government Policy Responses

  • Governments responded to industrialization's social problems unevenly — ranging from continued laissez-faire policies to active reform.
  • Reform efforts included laws limiting child labor and regulating working hours, public health and sanitation improvements in rapidly growing cities, and gradual expansion of voting rights.
  • Labor unions formed so workers could negotiate collectively for better wages and conditions, sometimes through strikes.
  • Some governments adopted early social welfare programs — notably, Bismarck's Germany introduced state social insurance partly as a strategy to undercut the appeal of socialist movements among workers.

6.4 Society in the Industrial Age

  • Rapid urbanization — mass migration from rural areas to industrial cities — often produced severe overcrowding, poor sanitation, and disease in working-class neighborhoods.
  • A new middle class of managers, professionals, and merchants grew alongside the industrial working class, creating a more complex class structure than the old aristocracy-vs-peasantry model.
  • Women and children made up a significant share of factory labor, particularly in textile mills, typically working long hours for low wages.
  • Middle-class "separate spheres" ideology idealized men as breadwinners and women as homemakers — but this ideal often contrasted sharply with the reality of working-class women's continued participation in wage labor.

6.5 Global Migration

  • The 19th century saw massive global migration flows driven by industrialization and changing economic opportunity.
  • Tens of millions of Europeans emigrated to the Americas, escaping rural poverty and seeking industrial or agricultural work.
  • After the abolition of slavery in much of the world, indentured labor migration filled the resulting labor gap — workers from India and China traveled (often under exploitative contracts) to plantations in the Caribbean, Africa, and Southeast Asia.
  • These migration patterns permanently reshaped the demographics of destination regions and created lasting diaspora communities.

6.6 New Imperialism: Causes & Methods

  • "New Imperialism" (c. 1870–1914) describes a renewed wave of colonial expansion by European powers (and later the U.S. and Japan), focused especially on Africa and Asia.
  • Causes: the economic need for raw materials and new markets for industrial goods, nationalist competition among rival European powers, and ideological justifications such as Social Darwinism and the "civilizing mission."
  • Methods varied: direct rule (European officials administering colonies directly), indirect rule (governing through existing local elites under European oversight), and informal/economic imperialism (spheres of influence and unequal treaties without full colonization, as in China).
  • The "Scramble for Africa" saw European powers rapidly partition nearly the entire continent, formalized at the Berlin Conference (1884–85) — with no African representatives present.
  • Technology made this expansion possible: steamships and railroads for transport, the machine gun for military dominance, the telegraph for coordination, and quinine (a treatment for malaria) that allowed Europeans to survive longer in tropical regions.

6.7 Industrialization & Reform Outside the West

  • Facing the threat of Western domination, some non-Western states pursued their own state-directed modernization.
  • Japan's Meiji Restoration (from 1868) was the most successful example — rapid, centralized modernization of industry, the military (Western-style conscript army and navy), and education, while preserving the emperor as a unifying national symbol. This allowed Japan to avoid colonization entirely and become an imperial power itself.
  • The Ottoman Empire's Tanzimat reforms (mid-1800s) attempted legal, military, and administrative modernization to strengthen the empire against European pressure — with only partial success, as the empire continued losing territory.
  • Egypt under Muhammad Ali pursued industrialization and military modernization, but accumulated heavy debts to European creditors, which contributed to British occupation in 1882.

6.8 Comparison: Responses to Western Pressure

Why did Japan succeed at modernization where others struggled? This comparison is a favorite AP prompt.

State Reform Approach Outcome
Japan Meiji Restoration: centralized, state-led adoption of Western industry, military, and education while preserving the emperor as a unifying symbol Avoided colonization; became an imperial power itself
Ottoman Empire Tanzimat reforms: legal and military modernization Partial success — continued territorial losses ("the sick man of Europe")
Egypt Muhammad Ali's industrial & military modernization, financed by foreign debt Debt crisis led to British occupation (1882)
Qing China Self-Strengthening Movement: limited reforms amid internal resistance Continued foreign concessions and spheres of influence

Exam Tip

Japan's success is usually explained by strong central authority that could implement reform quickly and consistently. When comparing, focus on why reform succeeded or stalled — political centralization, degree of foreign debt/dependency, and whether reform had broad elite support are all strong analytical angles.

Key Vocabulary

Bourgeoisie
The capital- and factory-owning class under industrial capitalism, per Marxist theory.
Proletariat
The industrial wage-laboring class, per Marxist theory.
Laissez-Faire
Policy of minimal government intervention in the economy.
Labor Union
Worker organization that negotiates collectively for wages and working conditions.
Indentured Labor
Contracted labor migration, often exploitative, that replaced slave labor on plantations after abolition.
New Imperialism
The renewed wave of European (and later U.S./Japanese) colonial expansion from roughly 1870–1914.
Social Darwinism
Misapplication of evolutionary theory to justify imperial domination as "natural" hierarchy among peoples.
Berlin Conference
1884–85 meeting where European powers divided up African territory without African representation.
Meiji Restoration
Japan's program of rapid, state-led modernization beginning in 1868.
Sphere of Influence
A region where a foreign power has exclusive economic/trade privileges without formal colonial rule.
