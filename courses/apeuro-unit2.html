Unit 2 Notes

Age of Reformation

c. 1450–1648 ~8% of exam 4 topics + comparison

Big Picture

Building directly on Unit 1's Christian humanism, Unit 2 covers the Protestant Reformation — the permanent shattering of Western Christianity's religious unity — and the century of conflict that followed. The Catholic Church responded with its own reform movement, but compromise proved impossible, and religious differences fueled wars that devastated much of the continent until the Peace of Westphalia (1648) established a new principle for organizing European states: sovereignty.

2.1 Luther & the Protestant Reformation

  • Martin Luther's 1517 Ninety-Five Theses challenged Catholic practices like the sale of indulgences and core doctrines, proposing justification by faith alone and the authority of scripture (sola scriptura) over Church tradition.
  • The printing press (Unit 1) spread Luther's ideas rapidly across German-speaking lands; Lutheranism became established in much of northern Germany and Scandinavia.
  • The Peace of Augsburg (1555) allowed individual German princes to choose whether their territory would be Lutheran or Catholic — a temporary settlement that left underlying tensions unresolved.
  • Luther's break with Rome was as much about challenging papal and ecclesiastical authority as it was about specific doctrines — once that authority was questioned, other reformers quickly pushed in even more radical directions.

2.2 Calvin, Zwingli & the Spread of Protestantism

  • Huldrych Zwingli led an early reform movement in Zurich, but it was Calvinism (John Calvin, based in Geneva) — emphasizing predestination, the doctrine that God has already determined who is saved — that spread most widely, reaching Switzerland, the Netherlands, Scotland (as Presbyterianism), and parts of France (the Huguenots).
  • The English Reformation began with Henry VIII's break from Rome in the 1530s for largely political and dynastic reasons, creating the Church of England — its theology then swung between more Protestant (under Edward VI) and Catholic (under Mary I) before settling into a moderate Protestant "middle way" under Elizabeth I.
  • More radical reformers, the Anabaptists, pushed further still — advocating adult ("believer's") baptism and separation of church and state — and were persecuted by Catholics and mainstream Protestants alike.
  • By the mid-1500s, Western Christianity had splintered into multiple competing churches, each claiming to represent true Christianity — setting the stage for the religious wars in Topic 2.4.

2.3 The Catholic (Counter) Reformation

  • The Council of Trent (1545–1563) reaffirmed core Catholic doctrines while reforming clergy training and addressing abuses — the heart of the Catholic Reformation's response to Protestant criticism.
  • The Jesuits (Society of Jesus, founded by Ignatius of Loyola) became key agents of Catholic renewal through education and missionary work in Europe and abroad.
  • Baroque art and architecture (Bernini, Caravaggio) — an emotional, dramatic style — was deployed by the reinvigorated Catholic Church to inspire devotion and visually reassert its authority in the face of Protestant challenges.
  • Rather than seeking compromise, the Catholic Reformation drew clearer doctrinal lines than before — making the religious divisions of the period more rigid, not less, and setting up the armed conflicts of Topic 2.4.

2.4 Religious Wars & the Peace of Westphalia

  • Religious conflict wracked Europe for over a century: the French Wars of Religion pitted Catholics against Huguenots until Henry IV's Edict of Nantes (1598) granted the Huguenots limited toleration, while Spain fought for decades to suppress Protestant rebellion in the Netherlands.
  • The Thirty Years' War (1618–1648) began as a religious conflict within the Holy Roman Empire but grew into a broader political struggle involving most major European powers, devastating much of Germany.
  • The decades of religious war also coincided with widespread social anxiety — population pressure and price inflation (partly from American silver, Unit 1) contributed to waves of witch-hunts and intensified debates about women's proper role in society (the "querelle des femmes").
  • The Peace of Westphalia (1648) ended the Thirty Years' War, recognized Dutch and Swiss independence, and established the principle of state sovereignty — each ruler's right to govern their territory without outside interference — often seen as the foundation of the modern European state system and the starting point for Unit 3.

2.5 Comparison: The Major Reformation Movements

By 1648, Western Christianity had splintered into several major branches. The AP exam frequently asks students to compare their theology, organization, and geographic spread.

Lutheranism Calvinism Catholicism (post-Trent) Anglicanism
View of Salvation Justification by faith alone Predestination Faith plus good works/sacraments Faith, with retained sacraments
Church Authority Scripture over tradition Scripture; elected church elders Pope and Church tradition Monarch as head of church
Where It Took Hold Northern Germany, Scandinavia Switzerland, Netherlands, Scotland Spain, Italy, southern Germany, Poland England

Exam Tip

When explaining why the Reformation spread or why specific rulers adopted a faith, remember that political motives (control over Church property, independence from Rome or the Holy Roman Emperor) were often as important as genuinely religious conviction — rarely is this a single-cause story.

Key Vocabulary

Protestant Reformation
16th-century religious movement that split Western Christianity into competing churches.
Justification by Faith
Luther's doctrine that salvation comes through faith alone, not works or sacraments.
Predestination
Calvinist doctrine that God has already determined who is saved.
Anabaptists
Radical reformers who advocated adult baptism and separation of church and state.
Counter-Reformation
Catholic Church's response to Protestantism, reaffirming doctrine and reforming abuses.
Jesuits
Catholic religious order founded by Ignatius of Loyola, active in education and missions.
Edict of Nantes
1598 decree granting limited religious toleration to French Protestants.
Thirty Years' War
Devastating 1618–1648 conflict that began over religion and reshaped Central Europe.
Peace of Westphalia
1648 treaty establishing state sovereignty as the basis of international relations.
