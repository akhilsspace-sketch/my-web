Unit 6 Notes

Industrialization and Its Effects

c. 1815–1914 ~14% of exam 4 topics + comparison

Big Picture

Unit 6 covers the Industrial Revolution — the transformation of production from agrarian and artisanal methods to mechanized factories, beginning in Britain and gradually spreading across the continent. This economic transformation reshaped society, creating new social classes whose conflicts gave rise to the competing ideological "responses" — liberalism and socialism — that Unit 7 will trace through the political upheavals of the 19th century.

6.1 The Industrial Revolution: Origins in Britain

  • Industrialization began in Britain (textiles, steam power, coal and iron) in the late 1700s–early 1800s, driven by available capital, abundant natural resources, an agricultural surplus that freed up labor (building on the agricultural improvements discussed in Unit 4), and a growing population to work in new industries.
  • The factory system concentrated production and workers in urban settings, fundamentally restructuring work from agrarian and artisanal patterns toward wage labor with fixed hours and centralized supervision.
  • Steam power — applied first to textile production and then to transportation — freed industry from dependence on water power and allowed factories to locate near labor and markets rather than rivers.
  • Britain's early lead gave it an enormous economic advantage that would shape its role in the New Imperialism covered in Unit 7.

6.2 Industrialization Spreads to the Continent

  • Industrialization spread unevenly to the continent — Belgium and France industrialized relatively early, while Germany industrialized rapidly in the second half of the century, especially after unification (Unit 7) provided a unified market and infrastructure.
  • Eastern and Southern Europe lagged considerably behind Western Europe, a economic gap that persisted into the 20th century and shaped patterns of migration and political instability.
  • The "Second Industrial Revolution" (later 1800s) introduced steel, chemicals, electricity, and railroads — a new wave of technologies that accelerated economic transformation and tied European economies together (and to the wider world) more tightly than ever.
  • Railroads in particular transformed both economies — by cheaply moving goods and people — and the military capabilities of states, which could now mobilize troops on a scale and speed unimaginable in the Napoleonic era (Unit 5).

6.3 Social Effects: Urbanization & New Class Structure

  • Industrialization created a new social landscape: a wealthy industrial bourgeoisie who owned factories and capital, and a growing urban proletariat (working class) who faced harsh factory conditions, low wages, and crowded, unsanitary housing.
  • Rapid urbanization concentrated these problems — overcrowding, disease, and pollution — in industrial cities, eventually prompting public health reforms (sanitation systems, building codes) by mid-century.
  • Factory acts gradually regulated child labor and working hours, reflecting growing recognition that unregulated industrial capitalism produced serious social costs.
  • Political power slowly followed economic change: Britain's Reform Acts of 1832, 1867, and 1884 progressively extended the vote first to the middle class and then to working-class men — a gradual process that liberal reformers in Topic 6.4 saw as proof their approach could work.

6.4 Responses: Liberalism, Socialism & Marxism

  • Liberalism — building on the laissez-faire economics of Unit 4 — generally viewed industrial capitalism as a source of progress and wealth, favoring gradual reform (factory acts, expanded suffrage) over fundamental change to the economic system.
  • Early "Utopian Socialists" (Saint-Simon, Fourier, Owen) responded to industrialization's social costs by proposing cooperative or planned communities as alternatives to competitive capitalism — predating, and influencing, more systematic socialist theory.
  • Marxism (Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels's Communist Manifesto, 1848) argued that history was driven by class conflict between the bourgeoisie and proletariat, and predicted that capitalism would inevitably be overthrown by revolution — becoming the foundation for later socialist and communist movements.
  • By the late 1800s, labor unions and socialist political parties (such as the German Social Democratic Party) emerged as organized vehicles for working-class power, generally working within — rather than violently against — parliamentary systems, narrowing the practical gap between reform-minded liberals and reform-minded socialists even as their theories remained opposed.

6.5 Comparison: Liberal vs. Socialist Responses to Industrialization

Liberalism and socialism agreed that industrialization had transformed society — but disagreed sharply about whether the system itself needed fixing or replacing.

Liberalism Socialism / Marxism
View of Industrial Capitalism A source of progress and wealth, despite some social costs A system built on the exploitation of the working class
Role of Government Protect property and contracts; minimal economic interference Either overthrow (Marxism) or actively plan/redistribute the economy
Path to Change Gradual legislative reform (factory acts, expanded suffrage) Revolution (early Marxism) or organized labor and parliamentary parties (by century's end)
Example Movements British Liberal Party, free-trade economists German Social Democratic Party, trade unions

Exam Tip

Be ready to explain how, by 1900, the practical gap between reform-minded liberals and reform-minded socialists had narrowed even though their underlying theories remained opposed — both increasingly worked through elections and legislation rather than revolution.

Key Vocabulary

Industrial Revolution
Transformation of production from agrarian/artisanal methods to mechanized factory systems.
Factory System
Concentration of production and workers under one roof with centralized supervision.
Second Industrial Revolution
Late 19th-century wave of innovation in steel, chemicals, electricity, and railroads.
Bourgeoisie
The wealthy, property-owning middle and industrial-owning class.
Proletariat
Marxist term for the urban industrial working class.
Utopian Socialism
Early socialist thought proposing cooperative communities as alternatives to capitalism.
Marxism
Ideology holding that history is driven by class conflict and capitalism will be overthrown by revolution.
Reform Act
British legislation (1832, 1867, 1884) that progressively expanded voting rights.
Labor Union
Organization of workers formed to negotiate wages and conditions with employers.
