Unit 4 Notes

Scientific, Philosophical & Political Developments

c. 1648–1815 ~13% of exam 4 topics + comparison

Big Picture

Unit 4 traces how a new emphasis on reason reshaped European thought — first about the natural world (the Scientific Revolution), then about society, government, and the economy (the Enlightenment). These ideas could cut in different directions: some monarchs absorbed them into "enlightened absolutism" (reinforcing the systems from Unit 3), while others — as Unit 5 will show — saw them used to justify revolution against existing rulers altogether. Meanwhile, the Atlantic economy and new economic theories were transforming how Europeans thought about wealth itself.

4.1 The Scientific Revolution

  • The Scientific Revolution (Copernicus, Galileo, Newton) replaced an Aristotelian, Church-sanctioned cosmology with a mechanistic universe governed by natural, mathematically describable laws.
  • Copernicus's heliocentric model and Galileo's observations challenged the Church-endorsed geocentric view, while Newton's Principia Mathematica (1687) unified the new physics into a single coherent system of universal laws.
  • Beyond its specific discoveries, the Scientific Revolution established a new method — observation, experimentation, and mathematical reasoning — as a source of authority that could rival, and sometimes openly contradict, tradition and religious doctrine.
  • This was the crucial precondition for the Enlightenment: if reason and observation could uncover the laws governing nature, thinkers asked, why not apply the same approach to society, government, and the economy?

4.2 The Enlightenment: Reason, Natural Rights & Social Contract

  • The Enlightenment applied "reason" to society and government: John Locke argued for natural rights (life, liberty, property) and government by consent; Montesquieu proposed a separation of powers among branches of government to prevent tyranny; Rousseau described the social contract and the "general will" as the basis of legitimate authority; and Voltaire championed religious toleration and criticized both absolutism and the Catholic Church.
  • These thinkers drew directly on the contrasting political models from Unit 3 — Locke's ideas grew out of England's Glorious Revolution, while Voltaire pointed admiringly to Dutch religious toleration as proof that government without absolutism was possible.
  • Salons (often hosted by women), academies, and the printing press spread Enlightenment ideas among educated elites across national borders, creating a transnational "Republic of Letters" — most famously embodied in Diderot's Encyclopédie, which compiled Enlightenment knowledge for a wide readership.
  • Taken to their logical conclusion, these ideas — particularly that government derives its legitimacy from the consent of the governed — posed a direct challenge to absolutism's claim of divine right (Unit 3), a tension Unit 5's French Revolution would bring to a head.

4.3 Enlightened Absolutism

  • Enlightened absolutism describes rulers — Frederick the Great of Prussia, Catherine the Great of Russia, and Joseph II of Austria among them — who adopted some Enlightenment-inspired reforms while retaining, or even strengthening, autocratic power.
  • Typical reforms included expanding religious toleration, codifying and rationalizing laws, promoting education, and patronizing the arts and sciences — measures that made government more efficient without sharing power.
  • These rulers corresponded with and patronized Enlightenment philosophers (Frederick the Great hosted Voltaire, Catherine the Great corresponded with Diderot), lending their courts an air of intellectual prestige.
  • Enlightened absolutism reveals a central irony of the period: the same ideas that could justify limiting a monarch's power (Topic 4.2) could also be selectively used to make absolutist rule (Unit 3) more effective and more legitimate-seeming — a contradiction that would not hold indefinitely.

4.4 Economic Thought & the Atlantic Economy

  • Agricultural improvements — new crops and techniques, and the enclosure of common land in England — increased food production, freed up labor for other pursuits, and contributed to sustained population growth.
  • The Atlantic economy — the transatlantic slave trade and plantation agriculture (sugar, tobacco) — generated enormous wealth for Western European powers and was deeply tied to mercantilist competition, especially between Britain and France.
  • Proto-industrialization (the rural "putting-out" system) expanded textile production outside traditional guild structures, laying groundwork for the Industrial Revolution covered in Unit 6.
  • Economic thought began shifting away from mercantilism's focus on accumulating bullion (Unit 1): Adam Smith's Wealth of Nations (1776) argued for laissez-faire — free markets, competition, and the division of labor — as the true source of national wealth.

4.5 Comparison: Hobbes vs. Locke

Writing decades apart but both shaped by England's 17th-century turmoil (Unit 3), Thomas Hobbes and John Locke reached very different conclusions about human nature and the purpose of government — conclusions that fed directly into the social-contract thinking of Topic 4.2.

Thomas Hobbes John Locke
View of Human Nature Selfish and competitive Rational and capable of cooperation
State of Nature A "war of all against all" — life is dangerous and chaotic Relatively peaceful, but natural rights lack enforcement
Purpose of Government Impose order by holding a monopoly on power Protect natural rights (life, liberty, property)
When Is Rebellion Justified? Never — any government is better than chaos When government fails to protect natural rights

Exam Tip

Hobbes's Leviathan (1651) is often used to justify absolutism (Unit 3), while Locke's Two Treatises (1689) is often used to justify constitutionalism and, later, revolution. When an exam question asks you to explain the "intellectual justification" for a political system, matching the right philosopher to the right system is half the battle.

Key Vocabulary

Scientific Revolution
Shift to a mechanistic view of nature governed by mathematical laws, based on observation and experiment.
Enlightenment
18th-century intellectual movement applying reason to society, government, and religion.
Natural Rights
Rights (life, liberty, property) that Locke argued belong to individuals regardless of government.
Social Contract
Idea that government's legitimacy derives from an agreement among the governed.
Separation of Powers
Montesquieu's proposal to divide government into branches to prevent tyranny.
General Will
Rousseau's concept of the collective interest that legitimate government must serve.
Enlightened Absolutism
Rule by monarchs who adopted Enlightenment reforms while retaining autocratic power.
Proto-Industrialization
Rural "putting-out" system of textile production that preceded factory industrialization.
Laissez-Faire
Adam Smith's principle of minimal government interference in free markets.
