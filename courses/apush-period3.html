Period 3 Notes

Revolution & the New Nation

1754–1800 ~12% of exam 7 topics + comparison

Big Picture

In just 46 years, Britain's American colonies went from loyal subjects helping fight Britain's wars to an independent republic that had already experimented with — and replaced — its first national government. The throughline is a recurring debate over the proper balance between liberty and authority: first colonists vs. Britain, then states vs. a national government, and finally competing visions for how powerful that national government should be.

3.1 The French and Indian War & Its Aftermath

  • The French and Indian War (1754–1763), the North American theater of the global Seven Years' War, pitted Britain and its colonial militias against France and its Native allies for control of the Ohio Valley and beyond.
  • British victory removed France as a major colonial power in North America (Treaty of Paris, 1763) — but left Britain with enormous war debt.
  • To stabilize the frontier and reduce conflict with Native nations, Britain issued the Proclamation of 1763, restricting colonial settlement west of the Appalachian Mountains — a deeply unpopular move among land-hungry colonists.
  • Pontiac's Rebellion (1763), Native American resistance to British expansion in the Ohio Valley, further shaped British frontier policy.
  • Britain's search for new revenue to pay off war debt set the stage for the taxation conflicts of the next decade.

3.2 Taxation Without Representation: Colonial Resistance

  • Seeking revenue, Parliament passed a series of new taxes: the Sugar Act (1764), the Stamp Act (1765, a direct tax on printed materials), and the Townshend Acts (1767, taxes on imported goods).
  • Colonists protested under the principle "no taxation without representation" — arguing that only their own elected assemblies, not a distant Parliament, had the authority to tax them.
  • Resistance took many forms: boycotts of British goods, groups like the Sons and Daughters of Liberty, and the Committees of Correspondence that coordinated resistance across colonies.
  • Key flashpoints — the Boston Massacre (1770) and the Boston Tea Party (1773, protesting the Tea Act) — escalated tensions and prompted harsher British responses, including the "Intolerable Acts" of 1774.

3.3 The Road to Revolution & Declaration of Independence

  • In response to the Intolerable Acts, colonial representatives convened the First Continental Congress (1774) to coordinate a unified response.
  • Fighting broke out at Lexington and Concord (April 1775) — the "shot heard round the world."
  • The Second Continental Congress assumed governing functions, created the Continental Army under George Washington, and gradually moved toward independence.
  • Thomas Paine's Common Sense (1776) — a widely-read pamphlet arguing forcefully for independence and against monarchy — helped shift public opinion toward separation.
  • The Declaration of Independence (July 4, 1776), drafted primarily by Thomas Jefferson, drew on Enlightenment ideas — natural rights and government by consent — to justify the break from Britain.

3.4 The American Revolution: Conduct & Course of the War

  • Early fighting (1775–77) was concentrated in the North. Despite British advantages in resources and training, simply keeping the Continental Army intact counted as a strategic success.
  • The Battle of Saratoga (1777) was a turning point — it convinced France to formally ally with the United States, providing crucial military and financial support and turning the war into a wider international conflict (Spain and the Netherlands later joined against Britain).
  • The war effectively ended with the American/French victory at Yorktown (1781), and the Treaty of Paris (1783) formally recognized American independence and extended U.S. territory to the Mississippi River.
  • The Revolution also reshaped American society: it sparked early debates over slavery (some Northern states began gradual abolition), expanded — though did not fully realize — ideas about political participation, and prompted figures like Abigail Adams to raise questions about women's roles ("Remember the Ladies").

3.5 The Articles of Confederation

  • The nation's first government, the Articles of Confederation (ratified 1781), created a deliberately weak central government that prioritized state sovereignty — a direct reaction to fears of centralized power after experience with Britain.
  • A genuine achievement: the Northwest Ordinance (1787) established an orderly process for admitting new states from western territory and banned slavery in the Northwest Territory.
  • Major weaknesses: Congress could not levy taxes, regulate interstate commerce, or enforce its own laws — it depended entirely on voluntary state cooperation.
  • Shays' Rebellion (1786–87) — an uprising of indebted Massachusetts farmers protesting debt collection and property seizures — exposed the national government's inability to respond effectively, galvanizing support for a stronger central government.

3.6 The Constitutional Convention & Ratification Debate

  • Delegates met in Philadelphia in 1787, ostensibly to revise the Articles of Confederation — but ultimately drafted an entirely new Constitution.
  • Key compromises: the Great Compromise created a bicameral legislature balancing the interests of large and small states, while the Three-Fifths Compromise counted enslaved people as a fraction of a state's population for representation and taxation purposes.
  • The new Constitution created a stronger federal government built on separation of powers (legislative, executive, judicial) and checks and balances between them.
  • Ratification sparked fierce debate between Federalists (who supported the Constitution, defending it in the Federalist Papers by Hamilton, Madison, and Jay) and Anti-Federalists (who feared excessive central power and the lack of protections for individual rights).
  • The promise of a Bill of Rights — ratified in 1791 — helped secure ratification by directly addressing Anti-Federalist concerns.

3.7 The New Republic: Washington's Administration

  • President Washington's administration established lasting precedents, including the cabinet system and the tradition of a two-term presidency.
  • Alexander Hamilton's financial plan — federal assumption of state debts, a national bank, and tariffs to encourage domestic manufacturing — sparked early disagreements over the proper scope of federal power.
  • These disagreements crystallized into the nation's first political parties: Federalists (Hamilton — broad federal power, a national bank, pro-commercial interests) and Democratic-Republicans (Jefferson — strict construction of the Constitution, agrarian interests, sympathetic to revolutionary France).
  • The Whiskey Rebellion (1794) — a protest against a federal excise tax, suppressed by federal troops under Washington — demonstrated the new government's willingness and ability to enforce federal law, a sharp contrast to the Articles era.
  • Foreign policy proved divisive: Washington's Proclamation of Neutrality (1793) sought to keep the U.S. out of the wars of the French Revolution, while the Alien and Sedition Acts (1798, under John Adams) restricted immigrants' rights and criticism of the government — provoking strong Democratic-Republican opposition.

3.8 Comparison: Articles of Confederation vs. Constitution

The Constitution was a direct response to the Articles' failures — pairing each weakness with a specific fix is a reliable way to organize an essay on this topic.

Feature Articles of Confederation Constitution
Federal Power Very weak; states retained most power Stronger national government with enumerated powers
Legislature Unicameral, one vote per state Bicameral — House (population-based) and Senate (equal per state)
Power to Tax None — could only request funds from states Congress can levy taxes directly
Executive & Judiciary None — no president or national courts Separate executive (president) and federal judiciary
Amendment Process Required unanimous consent of all states Requires 3/4 of states — still difficult, but achievable

Exam Tip

Shays' Rebellion is the single best piece of evidence for "why the Constitution replaced the Articles" — it directly demonstrates the consequences of a government with no power to tax, raise an army, or enforce law. Strong essays connect specific Articles-era failures to specific constitutional solutions rather than discussing the two documents in isolation.
