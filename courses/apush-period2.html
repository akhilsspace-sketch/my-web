Period 2 Notes

Colonial Society

1607–1754 ~10% of exam 6 topics + comparison

Big Picture

Over nearly 150 years, England's American colonies grew from struggling outposts into well-established — and increasingly distinct — regional societies. New England's tight-knit Puritan towns looked nothing like the Chesapeake's plantation society or the Middle Colonies' diversity. Yet all of them were bound together by Britain's mercantilist trade system and by a growing dependence on enslaved African labor. The regional differences that take shape here will keep reappearing for the rest of the course.

2.1 Continued Colonial Competition: Spain, France & the Dutch

  • While England became the dominant colonial power in what is now the U.S. during this period, Spain, France, and the Netherlands all maintained competing colonial presences.
  • Spain expanded its mission system in Florida and the Southwest — but the Pueblo Revolt (1680) saw Pueblo peoples temporarily drive Spanish colonizers out of New Mexico, reflecting ongoing resistance to forced labor and forced religious conversion.
  • France established New France, centered on the fur trade in the Great Lakes and St. Lawrence regions — this trade depended on maintaining alliances with Native nations, since France relied on Indigenous hunters and trade networks.
  • The Dutch established New Netherland, a commercially oriented colony known for ethnic and religious diversity — England seized it in 1664 and renamed it New York.
  • These overlapping claims created ongoing Anglo-French and Anglo-Spanish rivalries that would erupt into larger armed conflicts in Period 3.

2.2 English Colonization: Founding the Colonies

  • Jamestown (1607, Virginia) was founded by the Virginia Company — a joint-stock company — primarily for profit. It nearly failed due to disease and famine until tobacco cultivation gave it economic viability.
  • The New England colonies (Plymouth, 1620; Massachusetts Bay, 1630) were founded largely by Puritan and Separatist religious dissenters seeking to practice their faith, building tightly-knit communities centered on the church and the town meeting.
  • The Middle Colonies (New York, Pennsylvania, New Jersey, Delaware) developed notable ethnic, religious, and economic diversity. Pennsylvania, founded by William Penn on Quaker principles, became especially known for religious tolerance.
  • The Southern Colonies (Maryland, the Carolinas, Georgia) built economies around cash crops — tobacco, rice, and indigo — grown on plantations using large-scale enslaved labor.
  • Colonial charters varied in type — joint-stock, proprietary, and royal — which determined different levels of self-governance versus direct crown oversight.

2.3 Transatlantic Trade & Mercantilism

  • England, like other European powers, operated under mercantilism — the idea that colonies existed to enrich the mother country by supplying raw materials and serving as a market for finished goods.
  • The Navigation Acts (beginning 1651) required that colonial trade use English ships and that certain goods be routed through England before reaching other markets.
  • Triangular trade routes connected the colonies, Europe, Africa, and the Caribbean, exchanging manufactured goods, enslaved people, and raw materials such as sugar and tobacco.
  • In practice, enforcement of these trade restrictions was loose for much of this period — a policy historians call salutary neglect — which allowed colonies significant economic autonomy, including widespread smuggling.

2.4 Labor Systems: From Indentured Servitude to Slavery

  • Early Chesapeake labor relied heavily on indentured servants — mostly poor English men who worked for a fixed term (often 4–7 years) in exchange for passage to the colonies and the eventual promise of land.
  • Bacon's Rebellion (1676) — a revolt by poor Virginia colonists, including former indentured servants, against the colonial government over land access and Native policy — is often cited as accelerating the shift toward African slavery, as wealthy planters sought a labor force less likely to unite with poor whites against them.
  • By the late 17th and early 18th centuries, racialized chattel slavery became entrenched, especially in the South, codified through "slave codes" that defined enslaved people as property and made enslaved status hereditary.
  • The enslaved population grew rapidly through the transatlantic slave trade, providing the labor base for tobacco, rice, and indigo plantations.

2.5 Colonial Society, Religion & the Great Awakening

  • Religious diversity grew unevenly across the colonies — New England remained largely Puritan/Congregationalist, the Middle Colonies became home to Quakers, Lutherans, and many other groups, while the South had fewer strong religious institutions outside the Anglican establishment.
  • The Great Awakening (1730s–40s) was a wave of religious revivalism led by preachers such as George Whitefield and Jonathan Edwards, emphasizing personal religious experience over established church hierarchy.
  • Its effects extended beyond religion: it contributed to religious pluralism, challenged established religious authority, and — some historians argue — fostered a spirit of individualism that fed into later revolutionary ideas.
  • Enlightenment ideas emphasizing reason, natural rights, and scientific inquiry also began circulating among colonial elites during this period, shaping figures like Benjamin Franklin.

2.6 Colonial Relations with Native Americans

  • As English settlements expanded, conflicts with Native nations intensified over land and resources.
  • King Philip's War (1675–76) in New England was one of the deadliest conflicts (proportionally) in American history, devastating many Native communities in the region.
  • The Covenant Chain — a series of alliances between the Iroquois Confederacy and English colonies, especially New York — shows how diplomacy and trade, not just conflict, shaped colonial–Native relations.
  • The overall pattern across this period: an expanding colonial population and growing land demands repeatedly displaced Native nations, even where initial relationships had been cooperative.

2.7 Comparison: Regional Colonial Societies

By 1754, the British colonies had developed three broad regional patterns — differences in economy, society, and labor that will continue to shape American history for the next century.

Region Economy Society & Religion Labor System
New England Small farms, fishing, shipbuilding, trade Puritan/Congregationalist; tight-knit towns centered on church and town meeting Family labor; little reliance on slavery
Middle Colonies Diverse farming (grain), trade, growing port cities Ethnically and religiously diverse (Quakers, Lutherans, others) Mix of family labor, indentured servants, and some slavery
Southern Colonies Cash crop plantations (tobacco, rice, indigo) Anglican gentry-dominated; fewer towns, more dispersed settlement Heavy reliance on enslaved African labor

Exam Tip

Regional comparison is one of the most-tested themes in APUSH. These differences in economy, religion, and especially labor systems are the seeds of the sectional divides that will dominate Periods 4 and 5 — keep this table in mind as a baseline to compare against later developments.

Key Vocabulary

Mercantilism
Economic theory that colonies exist to benefit the mother country through trade.
Navigation Acts
Laws requiring colonial trade to pass through England on English ships.
Triangular Trade
Trade network connecting the colonies, Europe, Africa, and the Caribbean.
Indentured Servant
A laborer bound to work for a fixed term in exchange for passage to the colonies.
Bacon's Rebellion
1676 Virginia uprising that accelerated the shift toward African slavery.
Salutary Neglect
Britain's loose enforcement of trade laws, giving colonies practical autonomy.
Great Awakening
1730s–40s religious revival movement emphasizing personal faith over church hierarchy.
Covenant Chain
Alliance system linking the Iroquois Confederacy with English colonies.
Pueblo Revolt
1680 uprising that drove Spanish colonizers out of New Mexico for over a decade.
