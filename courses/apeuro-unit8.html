Unit 8 Notes

20th-Century Global Conflicts

c. 1914–Present ~9% of exam 5 topics + comparison

Big Picture

Unit 8 covers the most violent three decades in European history — two world wars bookending an unstable interwar period in which fragile new democracies gave way to totalitarian regimes promising order and national renewal at horrific human cost. The throughline: the tensions of Unit 7 (nationalism, imperial rivalry, militarism) detonate, and the political extremes they produce — fascism and communism — offer radically different but equally total answers to the crises of war and depression.

8.1 World War I: Causes, Conduct & Consequences

  • WWI's causes built directly on Unit 7's tensions — the alliance system, nationalism, militarism, and imperial rivalry — triggered by the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand (June 1914) and the resulting chain of ultimatums and mobilizations.
  • The war became a "total war" — mobilizing entire economies and societies, not just militaries — and introduced industrialized weapons (machine guns, poison gas, tanks, airplanes) that produced unprecedented casualties, especially in trench warfare on the Western Front.
  • The Eastern Front was more fluid but equally costly, and the strain of war contributed directly to the collapse of the Russian Empire, opening the door to the revolution covered in Topic 8.2.
  • U.S. entry (1917) and the collapse of Germany's last offensives led to the Armistice (November 1918), ending the fighting but leaving deep scars and unresolved tensions that would shape the interwar crisis of Topic 8.3.

8.2 The Russian Revolution & Rise of the USSR

  • The strains of WWI — military defeats, food shortages, autocratic mismanagement — led to the February 1917 Revolution, which overthrew Tsar Nicholas II and established a Provisional Government.
  • The Bolsheviks under Lenin seized power in the October Revolution (1917), promising "Peace, Land, and Bread," and withdrew Russia from WWI via the Treaty of Brest-Litovsk.
  • A bloody civil war (1918–1921) between the Bolshevik "Reds" and various anti-Bolshevik "Whites" ended in Bolshevik victory, establishing the Soviet Union.
  • Under Stalin (from the late 1920s), the USSR pursued forced rapid industrialization through Five-Year Plans and collectivization of agriculture — achieved at enormous human cost, including famine (especially in Ukraine) and political purges, establishing the totalitarian model compared in Topic 8.6.

8.3 Interwar Crisis: Versailles, Depression & the Rise of Fascism

  • The Treaty of Versailles (1919) imposed harsh terms on Germany — territorial losses, military restrictions, reparations, and a "war guilt" clause — which many Germans viewed as a humiliating "Diktat."
  • New democratic governments, such as Germany's Weimar Republic and the successor states of Austria-Hungary, faced severe challenges: economic instability (including Germany's catastrophic 1923 hyperinflation), political polarization, and weak democratic traditions, while the League of Nations lacked the enforcement power to maintain collective security.
  • The Great Depression (beginning 1929) devastated European economies already weakened by war debts and reparations, and discredited liberal democratic governments in the eyes of many citizens.
  • Fascism — first established by Mussolini in Italy (1922) — rejected liberal democracy, communism, and individualism in favor of extreme nationalism, a powerful state, and a charismatic leader cult; Hitler's National Socialism combined these fascist elements with virulent antisemitism, exploiting the Depression's desperation and Weimar's paralysis to gain power legally in 1933 and then dismantle democratic institutions entirely.

8.4 The Path to World War II & War in Europe

  • Hitler's foreign policy aggressively pursued treaty revision and territorial expansion — remilitarization of the Rhineland, the Anschluss with Austria (1938), and the Munich Agreement (1938), in which Britain and France pursued "appeasement" by allowing Germany to annex the Sudetenland in exchange for promises of peace.
  • Appeasement failed to satisfy Hitler's ambitions — Germany occupied the rest of Czechoslovakia (1939) and then invaded Poland (September 1939), prompting Britain and France to declare war.
  • The Nazi-Soviet Pact (1939) temporarily aligned Hitler and Stalin, enabling the joint invasion and partition of Poland, until Germany's surprise invasion of the USSR in 1941 (Operation Barbarossa) opened the war's most brutal front.
  • The war became global with Japan's entry and the U.S.'s entry following Pearl Harbor (1941), linking the European and Pacific theaters.

8.5 The Holocaust & the War's Aftermath

  • The Holocaust — the systematic Nazi genocide of six million European Jews, along with Roma, disabled people, and other targeted groups — represented the culmination of Nazi racial ideology, carried out through ghettos, mass shootings, and extermination camps.
  • The tide of the war turned with Soviet victories on the Eastern Front (Stalingrad, 1942–43) and the Western Allies' D-Day invasion (1944), leading to Germany's defeat in May 1945.
  • The war devastated Europe — tens of millions dead, cities destroyed, populations displaced — and left the continent's eastern half under Soviet military occupation, setting up the division explored in Unit 9.
  • War crimes trials at Nuremberg established precedents for international accountability, while the war's end also marked the definitive eclipse of Western Europe's global dominance by the United States and the USSR.

8.6 Comparison: Fascism vs. Communism

Both fascism and Soviet communism were "totalitarian" in their methods of control, but they rested on very different ideological foundations.

Fascism (Italy/Germany) Communism (USSR)
Core Ideology Extreme nationalism; race and nation above the individual Internationalist class struggle; abolition of class distinctions
Central Category Race / nation Economic class
Economic System State-directed capitalism (private property retained, tightly controlled) State ownership of the means of production (collectivization, planning)
Methods of Control Propaganda, secret police, single-party rule, leader cult Propaganda, secret police, single-party rule, leader cult

Exam Tip

Graders reward recognizing both the real similarities (single-party rule, propaganda, terror, leader cults) and the key ideological difference — race versus class as the central organizing category. Don't simply say "they were both bad" or "they were basically the same."

Key Vocabulary

Total War
Warfare that mobilizes a nation's entire economy and population, not just its military.
Bolsheviks
Radical Marxist faction led by Lenin that seized power in Russia in 1917.
Treaty of Versailles
1919 treaty imposing harsh terms on Germany after World War I.
Weimar Republic
Germany's fragile democratic government between 1919 and 1933.
Fascism
Ultranationalist, anti-democratic ideology emphasizing a powerful state and leader cult.
Totalitarianism
A regime seeking total control over political, economic, and social life.
Appeasement
Policy of conceding to an aggressor's demands to avoid conflict, notably toward Hitler.
Holocaust
Nazi Germany's systematic genocide of six million European Jews and other groups.
Operation Barbarossa
Germany's 1941 invasion of the Soviet Union, opening WWII's bloodiest front.
