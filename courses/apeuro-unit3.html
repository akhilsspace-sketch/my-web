Unit 3 Notes

Absolutism and Constitutionalism

c. 1648–1815 ~14% of exam 4 topics + comparison

Big Picture

With the Peace of Westphalia (Unit 2) establishing state sovereignty as the organizing principle of European politics, states pursued that sovereignty along two very different paths. Absolutism — epitomized by Louis XIV's France and adapted across Central and Eastern Europe — concentrated power in the monarch. Constitutionalism — developed in England and the Dutch Republic — limited royal power through representative institutions and law. Both models would shape how Enlightenment thinkers in Unit 4 imagined the ideal relationship between rulers and the ruled.

3.1 Absolutism in France: Louis XIV

  • Louis XIV ("the Sun King") epitomized absolutism — centralizing authority at his palace of Versailles, reducing the nobility to dependence on royal favor by requiring their attendance at court, and asserting that his power came directly from God ("divine right").
  • Louis maintained a large standing army and an extensive royal bureaucracy that bypassed traditional noble power structures, and pursued religious uniformity — most dramatically by revoking the Edict of Nantes (1685), reversing the toleration granted to Huguenots in Unit 2 and driving many to flee France.
  • In practice, absolutism was never total — local privileges, regional courts (parlements), and the nobility retained real influence that limited royal authority day to day.
  • The financial strain of maintaining Versailles, the army, and near-constant warfare left the French monarchy increasingly dependent on borrowing — a fiscal weakness that would resurface as a cause of the French Revolution in Unit 5.

3.2 Absolutism in Eastern Europe

  • Prussia under the Hohenzollerns built a highly militarized state, relying on an alliance between the crown and the Junkers (the landed noble class) who staffed both the army officer corps and the bureaucracy in exchange for control over their peasants.
  • The Austrian Habsburgs ruled a sprawling, multiethnic empire spanning German, Hungarian, Slavic, and Italian lands — a diversity that made the kind of administrative uniformity Louis XIV pursued far harder to achieve.
  • Russia under Peter the Great pursued aggressive Westernization — reforming the military, founding a new capital at St. Petersburg as a "window on the West," and compelling the nobility to adopt Western dress and customs.
  • As in France, these states relied on standing armies, centralized bureaucracies, and close ties between crown and an established church — but each adapted the absolutist model to its own social structure rather than simply copying Versailles.

3.3 Constitutionalism in England

  • England's 17th century was marked by conflict between crown and Parliament — the English Civil War (1640s) pitted Charles I against Parliamentary forces, ending in the king's execution (1649) and a republican Interregnum under Oliver Cromwell.
  • After the monarchy's restoration, continued conflict over royal power and religion culminated in the Glorious Revolution (1688), which replaced James II with William and Mary on terms set out in the English Bill of Rights (1689).
  • The Bill of Rights established constitutionalism — the principle that the monarch's power is limited by law and by Parliament, which alone could levy taxes and maintain a standing army — creating a durable constitutional monarchy.
  • This settlement gave England a stable framework strikingly different from absolutist France, and it would directly shape how Enlightenment thinkers like Locke (Unit 4) theorized government's purpose and limits.

3.4 The Dutch Republic

  • The Dutch Republic offered a third model entirely: a decentralized confederation of provinces, dominated by merchants rather than landed nobility or a monarch, governed through provincial assemblies (the States General) rather than a royal court.
  • Its 17th-century "Golden Age" combined enormous commercial wealth — built on shipping, finance, and overseas trade — with relatively high levels of religious toleration that drew persecuted minorities, including Jews and Protestant dissenters, from elsewhere in Europe.
  • By the 1700s the Republic declined relative to England and France, which could mobilize greater military and financial resources through more centralized institutions — but its example of prosperity without absolutism remained influential.
  • Together, English constitutionalism and Dutch toleration gave Enlightenment thinkers concrete alternatives to absolutism to point to — alternatives that Topic 4.2's social-contract theorists would draw on directly.

3.5 Comparison: Absolutism vs. Constitutionalism

France and England developed in strikingly different political directions after 1648 — a contrast the AP exam often asks students to explain and evaluate.

Absolutism (France) Constitutionalism (England)
Source of Authority Divine right of kings Consent embodied in Parliament/law
Role of Representative Bodies Estates-General not convened (1614–1789) Parliament holds the power of the purse
Religious Policy One official faith (revocation of Edict of Nantes, 1685) Established church, but growing toleration
Long-Term Outcome Crisis culminating in the French Revolution (Unit 5) Stable constitutional monarchy after 1688

Exam Tip

Don't overstate how "absolute" absolutism really was — even Louis XIV depended on cooperation from nobles and provincial elites to govern. The strongest essays treat "absolutism" as a goal monarchs pursued with varying success, not a description of how power actually worked day to day.

Key Vocabulary

Absolutism
System in which a monarch claims unrestricted authority, often justified by divine right.
Divine Right
Belief that a monarch's authority comes directly from God.
Versailles
Louis XIV's palace, symbol of French absolutism and a tool for controlling the nobility.
Glorious Revolution
1688 English revolution that established parliamentary supremacy.
English Bill of Rights
1689 document limiting royal power and affirming Parliament's authority.
Constitutionalism
Principle that a ruler's power is limited by law and representative institutions.
Junkers
Prussia's landed nobility, who staffed the army and bureaucracy under the Hohenzollerns.
Westernization
Peter the Great's program of adopting Western European models in Russia.
Dutch Republic
Decentralized, merchant-dominated state known for commercial wealth and toleration.
