Unit 5 Notes

Revolutions

c. 1750–1900 CE ~12–15% of exam 7 topics + comparison

Big Picture

Unit 5 traces how Enlightenment ideas about natural rights and government by consent ignited a wave of political revolutions across the Atlantic World — and how nationalism reshaped Europe afterward. The key question to keep asking: did a revolution actually change the social order, or just who was in charge?

5.1 The Enlightenment

  • The Enlightenment was an intellectual movement (centered in 17th–18th century Europe) emphasizing reason, observation, and natural rights over tradition and religious authority.
  • John Locke argued people possess natural rights — life, liberty, and property — and that legitimate government rests on the consent of the governed.
  • Rousseau developed the idea of the social contract and the "general will" of the people as the basis for legitimate authority; Montesquieu argued for separation of powers to prevent tyranny.
  • Adam Smith argued for laissez-faire economics — markets regulate themselves best with minimal government interference (The Wealth of Nations, 1776).
  • These ideas directly challenged traditional justifications for absolute monarchy (divine right) and the privileged status of the nobility and clergy — providing the ideological fuel for the revolutions that follow.

5.2 The American Revolution (1775–1783)

  • Thirteen British colonies in North America rebelled, citing "taxation without representation" and the violation of natural rights described by Locke.
  • The resulting U.S. Constitution (1789) put Enlightenment political theory into practice: a federal system, separation of powers among three branches, and a Bill of Rights protecting individual liberties.
  • Often viewed as the first major revolution explicitly grounded in Enlightenment principles — its success and ideology helped inspire the French and Latin American revolutions that followed.

5.3 The French Revolution (1789–1799)

  • Causes: a severe financial crisis (worsened by debt from supporting the American Revolution), deep social inequality under the Estates System (clergy and nobility held privileges and paid little tax, while commoners bore the tax burden), and widespread circulation of Enlightenment ideas.
  • Key events: the storming of the Bastille (1789), the Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen, and the abolition of feudal privileges.
  • The revolution radicalized into the Reign of Terror under Robespierre and the Committee of Public Safety, during which thousands of perceived enemies of the revolution were executed.
  • Napoleon Bonaparte eventually seized power; his conquests spread revolutionary ideas (and the Napoleonic Code, a unified legal system) across Europe — even while he ruled as emperor.

5.4 The Haitian Revolution (1791–1804)

  • Saint-Domingue (French colony, today Haiti) was an extraordinarily profitable sugar colony built on a brutal slave system where the enslaved population vastly outnumbered free colonists.
  • Inspired in part by French revolutionary ideals of liberty and equality — but the Haitian Revolution pushed those ideals further than France itself was willing to go, demanding the end of slavery entirely.
  • Led by figures including Toussaint Louverture, the revolution resulted in Haiti's independence in 1804 — the only successful large-scale slave revolt to create an independent state, and the first Black-led republic in the world.
  • Haiti's example terrified slaveholding societies elsewhere in the Americas and influenced debates over slavery for decades.

5.5 Latin American Independence Movements

  • Independence movements across Spanish and Portuguese America were fueled by Enlightenment ideas, the example of the American and French (and Haitian) Revolutions, and a key triggering event: Napoleon's invasion of Spain (1808), which disrupted Spanish colonial authority.
  • Major leaders: Simón Bolívar ("El Liberador," led independence movements in northern South America) and José de San Martín (southern South America).
  • These movements were largely led by criollos (people of European descent born in the Americas) who wanted political independence and power for themselves — but often had little interest in restructuring society for Indigenous, mixed-race, or enslaved populations. Independence frequently did not mean major social change.
  • Brazil's path to independence (1822) was comparatively peaceful: it became independent under Pedro I, heir to the Portuguese throne — resulting in a monarchy rather than a republic.

5.6 Nationalism & Unification in Europe

  • Nationalism — the belief that people sharing a language, culture, or ethnicity should form their own self-governing state — became a powerful force in 19th-century Europe.
  • Italian unification (the Risorgimento) brought together a collection of separate states under the Kingdom of Sardinia-Piedmont, with figures like Giuseppe Garibaldi leading military campaigns; largely completed by 1871.
  • German unification was driven by Prussia under Otto von Bismarck, who used a series of deliberate wars (including the Franco-Prussian War) to unify German states under Prussian leadership, culminating in the German Empire (1871).
  • Nationalism was double-edged: it could unify fragmented regions into new states, but it also threatened to tear apart multi-ethnic empires (Ottoman, Austro-Hungarian, Russian) where different ethnic groups began demanding their own independent states — a tension that becomes central to Unit 7.

5.7 Causes of the Industrial Revolution

  • The Industrial Revolution began in Britain in the mid-1700s due to a convergence of factors: access to abundant coal and iron, agricultural improvements (the enclosure movement, which consolidated farmland and pushed rural workers toward cities), capital accumulated through colonial and Atlantic trade, relatively secure property rights, and growing consumer markets.
  • Key early innovations: the improved steam engine (James Watt) and mechanized textile production (the spinning jenny and power loom), which shifted production from homes/workshops to factories.
  • This topic is the bridge to Unit 6 — the Industrial Revolution's causes belong here, while its global consequences (imperialism, new economic ideologies, urbanization, environmental change) are covered next.

5.8 Comparison: How Revolutionary Were These Revolutions?

All four Atlantic revolutions invoked "liberty" and "equality" — but each defined those terms very differently in practice.

Revolution Primary Goal Enlightenment Influence Effect on Social Hierarchy
American Independence from Britain; self-governance Strong — natural rights, consent of the governed Limited: slavery persisted; property qualifications for voting often remained
French End absolute monarchy; restructure society Strong — popular sovereignty, Rights of Man Major upheaval (abolished feudal privileges) but ended in Napoleon's empire
Haitian End slavery and colonial rule Radicalized beyond France's own application of "liberty" Most transformative: slavery abolished, independent Black-led state created
Latin American Independence from Spain/Portugal Moderate — focused on political independence for criollo elites Limited: racial and class hierarchies largely persisted after independence

Exam Tip

A high-scoring response distinguishes political change (who holds power) from social change (how society is structured). Latin American independence is the clearest example: criollo elites replaced peninsular Spanish officials at the top of government, but the casta system and economic structures underneath largely stayed intact.

Key Vocabulary

Natural Rights
Rights (life, liberty, property) believed to belong to all people inherently, per Locke.
Social Contract
The idea that legitimate government derives from an agreement among the people it governs.
Laissez-Faire
Economic philosophy favoring minimal government intervention in markets.
Estates System
Pre-revolutionary French social structure dividing society into clergy, nobility, and commoners with unequal privileges.
Reign of Terror
Radical phase of the French Revolution marked by mass executions of perceived enemies of the revolution.
Napoleonic Code
Unified legal code spread across Europe by Napoleon, influencing legal systems long after his fall.
Criollo
A person of European descent born in the Americas; led most Latin American independence movements.
Nationalism
Belief that people sharing language, culture, or ethnicity should form their own self-governing state.
Risorgimento
The 19th-century movement for Italian unification.
Enclosure Movement
Consolidation of English farmland that displaced rural workers, contributing to urban industrial labor supply.
