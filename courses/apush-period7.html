Period 7 Notes

Wars & the Modern State

1898–1945 ~17% of exam 8 topics + comparison

Big Picture

Period 7 is the largest period on the exam, covering the U.S.'s emergence as a world power, the explosion of Progressive reform, two world wars, and the most severe economic crisis in American history. The throughline: the federal government's role in American life expands dramatically and permanently — from the relatively limited 19th-century state to the regulatory and social-welfare state that exists today.

7.1 American Imperialism & the Path to World Power

  • Following the Spanish-American War (1898), the U.S. acquired the Philippines, Puerto Rico, and Guam, and established a protectorate-like relationship with Cuba through the Platt Amendment.
  • The Philippine-American War (1899–1902) — a brutal conflict against Filipino independence fighters — sparked debate within the U.S. between anti-imperialists and those favoring overseas expansion.
  • The Open Door Policy toward China sought equal trading access for all foreign powers, reflecting U.S. interest in Asian markets without formal colonization.
  • Theodore Roosevelt's foreign policy — the "Big Stick" approach, the Roosevelt Corollary to the Monroe Doctrine (asserting a U.S. right to intervene in Latin America), and construction of the Panama Canal (completed 1914) — established the U.S. as the dominant power in the Western Hemisphere.

7.2 The Progressive Era: Reform at Home

  • Progressivism was a broad, sometimes contradictory reform movement responding to the problems of industrialization, urbanization, and immigration documented in Period 6.
  • Muckraking journalists — like Upton Sinclair, whose The Jungle exposed the meatpacking industry — built public support for reform by exposing corruption and unsafe conditions.
  • Political reforms expanded democracy: direct primaries, the initiative/referendum/recall, direct election of senators (17th Amendment, 1913), and women's suffrage (19th Amendment, 1920) after decades of activism by figures like Susan B. Anthony and Alice Paul.
  • Economic reforms included trust-busting under Roosevelt and (more aggressively) Wilson, the Federal Reserve System (1913), a graduated income tax (16th Amendment, 1913), and food/drug safety regulation (Pure Food and Drug Act, 1906).
  • Progressivism had real limits — it largely failed to address racial discrimination, and some Progressives held views (on eugenics or immigration restriction) that look troubling by later standards.

7.3 World War I: Causes, U.S. Entry & the Home Front

  • The U.S. initially pursued neutrality after WWI began in Europe (1914), but German submarine warfare (the sinking of the Lusitania, 1915) and the Zimmermann Telegram (1917, revealing a German proposal to ally with Mexico against the U.S.) pushed the U.S. toward entry in 1917.
  • On the home front, government expanded dramatically: the War Industries Board coordinated production, the Committee on Public Information produced propaganda, and the Espionage and Sedition Acts restricted dissent.
  • The Great Migration — large numbers of African Americans moving from the rural South to Northern cities for industrial jobs — began accelerating during this period, partly driven by wartime labor demands.
  • After the war, the Treaty of Versailles and the question of joining the League of Nations became a major political controversy; the Senate ultimately rejected the Treaty, reflecting a resurgence of isolationist sentiment.

7.4 The 1920s: Economy, Culture & Backlash

  • The "Roaring Twenties" saw economic growth driven by new consumer goods (automobiles, radios, household appliances) and new advertising and credit practices encouraging mass consumption.
  • The Harlem Renaissance — a flowering of African American literature, music (jazz), and art — and changing gender norms symbolized by the "flapper" marked major cultural shifts.
  • A cultural backlash also emerged: a revived Ku Klux Klan (now targeting Catholics, Jews, and immigrants as well as African Americans), the Immigration Act of 1924 (imposing strict quotas favoring Northern and Western Europeans), and Prohibition (18th Amendment) — which proved difficult to enforce and fueled organized crime.
  • The decade ended with the stock market crash of October 1929, driven partly by speculative excess (buying stocks "on margin") and underlying weaknesses like overproduction and uneven income distribution.

7.5 The Great Depression: Causes & Effects

  • Beyond the stock market crash, deeper causes included a wave of bank failures that wiped out savings, overproduction in agriculture and industry relative to consumer purchasing power, and the collapse of international trade — worsened by protectionist tariffs like Smoot-Hawley (1930).
  • Unemployment reached roughly 25% at its worst. Widespread suffering included "Hoovervilles" (shantytowns), breadlines, and the Dust Bowl — severe drought and soil erosion that devastated Great Plains agriculture and displaced many farming families.
  • President Hoover's response emphasized voluntary cooperation and limited federal intervention — seen by many as inadequate to the crisis's scale, contributing to his defeat in the 1932 election.

7.6 The New Deal: Programs & Debates

  • Franklin D. Roosevelt's New Deal (beginning 1933) dramatically expanded the federal government's role in the economy through programs often grouped as "relief, recovery, and reform."
  • Relief programs (Civilian Conservation Corps, Works Progress Administration) provided direct employment; recovery measures (National Recovery Administration, Agricultural Adjustment Act) attempted to stabilize prices and production; reform measures (Securities and Exchange Commission, Glass-Steagall Act, Social Security Act of 1935) aimed to prevent future crises and provide a lasting safety net.
  • The New Deal drew criticism from multiple directions: conservatives argued it gave government too much power and ran large deficits, while figures like Huey Long argued it didn't go far enough to redistribute wealth.
  • The Supreme Court initially struck down several New Deal programs as unconstitutional, leading to FDR's controversial — and ultimately abandoned — "court-packing" plan (1937).
  • The New Deal did not fully end the Depression (which persisted until wartime mobilization), but it permanently expanded expectations of the federal government's role in providing economic security — its most lasting legacy.

7.7 World War II: Path to War & Mobilization

  • Through the 1930s, the U.S. maintained official neutrality (the Neutrality Acts) even as fascist aggression escalated in Europe and Japan invaded China.
  • The U.S. provided growing support to Britain and other Allies through measures like Lend-Lease (1941) while officially remaining neutral.
  • Japan's attack on Pearl Harbor (December 7, 1941) brought the U.S. fully into the war against both Japan and, through alliance commitments, Germany and Italy.
  • The war drove total mobilization of American economy and society: government agencies coordinated production, women entered the workforce in large numbers (symbolized by "Rosie the Riveter"), and the draft expanded the military dramatically.

7.8 World War II: Conduct, the Home Front & Aftermath

  • The U.S. fought a two-front war — in Europe (alongside Britain and the USSR against Germany and Italy) and the Pacific (against Japan) — leading the European theater's D-Day invasion (1944) and a Pacific island-hopping campaign.
  • On the home front, wartime production ended the Depression's unemployment — but the war also brought serious civil liberties violations, most notably the internment of roughly 120,000 Japanese Americans (Executive Order 9066) based on ancestry rather than evidence of disloyalty.
  • African Americans and other minorities faced continued discrimination even while serving in segregated military units and contributing to war production — tensions that fed directly into the postwar civil rights movement (Period 8).
  • The war ended with Germany's surrender (May 1945) and Japan's surrender (September 1945) after the atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki — leaving the U.S. and USSR as the world's two dominant powers, setting up the Cold War (Period 8).

7.9 Comparison: Government's Role in the Economy

Both the Progressive Era and the New Deal expanded federal power over the economy — but for different reasons, and to very different degrees.

Progressive Era New Deal
Scope of Intervention Regulation of existing markets and businesses Direct relief programs, public employment, and a permanent safety net
Primary Goal Curb corruption and abuses of industrial capitalism Respond to mass unemployment and economic collapse
Key Legislation Federal Reserve Act, Pure Food and Drug Act, 16th/17th/19th Amendments Social Security Act, Glass-Steagall Act, Wagner Act
Lasting Legacy Established government's regulatory role over business Established government's role in providing economic security

Exam Tip

This is a classic continuity-and-change prompt: the New Deal did not invent the idea of government intervention — it extended a trajectory that began with Progressive reforms — but it dramatically increased the scale and permanence of that intervention, especially through Social Security.
