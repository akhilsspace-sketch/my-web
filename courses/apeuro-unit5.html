Unit 5 Notes

Conflict, Crisis & Reaction in the Late 18th Century

c. 1648–1815 ~8% of exam 4 topics + comparison

Big Picture

Unit 5 covers the most dramatic rupture of the early modern period: the French Revolution and its aftermath. Ideas about natural rights and popular sovereignty (Unit 4) collided with the realities of absolutist France (Unit 3), producing a revolution that moved from moderate reform to radical terror, gave rise to Napoleon's empire, and ended with a conservative settlement at the Congress of Vienna — a settlement that tried, but ultimately failed, to put the genie of nationalism and liberalism back in the bottle, as Unit 7 will show.

5.1 The French Revolution: Causes & Moderate Phase

  • The Revolution's causes combined Enlightenment ideas about rights and government (Unit 4), a severe financial crisis driven by decades of war debts (Unit 3), and resentment of the Third Estate toward the legal and tax privileges of the nobility and clergy.
  • 1789 brought the convening of the Estates-General, the Third Estate's break to form a National Assembly, and the storming of the Bastille — followed by the National Assembly's August decrees abolishing feudal privileges and its Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen, which translated Locke's and Rousseau's ideas (Unit 4) into a political program.
  • In its moderate phase, the Assembly wrote a new constitution (1791) that preserved the monarchy but subordinated it to an elected legislature — a constitutional monarchy modeled loosely on the English system from Unit 3.
  • This moderate settlement proved short-lived: foreign monarchies, alarmed by events in France, threatened intervention, while many French citizens grew suspicious that the king himself was conspiring with France's enemies.

5.2 The Radical Phase & Reign of Terror

  • War with Austria and Prussia, combined with internal counter-revolutionary uprisings, pushed the Revolution toward radicalism: the monarchy was abolished and a republic declared (1792), and Louis XVI was tried and executed (1793).
  • The Reign of Terror (1793–1794), led by Robespierre and the Committee of Public Safety, used mass executions by guillotine to defend the Revolution against perceived internal and external enemies.
  • The levée en masse — mass conscription of citizens into the army — combined with revolutionary nationalism and the idea of popular sovereignty to create a new kind of mass, ideologically-driven warfare, unlike the smaller professional armies of the absolutist era (Unit 3).
  • The Terror ended with Robespierre's own execution in 1794 (the Thermidorian Reaction), and a more moderate but unstable government (the Directory) took power — setting the stage for Napoleon's rise.

5.3 Napoleon's Rise, Empire & Fall

  • Napoleon Bonaparte rose to power amid the Directory's instability, seizing power as First Consul in a 1799 coup and crowning himself Emperor in 1804.
  • Napoleon consolidated many revolutionary changes through the Napoleonic Code — establishing legal equality before the law, religious toleration, and the permanent abolition of feudal privileges across the territories he controlled.
  • His conquests spread these reforms across much of Europe, but the experience of French occupation also provoked nationalist resistance in Spain, the German states, and elsewhere — planting seeds of the nationalism that Unit 7 will trace through the unifications of Italy and Germany.
  • The disastrous invasion of Russia (1812) destroyed the bulk of Napoleon's army, and a coalition of European powers defeated him by 1815 (Waterloo) — clearing the way for the conservative settlement of Topic 5.4.

5.4 The Congress of Vienna

  • The Congress of Vienna (1814–1815) brought together the victorious powers — led by Austria's Metternich, alongside Russia, Prussia, Britain, and a restored France represented by Talleyrand — to redraw Europe's map after over two decades of revolutionary and Napoleonic warfare.
  • The settlement rested on three guiding principles: legitimacy (restoring deposed royal dynasties, including the Bourbons in France), balance of power (ensuring no single state, especially France, could again dominate the continent), and compensation (redistributing territory among the great powers for their wartime losses and efforts).
  • To contain France, the Congress created stronger "buffer states" on its borders — including a strengthened Kingdom of the Netherlands and an enlarged Prussia along the Rhine — and established the Concert of Europe, in which the great powers would meet periodically to preserve this settlement.
  • The Vienna settlement deliberately tried to suppress the nationalism and liberal, constitutional ideas unleashed by the Revolution and Napoleon (Topics 5.2–5.3) — but as Unit 7 will show, this conservative order could contain those forces only temporarily.

5.5 Comparison: Revolutionary Goals vs. 1815 Outcomes

By 1815, the political map looked conservative — but the Revolution's social and ideological legacy proved far more durable than its governments.

1789 Revolutionary Goals 1815 Outcomes
Political System Constitutional, representative government Bourbon monarchy restored in France; conservative monarchies across Europe
Social Structure Abolish feudal privilege and legal inequality Legal equality and the end of feudalism largely preserved via the Napoleonic Code's spread
Ideas Unleashed Natural rights and popular sovereignty (Unit 4) Nationalism and liberalism become permanent forces challenging the Vienna settlement (Unit 7)
International Order Not an initial goal Concert of Europe / balance of power established to prevent future revolutionary wars

Exam Tip

A high-scoring essay on the French Revolution's "legacy" should note this paradox: politically, 1815 looks like a return to the past (restored monarchies), but socially and ideologically, there was no going back — legal equality, nationalism, and the principle of popular sovereignty all survived and would resurface throughout the 19th century.

Key Vocabulary

Estates-General
France's traditional representative assembly, convened in 1789 for the first time since 1614.
National Assembly
Body formed by the Third Estate that began the French Revolution's moderate phase.
Declaration of the Rights of Man
1789 document proclaiming liberty, equality, and natural rights as the basis of government.
Reign of Terror
1793–94 period of mass executions during the radical phase of the French Revolution.
Levée en Masse
Mass conscription policy that mobilized the French population for war.
Napoleonic Code
Legal code establishing equality before the law across Napoleon's empire.
Congress of Vienna
1814–15 meeting that redrew Europe's map and restored conservative order after Napoleon.
Balance of Power
Principle that no single state should be powerful enough to dominate the continent.
Concert of Europe
System of great-power meetings established after 1815 to preserve the Vienna settlement.
