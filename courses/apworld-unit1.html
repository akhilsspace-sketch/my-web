Unit 1 Notes

The Global Tapestry

c. 1200–1450 CE ~8–10% of exam 6 topics + comparison

Big Picture

Before sustained contact between hemispheres, societies across Asia, Africa, the Americas, and Europe developed along their own paths — but they shared common challenges: how to legitimize a ruler's power, organize an economy, and hold together diverse populations. Watch for how each region answers these questions differently — that comparison is the heart of Unit 1.

1.1 East Asia: Song China & Japan

  • Song Dynasty (960–1279) ruled a prosperous, culturally advanced China after the chaos following the Tang collapse.
  • Government run by scholar-officials chosen through a civil service examination system based on Confucian classics — in theory meritocratic, though wealthy families could afford the tutors needed to pass.
  • Economic boom driven by Champa rice (a fast-ripening strain that allowed multiple harvests per year), expansion of the Grand Canal, and growth of manufacturing (iron, steel, porcelain).
  • Major innovations: the magnetic compass (improved long-distance navigation), gunpowder weapons, and movable-type printing, which spread literacy and Confucian learning.
  • Neo-Confucianism revived Confucian ethics while absorbing Buddhist and Daoist ideas — it reinforced rigid gender roles (e.g., foot binding among elite women) and the importance of filial piety.
  • The Yuan Dynasty (1271–1368) — Mongol rule of China under Kublai Khan — kept the bureaucracy but placed foreigners (including Central Asians and some Europeans, like Marco Polo) above ethnic Chinese in the administration.
  • Japan developed a decentralized, feudal-style structure: the emperor was a symbolic figurehead while real power rested with the shogun (military ruler) and regional daimyo lords, defended by samurai bound by a code of loyalty and honor.

1.2 Dar al-Islam

  • The Abbasid Caliphate (centered in Baghdad) had unified much of the Islamic world but fractured as regional governors became effectively independent; the Mongol sack of Baghdad in 1258 ended it as a political power.
  • In its place, regional powers emerged: the Seljuk Turks (Central Asian converts to Islam who took over much of the former Abbasid heartland) and the Mamluk Sultanate in Egypt, founded by former slave-soldiers who seized power from their rulers.
  • Despite political fragmentation, Dar al-Islam ("the house of Islam") remained a single cultural and economic zone — sitting at the crossroads of the trans-Saharan, Indian Ocean, and Silk Road trade networks.
  • Centers of learning preserved and expanded on Greek, Persian, and Indian scholarship in mathematics, astronomy, and medicine — knowledge that would later flow into Europe.
  • Sufism — a mystical, devotional branch of Islam emphasizing a direct personal relationship with God — proved highly adaptable to local customs. Sufi missionaries were major drivers of Islam's spread into Anatolia, sub-Saharan Africa, and South/Southeast Asia.

1.3 South & Southeast Asia

  • The Delhi Sultanate (1206–1526) brought Turkic Muslim rule to a Hindu-majority northern India. Policy toward non-Muslims ranged from relative tolerance (allowing Hindu worship in exchange for the jizya tax) to periods of temple destruction.
  • Religious blending was common: the bhakti movement (devotional Hinduism focused on a personal bond with a chosen deity) shared its emphasis on personal devotion with Sufi Islam, and the two traditions influenced each other.
  • Southeast Asian states were built on maritime trade rather than agriculture or conquest. Srivijaya (Sumatra) grew wealthy by controlling the Strait of Malacca — a chokepoint for Indian Ocean shipping.
  • Hinduism and Buddhism spread to Southeast Asia along these trade routes and were adopted by local rulers (e.g., the temple complexes at Angkor) to reinforce their authority — a pattern of religion legitimizing rule that recurs across this unit.

1.4 State Building in the Americas

  • The Mexica (Aztec) Empire built its capital, Tenochtitlan, on an island in Lake Texcoco, using chinampas (raised, highly productive farming plots) to feed a huge urban population.
  • The Aztecs ruled through the Triple Alliance (with Texcoco and Tlacopan) and expanded by conquest, but generally left conquered rulers in place — extracting tribute (goods, labor, and captives for religious sacrifice) rather than directly administering new territory.
  • The Inca Empire took the opposite approach in the Andes: a highly centralized bureaucracy connected by an extensive road network (roughly 25,000 miles), with quipu (knotted cords) used for record-keeping.
  • Instead of monetary taxes, the Inca used the mit'a system — a labor tax requiring communities to contribute work on roads, terraces, and military service.
  • In North America, the Mississippian culture (centered on the mound-building city of Cahokia) supported a large population through maize agriculture and a chiefdom-based social structure, while Ancestral Pueblo peoples in the arid Southwest built cliff dwellings and relied on irrigation.

1.5 State Building in Africa

  • The Mali Empire (c. 1230–1600) grew rich by controlling the trans-Saharan gold-salt trade. Its most famous ruler, Mansa Musa, made a lavish pilgrimage (hajj) to Mecca in 1324–25 that advertised Mali's wealth across the Islamic world and beyond.
  • Timbuktu, part of Mali, became a major center of Islamic scholarship, with libraries and a university attracting students from across Africa and the Middle East.
  • Great Zimbabwe was a stone-walled city-state in southeastern Africa that controlled gold flowing toward the Swahili Coast and into Indian Ocean trade networks.
  • The Swahili city-states (e.g., Kilwa, Mombasa) blended Bantu and Islamic cultures — producing the Swahili language (Bantu grammar with Arabic loanwords) — and prospered as hubs of Indian Ocean commerce.
  • Ethiopia, under its Christian Solomonic dynasty, remained a Christian kingdom (with rock-hewn churches at Lalibela) even while surrounded by predominantly Muslim neighbors — maintaining ties to other Christian states across the Mediterranean.

1.6 Developments in Europe

  • The Catholic Church was the major unifying institution in a politically fragmented Western Europe — the pope held religious authority and significant political influence, and the Church was a major landholder.
  • Political power was organized through feudalism (monarchs granted land, or fiefs, to nobles in exchange for military service) and manorialism (an economic system where serfs worked a lord's land in exchange for protection).
  • The Holy Roman Empire was a loose confederation of mostly German and Italian territories — the emperor's authority was often more symbolic than real.
  • The Byzantine Empire (the continuation of the Eastern Roman Empire) preserved Orthodox Christianity, which had formally split from Catholicism in the Great Schism of 1054, and its legal tradition (Justinian's Code) influenced law across Europe.

1.7 Comparison: How States Built & Justified Power

This topic is where Unit 1 gets tested most directly — you're expected to compare the methods different regions used to consolidate and legitimize political authority during this period.

Region How Rulers Legitimized Power Administrative Style Economic Foundation
Song / Yuan China Neo-Confucian ideology & the "mandate of heaven" supported a scholar-official class Centralized bureaucracy staffed via civil service exams Intensive agriculture (Champa rice) + manufacturing & trade
Dar al-Islam Religious authority (caliph, later sultans) tied to Islam; Sufism aided integration of new converts Shifted from unified caliphate to competing regional sultanates Control of trans-regional trade routes (Silk Road, Indian Ocean)
Mali (Africa) Rulers adopted Islam for prestige & diplomatic ties while blending it with local traditions Centralized monarchy with provincial governors Trans-Saharan gold-salt trade
Aztec (Americas) Military conquest & religious ideology (sacrifice to sustain the cosmos) justified dominance Indirect rule through tribute; local rulers often kept in place Tribute from subject states + chinampa agriculture
Inca (Americas) Emperor considered descendant of the sun god; state religion reinforced hierarchy Highly centralized bureaucracy; roads & quipu enabled control Mit'a labor tax (rotational labor instead of goods/money)
Europe Catholic Church provided shared religious legitimacy amid political division Decentralized feudal hierarchy of monarchs, nobles, and vassals Manorialism / localized agriculture

Exam Tip

A common FRQ/DBQ angle is "compare how two of these societies used religion (or tribute, or bureaucracy) to maintain power." Notice that religion-as-legitimacy shows up almost everywhere — but the relationship between ruler and religious institution differs (e.g., the Inca emperor was divine, while the Catholic Church was an institution separate from — and sometimes rival to — European monarchs).

Key Vocabulary

Neo-Confucianism
Revival of Confucian ethics blended with Buddhist/Daoist ideas; reinforced strict social and gender hierarchies in Song China.
Champa Rice
Fast-ripening rice strain that allowed multiple harvests per year, fueling population growth in Song China.
Dar al-Islam
"The house of Islam" — the broad cultural and economic zone of Muslim-influenced regions, even when politically divided.
Sufism
Mystical branch of Islam emphasizing personal connection to God; its missionaries spread Islam through adaptation to local customs.
Mamluk Sultanate
Egypt-based state founded by former slave-soldiers (mamluks) who seized power from their rulers.
Bhakti Movement
Hindu devotional movement emphasizing a personal relationship with a chosen deity; shared themes with Sufism.
Srivijaya
Maritime trade-based kingdom on Sumatra that controlled the Strait of Malacca.
Chinampas
Raised agricultural plots built in lake shallows; allowed Tenochtitlan to support a dense population.
Triple Alliance
Coalition of Tenochtitlan, Texcoco, and Tlacopan that formed the core of the Aztec Empire.
Mit'a System
Inca labor tax requiring rotational work on state projects in place of monetary or goods-based taxes.
Quipu
System of knotted cords used by the Inca for record-keeping and communication across the empire.
Trans-Saharan Trade
Network of routes across the Sahara linking West Africa's gold and salt to North Africa and the Mediterranean.
Swahili Coast
East African coastal region where Bantu and Islamic cultures blended; key node in Indian Ocean trade.
Manorialism
Economic system in medieval Europe where serfs worked a lord's land in exchange for protection.
Great Schism (1054)
Formal split between the Roman Catholic and Eastern Orthodox churches.
Jizya
Tax paid by non-Muslims under Muslim rule, often in exchange for protection and freedom of worship.
