Unit 7 Notes

Global Conflict

c. 1900–present ~8–10% of exam 7 topics + comparison

Big Picture

Unit 7 covers the two world wars and the turbulent period between them. The throughline: industrial-scale warfare made conflict more destructive than ever, while the unresolved problems of one war directly fed into the next. Pay close attention to causation chains — this unit rewards tracing how one event's consequences become the next event's causes.

7.1 Shifting Power & the Causes of World War I

  • By 1900, European powers had built dense webs of military alliances — the Triple Alliance (Germany, Austria-Hungary, Italy) and Triple Entente (France, Russia, Britain) — alongside rising nationalism, militarism, and competition over colonial territory.
  • The assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand of Austria-Hungary (1914) was the immediate trigger, but it was the alliance system that turned a regional crisis in the Balkans into a continent-wide — and eventually global — war.
  • New industrial military technology — machine guns, poison gas, tanks, airplanes, and submarines — produced unprecedented casualties and the grinding stalemate of trench warfare on the Western Front.

7.2 World War I: Conduct & Effects

  • WWI was a total war — entire economies and civilian populations were mobilized for the war effort through rationing, propaganda, and, notably, women entering industrial jobs vacated by soldiers.
  • Colonial troops and resources from across European empires were drawn into the fighting, exposing colonial subjects to new ideas (including nationalism) that would fuel later independence movements.
  • The Treaty of Versailles (1919) imposed harsh reparations and territorial losses on Germany — fueling deep resentment that later contributed to the rise of Nazism.
  • The German, Austro-Hungarian, Russian, and Ottoman empires all collapsed, redrawing the map of Europe and the Middle East into new nation-states based on (imperfectly applied) nationalist principles.
  • The mandate system, under the new League of Nations, placed former Ottoman and German colonial territories under British or French administration rather than granting independence — frustrating nationalist movements, especially in the Middle East.
  • The League of Nations was created to prevent future wars but had no real enforcement power, and crucially, the United States never joined.

7.3 The Russian Revolution

  • Long-standing grievances against autocratic tsarist rule, combined with WWI's enormous casualties and economic strain, triggered the 1917 revolutions.
  • The February Revolution overthrew the tsar; the October Revolution brought the Bolsheviks, led by Lenin, to power on the promise of "Peace, Land, Bread."
  • Russia exited WWI via the Treaty of Brest-Litovsk, then fought a civil war between the Bolshevik "Reds" and anti-Bolshevik "Whites" — the Bolsheviks won, establishing the Soviet Union in 1922.
  • The USSR became the world's first major communist state, implementing state control of the economy through Five-Year Plans under Stalin — and serving as both inspiration and ideological rival for movements worldwide for the rest of the century.

7.4 The Interwar Period: Economic Crisis & Authoritarianism

  • The Great Depression (beginning with the 1929 U.S. stock market crash) caused a severe global economic downturn — mass unemployment and the collapse of international trade.
  • Economic instability, combined with perceived failures of liberal democracy, fueled the rise of authoritarian and fascist movements.
  • Fascism — in Mussolini's Italy and Hitler's Germany — combined extreme nationalism, a powerful single leader, suppression of political opposition, and (in Nazi Germany's case) a genocidal racial ideology targeting Jews and other groups.
  • Totalitarian states, whether fascist or communist, sought to control nearly all aspects of public and private life through propaganda, surveillance, and terror.

7.5 Causes of World War II

  • Unresolved grievances from WWI — especially German resentment of the Treaty of Versailles — created fertile ground for renewed conflict.
  • The League of Nations repeatedly failed to stop aggression: Japan's invasion of Manchuria (1931) and Italy's invasion of Ethiopia (1935) both went unpunished.
  • Appeasement — most famously the 1938 Munich Agreement, in which Britain and France allowed Hitler to annex part of Czechoslovakia in hopes of avoiding war — emboldened further German expansion.
  • The war began in separate theaters that merged into one global conflict: Japan's invasion of China (1937) in Asia, and Germany's invasion of Poland (1939) in Europe. The U.S. entered after Japan's attack on Pearl Harbor (1941).

7.6 World War II: Conduct & Mass Atrocities

  • WWII was fought across truly global theaters — Europe, North Africa, and the Pacific — and pushed military technology even further, including the aerial bombing of cities and, finally, atomic bombs dropped on Hiroshima and Nagasaki (1945).
  • The Holocaust — Nazi Germany's systematic genocide of six million European Jews, alongside the persecution and murder of Roma people, people with disabilities, and political dissidents — stands as the era's defining atrocity.
  • Other state-organized mass violence against civilians occurred across this broader period as well (e.g., the Armenian Genocide during WWI, Japanese war crimes across occupied Asia) — a reminder that this period's "total wars" repeatedly blurred the line between combatants and civilian populations.

7.7 Effects of World War II

  • WWII caused tens of millions of deaths and massive infrastructure destruction, especially across Europe and East Asia.
  • The war ended European dominance of global affairs — the United States and the Soviet Union emerged as the world's two superpowers, setting up the Cold War (Unit 8).
  • The United Nations (1945) was established with stronger enforcement mechanisms than the League of Nations — though still limited by the veto power of its Security Council's permanent members.
  • The war severely weakened European colonial powers militarily, economically, and morally — directly accelerating the decolonization movements that define much of Unit 8.

7.8 Comparison: Causes of WWI vs. WWII

A classic causation question: to what extent did WWI cause WWII? Notice the direct causal chain alongside the genuinely separate factors.

World War I World War II
Underlying Causes Alliance systems, militarism, nationalism, imperial rivalry WWI's unresolved grievances (Versailles), Great Depression, expansionist fascist ideology
Trigger Assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand (1914) Invasion of Poland (1939) / invasion of China (1937) in Asia
Role of International System Rigid alliances escalated a regional crisis into continental war League of Nations' failure to stop aggression + policy of appeasement
Resulting Order League of Nations (weak); redrawn map of Europe/Middle East United Nations (stronger); U.S.–USSR bipolar order (Cold War)

Exam Tip

Don't treat WWII as only a consequence of WWI — the Great Depression and Japanese imperial ambitions in Asia had largely independent origins. A strong causation essay acknowledges both the direct chain (Versailles → German resentment → Hitler's rise) and factors that would have caused instability regardless.

Key Vocabulary

Total War
A war effort that mobilizes a nation's entire population and economy, not just its military.
Treaty of Versailles
1919 treaty ending WWI that imposed harsh reparations and territorial losses on Germany.
Mandate System
League of Nations arrangement placing former German/Ottoman colonies under British or French administration.
Bolsheviks
Communist party led by Lenin that seized power in Russia's October Revolution.
Five-Year Plans
Soviet programs of centrally planned, state-directed economic development under Stalin.
Great Depression
Severe global economic downturn beginning in 1929.
Fascism
Authoritarian ultranationalist ideology, exemplified by Mussolini's Italy and Hitler's Germany.
Appeasement
Policy of conceding to an aggressor's demands to avoid conflict, exemplified by the 1938 Munich Agreement.
Holocaust
Nazi Germany's systematic genocide of six million Jews and persecution of other groups during WWII.
United Nations
International organization founded in 1945 to maintain peace, with stronger enforcement than the League of Nations.
