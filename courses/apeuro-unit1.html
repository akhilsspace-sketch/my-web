Unit 1 Notes

Renaissance and Exploration

c. 1450–1648 ~11% of exam 5 topics + comparison

Big Picture

Unit 1 opens the course with two transformative developments in early modern Europe: the Renaissance's revival of classical learning and human-centered art, and the Age of Exploration that connected Europe to a wider Atlantic World. Alongside these, newly centralizing "New Monarchs" began building the early modern states that would compete for power for centuries. Together, these developments set the stage for the religious upheavals of Unit 2 and the political struggles of Unit 3.

1.1 The Italian Renaissance: Humanism & Art

  • The Renaissance ("rebirth") began in wealthy Italian city-states like Florence and Venice in the 1300s–1400s, fueled by trade wealth and powerful patron families such as the Medici.
  • Humanism — a renewed focus on classical Greek and Roman texts and on human potential and achievement — encouraged secular subjects to be studied alongside religious ones. Petrarch was an early and influential humanist figure.
  • Renaissance art (Leonardo da Vinci, Michelangelo, Raphael) emphasized realism, linear perspective, and the human form, reflecting humanist values about the dignity and capability of individuals.
  • Patronage by wealthy families and the Church drove artistic production — competition for prestige among Italian city-states fueled an explosion of new art, architecture, and scholarship.

1.2 The Renaissance Spreads North & the Printing Press

  • The printing press (Gutenberg, c. 1450) dramatically accelerated the spread of ideas and literacy — a precondition for how quickly Reformation ideas would spread in Unit 2.
  • The Northern Renaissance (Erasmus, Dürer) had a more religious and moral-reform flavor than the Italian Renaissance's classical revival, applying humanist methods to religious life.
  • Christian humanism — associated above all with Erasmus — applied humanist textual criticism to scripture itself, producing new translations and exposing inconsistencies between the early Church and contemporary practice.
  • By criticizing clerical corruption and calling for a return to scripture, Christian humanists like Erasmus did not intend to split the Church — but their critiques supplied much of the intellectual ammunition that reformers in Unit 2 would use to justify a much more radical break.

1.3 Technology & the Age of Exploration

  • Portuguese and Spanish voyages (Columbus, da Gama) opened direct sea routes to Asia and to the Americas, beginning sustained European overseas expansion.
  • New technologies made these voyages possible: the caravel (a smaller, more maneuverable ship capable of sailing against the wind), the magnetic compass, the astrolabe, and improved cartography all allowed sailors to venture out of sight of land for long periods.
  • Motivations for exploration combined the desire for direct access to Asian spice markets (bypassing Ottoman-controlled overland routes), religious zeal to spread Christianity, and competition for prestige and wealth among Atlantic-facing states — above all Portugal and Spain.
  • Portugal pioneered exploration along the African coast and into the Indian Ocean, while Spain, after Columbus's 1492 voyage, focused its ambitions on the Americas — a division formalized by the Treaty of Tordesillas (1494).

1.4 The Atlantic World & the Columbian Exchange

  • The Columbian Exchange transferred crops, animals, diseases, and people between the Old and New Worlds, with devastating demographic effects on Native American populations, who lacked immunity to Old World diseases like smallpox.
  • Mercantilism — the theory that a nation's wealth depended on accumulating bullion and maintaining favorable trade balances — shaped colonial policy for the major European powers.
  • A "Commercial Revolution" — growth of joint-stock companies, banking, and new trade routes — shifted Europe's economic center of gravity from the Mediterranean toward the Atlantic seaboard.
  • New World silver, especially from Spanish America, flowed into Europe in huge quantities, fueling both the rise of Atlantic-facing economies and the price inflation that strained traditional economies and governments alike.

1.5 State-Building in Renaissance Europe

  • "New Monarchs" in Spain, France, and England (late 1400s–1500s) consolidated royal power — reducing the independence of nobles, building standing armies, and centralizing tax collection.
  • Spain under Charles V and Philip II became Europe's dominant power in the 1500s, funded partly by American silver, though constant warfare eventually strained Spanish finances.
  • France's monarchy steadily expanded its authority over the nobility and the church in this period, building administrative foundations that would support the much stronger royal power explored in Unit 3.
  • England developed differently — Tudor monarchs strengthened the state, but Parliament retained real influence over taxation and law, foreshadowing the constitutional conflicts covered in Unit 3.

1.6 Comparison: Italian vs. Northern Renaissance

The Renaissance looked different depending on where it took place — a contrast that helps explain why the Northern Renaissance fed so directly into the Reformation.

Italian Renaissance Northern Renaissance
Geographic Center Florence, Venice, Rome Low Countries, Germany, England
Primary Focus Classical antiquity, secular themes, art & architecture Religious and moral reform, textual criticism of scripture
Key Figures Petrarch, da Vinci, Michelangelo, Raphael Erasmus, Dürer
Relationship to the Church Largely coexisted with, and was patronized by, the Church Critiqued clerical abuses and helped inspire the Reformation

Exam Tip

When asked to explain the origins of the Reformation, draw a direct line from Northern Renaissance Christian humanism (Erasmus's biblical scholarship and critiques of clerical corruption) to Luther's 1517 challenge in Unit 2 — the Reformation didn't emerge from nowhere.

Key Vocabulary

Humanism
Renaissance intellectual movement emphasizing classical learning and human potential.
Printing Press
Gutenberg's invention that accelerated the spread of ideas across Europe.
Christian Humanism
Erasmus's application of humanist textual criticism to scripture and Church practice.
Caravel
A small, maneuverable ship that made long Atlantic voyages possible.
Columbian Exchange
Transfer of crops, animals, diseases, and people between the Old and New Worlds.
Mercantilism
Economic theory linking national wealth to bullion accumulation and favorable trade balances.
Commercial Revolution
Growth of banking, joint-stock companies, and trade that shifted Europe's economy toward the Atlantic.
Joint-Stock Company
A business owned by shareholders, used to fund risky overseas trading ventures.
New Monarchs
Late 15th–16th century rulers who consolidated royal power over nobles and territory.
