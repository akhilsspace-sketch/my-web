Unit 1 Notes

Foundations of American Government

Constitutional Foundations ~18% of exam 6 topics + comparison

Big Picture

Unit 1 lays the philosophical and structural groundwork for the entire course. American government rests on Enlightenment ideas about natural rights and the social contract, expressed through a Constitution that divides power both horizontally (separation of powers, checks and balances) and vertically (federalism). The debates over how much power to give the national government — debates that began before the ink on the Constitution was dry — still drive political arguments today.

1.1 Natural Rights, the Social Contract & the Declaration of Independence

  • Enlightenment philosopher John Locke argued that individuals possess natural rights — to life, liberty, and property — that exist independent of government, and that government's purpose is to protect those rights.
  • Under social contract theory, government derives its legitimacy from the consent of the governed (popular sovereignty); if government fails to protect natural rights, the people retain a right to alter or abolish it.
  • The Declaration of Independence (1776) applies these Lockean principles directly — listing grievances against King George III and asserting the colonies' right to break from a government that had violated their natural rights.
  • These ideas — natural rights, limited government, and consent of the governed — are not just historical artifacts; they underpin arguments made throughout the course about the proper scope of government power.

1.2 The Articles of Confederation & the Constitutional Convention

  • The Articles of Confederation (1781) created a deliberately weak national government — no power to tax, no national executive or judiciary, one vote per state, and amendments requiring unanimous consent.
  • Shays' Rebellion (1786–87), an armed uprising by indebted Massachusetts farmers, exposed the national government's inability to respond to internal unrest and built momentum for reform.
  • The Constitutional Convention (1787) resolved deep disagreements through compromise: the Great Compromise created a bicameral Congress (a House apportioned by population, a Senate with equal state representation); the Three-Fifths Compromise counted enslaved people for representation and taxation; and a separate compromise created the Electoral College to choose the president.
  • These compromises reveal the founding's central tensions — between large and small states, and a troubling accommodation of slavery that would have lasting consequences explored in later units.

1.3 Separation of Powers & Checks and Balances

  • The Constitution divides governmental power among three branches — legislative (Article I), executive (Article II), and judicial (Article III) — each with distinct constitutional responsibilities.
  • Checks and balances give each branch tools to limit the others: the presidential veto (checked by a congressional override), Senate confirmation of appointments and treaties, judicial review, and impeachment.
  • Federalist No. 10 and Federalist No. 51 (Madison) argue that "ambition must be made to counteract ambition" — structuring government so each branch's self-interest in preserving its own power serves as a check on the others, reducing reliance on virtue alone.
  • This system was designed to prevent tyranny, but it also produces built-in friction — sometimes called "gridlock" — a recurring theme in Unit 2's exploration of how the three branches interact.

1.4 Federalism: Dividing Power Between National & State Governments

  • Federalism divides power between national and state governments through enumerated powers (explicitly granted to Congress in Article I, Section 8), reserved powers (retained by states under the 10th Amendment), and concurrent powers (shared by both, such as taxation).
  • The Necessary and Proper Clause ("elastic clause") allows Congress to exercise implied powers beyond those explicitly listed — a major source of expanding federal authority over time.
  • McCulloch v. Maryland (1819) upheld Congress's implied power to create a national bank and established that states cannot tax federal institutions, affirming federal supremacy.
  • Federalism's balance has shifted from "dual federalism" (distinct spheres for national and state governments) toward "cooperative federalism" (overlapping responsibilities, federal grants with conditions attached) — though United States v. Lopez (1995) showed the Court reasserting limits on federal power under the Commerce Clause.
  • Categorical grants (specific purposes, more federal control) versus block grants (broader purposes, more state discretion), along with unfunded mandates, remain central to ongoing debates over the proper balance of federalism.

1.5 The Federalist/Anti-Federalist Debate & the Bill of Rights

  • During ratification, Federalists (supporting the Constitution) argued for a stronger national government, while Anti-Federalists feared it would threaten individual liberty and state sovereignty.
  • Federalist No. 10 (Madison) argues that a large republic, encompassing many diverse interests ("factions"), would prevent any single faction from dominating — making a large, diverse republic safer for liberty than a small one.
  • Brutus 1, an influential Anti-Federalist essay, argues the opposite: a republic spanning such a vast, diverse territory could not adequately represent its citizens and would tend toward centralized, oppressive power.
  • The Bill of Rights (ratified 1791) was added as a concession to Anti-Federalist concerns, explicitly enumerating individual liberties protected from federal government infringement.

1.6 Amending & Interpreting the Constitution

  • The formal amendment process (Article V) requires proposal by two-thirds of both houses of Congress (or a national convention) and ratification by three-fourths of the states — a deliberately difficult process that has produced only 27 amendments in over two centuries.
  • Informal methods of constitutional change are far more common — judicial interpretation (how courts read phrases like "due process" or the Commerce Clause), executive actions that establish precedent, congressional legislation, and evolving political practices (such as the rise of political parties and primaries, neither mentioned in the original Constitution).
  • This tension — between the Constitution's formal rigidity and its informal adaptability — is a recurring theme: the document's brevity and ambiguity on many points leaves room for ongoing interpretation and political conflict, explored throughout the rest of the course.

1.7 Comparison: Federalist No. 10 vs. Brutus 1

These two required foundational documents represent the core disagreement at the heart of ratification — and one of the most frequently tested comparisons on the AP exam.

Federalist No. 10 (Madison) Brutus 1 (Anti-Federalist)
View of a Large Republic Safer — diversity of interests prevents any one faction from dominating Dangerous — too large and diverse to be governed by laws reflecting the people's will
View of Representation Representatives filter and refine public opinion Representatives in a large republic can't truly know constituents' interests
Primary Concern Majority tyranny / dominance by a single faction Loss of liberty and state sovereignty to a distant central government

Exam Tip

This comparison appears constantly in free-response questions. The core disagreement is about scale: does a large, diverse republic protect liberty (Madison) or threaten it (Brutus)? Connect this debate to the federalism and checks-and-balances structures that ultimately emerged as compromises between these two positions.

Key Foundational Clauses & Cases

Declaration of Independence (1776)
Established natural rights philosophy and the right of revolution as the basis for the new nation.
Articles of Confederation (1781)
First U.S. government framework; its weaknesses led directly to the Constitutional Convention.
The Great Compromise (1787)
Created a bicameral Congress balancing large- and small-state interests.
Necessary & Proper Clause (Art. I, Sec. 8)
The "elastic clause" allowing Congress to exercise implied powers.
10th Amendment (1791)
Reserves powers not delegated to the federal government to the states.
McCulloch v. Maryland (1819)
Established federal supremacy and a broad reading of implied powers.
United States v. Lopez (1995)
Reasserted limits on federal power under the Commerce Clause.

Key Vocabulary

Natural Rights
Rights inherent to all people (life, liberty, property) that government exists to protect.
Social Contract
Theory that government's legitimacy comes from the consent of the governed.
Articles of Confederation
Weak first national government, replaced by the Constitution in 1789.
Great Compromise
Created a bicameral Congress with a House by population and a Senate by state.
Federalism
Division of power between national and state governments.
Necessary and Proper Clause
Constitutional basis for Congress's implied powers, also called the "elastic clause."
Checks and Balances
System giving each branch tools to limit the powers of the others.
Enumerated Powers
Powers explicitly granted to Congress in Article I, Section 8.
Devolution
The transfer of power from the federal government back to the states.
