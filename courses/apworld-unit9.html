Unit 9 Notes

Globalization

c. 1900–present ~4–6% of exam 6 topics + comparison

Big Picture

The final unit asks whether the late 20th/21st century is something genuinely new, or just the latest phase of patterns from earlier units — global trade networks (Unit 2), unequal economic integration (Unit 4/6), and rapid technological/cultural exchange — now operating at digital speed and planetary scale.

9.1 Technology & the Digital Revolution

  • The rise of computers, the internet, and mobile communication dramatically increased the speed and scale of global information exchange — compressing communication that once took weeks into seconds.
  • Advances in transportation — commercial jet travel and standardized container shipping — sharply reduced the cost and time of moving goods and people globally, deepening economic integration.
  • This technology cuts both ways politically: it has been a powerful tool for organizing social and political movements (including via social media), but also for state surveillance and information control.

9.2 Globalization of Trade & Economics

  • The IMF (International Monetary Fund) and World Bank, established after WWII at the Bretton Woods Conference (1944), provide loans and financial assistance to countries — often attaching conditions (structural adjustment: privatization, reduced government spending) that critics argue can worsen conditions for the poor in recipient countries.
  • The World Trade Organization (WTO, est. 1995) works to reduce tariffs and other trade barriers between member nations.
  • Regional economic blocs deepened integration further — most notably the European Union, which combined a common market with (for many members) a shared currency, the euro.
  • Multinational corporations increasingly locate manufacturing wherever labor is cheapest ("outsourcing") — supporters argue this brings jobs and development to poorer regions, while critics point to exploitative labor conditions.

9.3 The Green Revolution & Industrialization in the Developing World

  • The Green Revolution (from the mid-20th century) introduced high-yield crop varieties, synthetic fertilizers, and improved irrigation, dramatically increasing agricultural output in countries like India and reducing famine risk — though it also raised concerns about environmental costs (water use, chemical runoff) and unequal benefits favoring larger landowners.
  • Industrialization continued shifting toward new regions: the East Asian "Tiger" economies (e.g., South Korea, Taiwan) and later China pursued export-oriented industrialization, becoming major global manufacturing centers.
  • This extends the pattern from Unit 6 — industrial production continues moving toward regions with lower labor costs, increasingly organized into global supply chains.

9.4 Human Rights & Liberalization Movements

  • The Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948) established global human rights norms, though enforcement has remained limited and inconsistent across countries.
  • Movements for civil rights, women's rights, and LGBTQ+ rights gained international traction in the latter 20th century — though progress varied enormously by region and frequently faced significant resistance.
  • The movement against apartheid in South Africa (ending in 1994) is a key example of international human rights activism combining with internal resistance to produce major political change.

9.5 Globalized Culture & Backlash

  • Global media and entertainment industries have spread cultural products — music, film, fashion — worldwide, leading some observers to describe a trend toward cultural homogenization (often associated with the dominance of U.S./Western media).
  • At the same time, local cultures persist and adapt global influences to their own contexts — a process sometimes called "glocalization."
  • Globalization has also provoked backlash: movements emphasizing religious or national identity have grown in response to perceived cultural homogenization and the economic disruptions of globalization.

9.6 Demographic & Environmental Change

  • Global population grew dramatically through the 20th century, though growth has slowed sharply in many developed countries (leading to aging populations) while remaining higher in parts of the developing world.
  • Urbanization has continued at a rapid pace — more than half the world's population now lives in cities, creating both opportunities and challenges around housing, infrastructure, and inequality.
  • Climate change, driven substantially by fossil fuel use since industrialization began (Unit 5/6), has become a major focus of international cooperation (e.g., the Paris Agreement), though implementation remains politically contested.
  • Global pandemics (HIV/AIDS, COVID-19) show how today's interconnectedness can spread disease worldwide at a pace far beyond the Black Death (Unit 2) — the same networks that move goods and people also move pathogens.

9.7 Comparison: Responses to Globalization

Not every region or group has responded to globalization the same way — compare strategies of integration versus resistance.

Region / Group Response to Globalization Example
East Asia Embraced export-oriented industrialization, integrated into global supply chains "Asian Tiger" economies; China as a global manufacturing hub
European Union Deepened regional economic & political integration Common market and shared currency (euro) among many members
Developing nations under IMF/World Bank programs Opened markets per loan conditions, often with social costs Privatization and reduced social spending under structural adjustment
Religious & nationalist movements Resistance to perceived cultural homogenization Growth of religious fundamentalist and nationalist political movements worldwide

Exam Tip

A favorite essay format for this unit is continuity and change over time (CCOT) — comparing globalization to earlier eras of connectivity (Units 2 and 4). Continuities: technology-driven exchange, economic interdependence, and unequal distribution of benefits. Changes: the speed/scale enabled by digital technology and the role of formal international institutions (IMF, WTO, UN) that didn't exist in earlier periods.

Unit Timeline

Key Vocabulary

Bretton Woods System
Post-WWII framework establishing the IMF and World Bank to manage international finance.
IMF / World Bank
International institutions providing loans and financial assistance, often with policy conditions attached.
World Trade Organization
International body that regulates trade and reduces barriers between member nations.
Multinational Corporation
A company that operates production and sales across multiple countries.
Green Revolution
Mid-20th century introduction of high-yield crops, fertilizers, and irrigation that boosted agricultural output.
Structural Adjustment
Economic reforms (privatization, spending cuts) required as conditions for IMF/World Bank loans.
Glocalization
The adaptation of global products or ideas to fit local cultural contexts.
Apartheid
South Africa's system of legalized racial segregation, ended in 1994.
