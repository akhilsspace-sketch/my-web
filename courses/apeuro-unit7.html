Unit 7 Notes

19th-Century Perspectives & Political Developments

c. 1815–1914 ~14% of exam 5 topics + comparison

Big Picture

Unit 7 traces the political consequences of the ideological forces unleashed by the French Revolution (Unit 5) and reshaped by industrialization (Unit 6). The conservative order established at Vienna faced repeated challenges from liberalism and nationalism, which eventually reshaped the map of Europe through Italian and German unification. By century's end, that new map — combined with imperial competition and rigid alliance systems — set the stage for the catastrophe of Unit 8.

7.1 The Concert of Europe & Conservative Order

  • The Concert of Europe (Austria, Russia, Prussia, Britain, later France), established at the Congress of Vienna (Unit 5) and guided by Metternich's conservatism, sought to preserve monarchy, traditional social hierarchy, and the post-Vienna territorial settlement against revolution.
  • Liberalism — emphasizing individual rights, constitutional government, free markets, and (often) a limited franchise based on property — challenged the conservative order, especially among the rising middle class created by industrialization (Unit 6).
  • Nationalism — the idea that people sharing language, culture, and history should form their own sovereign state — was a revolutionary force in a Europe still dominated by multinational empires (Austria, Russia, the Ottomans).
  • These three forces — conservatism, liberalism, and nationalism — would collide repeatedly throughout the 19th century, beginning with the revolutionary waves of Topic 7.2.

7.2 The Revolutions of 1830 and 1848

  • The 1830 revolutions — especially in France, where Charles X was overthrown in favor of the more liberal Louis-Philippe — reflected liberal, middle-class discontent with restored conservative monarchies.
  • The Revolutions of 1848 swept across much of Europe (France, the German states, the Austrian Empire, Italian states), combining liberal constitutional demands, nationalist aspirations, and the working-class economic grievances described in Unit 6.
  • Despite initial successes, most 1848 revolutions were suppressed or rolled back by 1849–1850 — but they demonstrated the strength of liberal and nationalist sentiment across the continent.
  • The failures of 1848 taught a generation of nationalist leaders — most notably Cavour and Bismarck — that idealistic popular uprisings alone were insufficient; unification, as Topic 7.3 shows, would instead come through calculated diplomacy and war.

7.3 Unification of Italy and Germany

  • Italian unification (achieved 1861–1870) combined the diplomacy and military alliances of Piedmont-Sardinia under Cavour, Garibaldi's popular nationalist movement (the "Red Shirts"), and the exploitation of great-power conflicts.
  • German unification (achieved 1871) was driven by Prussia under Otto von Bismarck's "Realpolitik" — using strategic wars against Denmark, Austria, and France to unify the German states under Prussian leadership while excluding Austria.
  • Both unifications created powerful new nation-states that disrupted the balance of power established at Vienna (Unit 5), contributing to the rising tensions that would culminate in World War I (Unit 8).
  • Nationalism within multinational empires (Austria-Hungary, the Ottoman Empire, Russia) produced the opposite effect — centrifugal pressure from subject nationalities, especially in the Balkans, that destabilized these empires and would help ignite the crisis of Topic 7.5.

7.4 New Imperialism & the Scramble for Africa

  • "New Imperialism" (roughly 1870s–1914) differed from earlier colonialism in scale and motivation — driven by industrial economies' need for raw materials and markets (Unit 6), nationalist prestige competition among the newly powerful states of Topic 7.3, and pseudo-scientific racial ideologies (Social Darwinism) that purported to justify domination.
  • The "Scramble for Africa," formalized at the Berlin Conference (1884–85), saw European powers divide nearly the entire continent among themselves with little regard for existing political or ethnic boundaries.
  • Imperial competition also extended to Asia — the "Great Game" in Central Asia and competing spheres of influence in China — further fueling tensions among European powers.
  • Imperialism's domestic effects included debates over its morality (anti-imperialist movements existed but were a minority view) and its role in fueling the nationalist pride that helped legitimize the political systems of the great powers — pride that would feed directly into the brinkmanship of Topic 7.5.

7.5 Alliance Systems & the Road to War

  • A complex alliance system — the Triple Alliance (Germany, Austria-Hungary, Italy) and the Triple Entente (France, Russia, Britain) — emerged from decades of shifting diplomacy after German unification (Topic 7.3) disrupted the old balance of power.
  • A series of crises (the Moroccan Crises, the Balkan Wars of 1912–13) heightened tensions without triggering general war, but normalized the expectation of conflict among the great powers.
  • Militarism — arms races (especially the Anglo-German naval race) and detailed mobilization plans such as Germany's Schlieffen Plan — meant that any crisis could rapidly escalate into continent-wide war.
  • By 1914, nationalism (Topics 7.1, 7.3), imperial rivalry (Topic 7.4), alliance commitments, and militarism had created conditions in which a single incident — the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand — could trigger a general European war, the subject of Unit 8.

7.6 Comparison: Unification of Italy vs. Germany

Both Italy and Germany unified within a single decade of each other — through strikingly similar methods, despite very different starting points.

Italian Unification German Unification
Leading State / Figure Piedmont-Sardinia / Cavour Prussia / Bismarck
Primary Method Diplomacy, alliances, plus popular nationalist uprisings (Garibaldi) "Realpolitik" — calculated wars against Denmark, Austria, France
Role of War Wars with Austria and France weakened opposition to unification Three short wars directly built the new German Empire
Resulting Government Constitutional monarchy under the House of Savoy Federal empire dominated by Prussia, with a limited parliament

Exam Tip

This is a classic "evaluate the extent" prompt. Strong answers note that both unifications were driven more by the strategic interests and calculation of a single dominant state (Piedmont, Prussia) than by a spontaneous popular nationalist movement alone — though popular nationalism provided important support and legitimacy.

Key Vocabulary

Concert of Europe
System of great-power cooperation aimed at preserving the post-1815 conservative order.
Liberalism
19th-century ideology emphasizing individual rights, constitutional government, and free markets.
Nationalism
Belief that people sharing a common identity should form their own sovereign state.
Realpolitik
Politics based on practical power and strategic calculation rather than ideals.
New Imperialism
Late 19th-century wave of European colonial expansion driven by industrial and nationalist motives.
Social Darwinism
Pseudo-scientific ideology applying "survival of the fittest" to justify imperial domination.
Berlin Conference
1884–85 meeting where European powers divided Africa among themselves.
Triple Entente
Alliance of France, Russia, and Britain that opposed the Triple Alliance before WWI.
Militarism
Policy of building up military strength and readiness for war, including arms races and mobilization plans.
