Unit 2 Notes

Interactions Among Branches of Government

Articles I, II & III ~30% of exam 9 topics + comparison

Big Picture

Unit 2 is the largest unit on the exam, and for good reason: it covers how Congress, the presidency, and the judiciary are each structured, how they exercise their powers, and — most importantly — how they check and constrain one another in practice. The separation-of-powers system from Unit 1 isn't just a diagram of three boxes; it's a constant, dynamic competition for influence. Expect exam questions that ask you to trace an action by one branch and identify how another branch can respond.

2.1 Congress: Structure & Constitutional Powers

  • Article I establishes a bicameral legislature and grants Congress its enumerated powers (Article I, Section 8) — including the power to tax, borrow and coin money, regulate interstate commerce, raise armies, and declare war.
  • The House of Representatives has 435 members serving 2-year terms, apportioned among states by population — designed to be closely tied to current public opinion.
  • The Senate has 100 members (two per state) serving staggered 6-year terms — originally chosen by state legislatures, until the 17th Amendment (1913) established direct election by voters.
  • Apportionment of House seats is recalculated after each census; the resulting redistricting can produce gerrymandering. Baker v. Carr (1962) established the "one person, one vote" principle, while Shaw v. Reno (1993) ruled that race cannot be the predominant factor in drawing district lines.

2.2 The Legislative Process & Congressional Behavior

  • A bill is introduced (revenue bills must originate in the House) and referred to committee, where most bills die. Floor procedure differs sharply: the House's Rules Committee limits debate, while the Senate allows unlimited debate unless 60 senators invoke cloture to end a filibuster. A conference committee resolves differences before the bill goes to the president to sign, veto, or let become law via pocket veto.
  • The Speaker of the House is the most powerful leadership position in Congress, controlling the floor agenda and committee assignments. The Senate Majority Leader schedules floor votes but holds comparatively less formal power, since norms like the filibuster give individual senators significant leverage.
  • Under the trustee model, representatives use independent judgment; under the delegate model, they vote as constituents direct. Most members blend the two — sometimes called the "politico" model.
  • Polarization — the growing ideological distance between the parties — has contributed to legislative gridlock and the rise of "safe" districts, where primary challenges from more ideologically extreme candidates pose a bigger electoral threat than general-election opponents.

2.3 Congressional Oversight of Other Branches

  • Oversight is Congress's power to monitor and investigate how the executive branch implements laws — through committee hearings, investigations, and reports from agencies like the Government Accountability Office.
  • Congress's "power of the purse" — control over federal appropriations — is one of its most powerful checks, since no money can be spent without congressional authorization.
  • The Senate alone confirms major presidential appointments (Cabinet officials, federal judges) by majority vote and ratifies treaties by a two-thirds vote.
  • Impeachment divides responsibility between the chambers: the House impeaches (brings charges) by majority vote, while the Senate conducts the trial and can convict and remove an official only with a two-thirds vote.

2.4 The Presidency: Constitutional & Expanding Powers

  • Article II vests "the executive power" in the president using far less specific language than Article I's detailed list of congressional powers — leaving substantial room for interpretation and growth.
  • The president's formal powers include serving as commander-in-chief, negotiating treaties (Senate ratification), appointing officials and judges (Senate confirmation), vetoing legislation, and granting pardons. Federalist No. 70 (Hamilton) argues that a single, "energetic" executive provides accountability and decisive action.
  • Informal powers grew through practice rather than constitutional text: executive orders, executive agreements (bypassing Senate ratification), signing statements, and "going public" (using mass communication to pressure Congress).
  • Executive power has expanded substantially since the 20th century, especially in foreign policy and national security — a trend sometimes called the "imperial presidency," and an ongoing source of debate about the constitutional balance from Unit 1.

2.5 The Executive Branch: Cabinet, Bureaucracy & Foreign Policy

  • The Cabinet consists of the heads of the 15 executive departments, appointed with Senate confirmation; the Executive Office of the President (EOP) — including White House staff, the Office of Management and Budget, and the National Security Council — is more directly responsive to the president than the Cabinet.
  • The federal bureaucracy implements and enforces laws through rulemaking, giving agencies significant practical discretion. "Iron triangles" — stable relationships among agencies, congressional committees, and interest groups — can come to dominate policymaking in a given area.
  • The president is the primary architect of foreign policy — commander-in-chief, treaty negotiator, appointer of ambassadors. Congress retains the power to declare war and controls military funding, but presidents have often committed troops without a formal declaration, prompting the War Powers Resolution.
  • Presidents generally have more latitude in foreign policy than domestic policy — the "two presidencies" thesis — because Congress tends to defer to the executive on foreign affairs, especially during crises. Policy generally moves through five stages — agenda setting, formulation, adoption, implementation (often by the bureaucracy), and evaluation — a cycle revisited throughout this unit.

2.6 Checks on Presidential Power

  • Congress checks the president through its power of the purse, Senate confirmation of appointments and treaties, the ability to override a veto with a two-thirds vote, oversight and investigations, and impeachment.
  • The War Powers Resolution (1973) requires the president to notify Congress of military deployments and limits deployments without congressional authorization to 60 days — though its effectiveness as a real constraint has often been debated.
  • The judiciary can check executive power through judicial review, striking down executive actions as unconstitutional, and by limiting executive privilege — the president's claimed right to withhold information from Congress or the courts, which the Court has held is not absolute.
  • These checks show that despite the practical growth of presidential power, the separation-of-powers system from Unit 1 continues to impose real constraints on the executive.

2.7 The Judiciary: Article III, Judicial Review & Marbury v. Madison

  • Article III establishes the Supreme Court and gives Congress the power to create the lower federal courts — resulting in a three-tier structure: district courts (trial courts), courts of appeals (circuit courts), and the Supreme Court (final appellate review, mostly discretionary).
  • Federal judges receive lifetime appointments ("during good Behaviour"), nominated by the president and confirmed by the Senate — insulating the judiciary from short-term political pressure so judges can decide cases based on law rather than immediate public opinion.
  • Judicial review — the power of courts to declare laws or executive actions unconstitutional — is not explicitly stated in the Constitution. Marbury v. Madison (1803) established it: Chief Justice Marshall's opinion declared "it is emphatically the province and duty of the judicial department to say what the law is."
  • Federalist No. 78 (Hamilton) had anticipated this role, arguing that lifetime tenure would secure judicial independence and that the judiciary — the "least dangerous branch," with no army or treasury of its own — could check legislative and executive overreach precisely because its only power was judgment.

2.8 How Cases Reach the Court & Judicial Philosophy

  • Most cases reach the Supreme Court through a writ of certiorari — a discretionary request that the Court review a lower court's decision, granted only under the "rule of four" (at least four of nine justices must agree). The Court is more likely to grant cert when lower courts disagree ("circuit splits") or a case raises a significant constitutional question.
  • After cert is granted, both sides submit briefs, the Court hears oral arguments, and it issues a written opinion — often with concurring opinions (agreeing with the outcome for different reasons) and dissenting opinions.
  • Precedent (or stare decisis) provides legal stability, but the Court can overturn it — as it did when Dobbs v. Jackson (2022) overturned Roe v. Wade (1973).
  • Judicial restraint favors deference to elected branches and precedent, while judicial activism describes a greater willingness to overturn laws or precedent. Originalism interprets the Constitution by its meaning at ratification, while the living Constitution approach applies its principles to evolving circumstances — related but distinct axes from restraint vs. activism.

2.9 Checks on Judicial Power & the Federal Budget Process

  • The judiciary has no enforcement power of its own — it depends on the executive branch and public compliance to give its rulings effect, famously summarized as having "no army and no purse."
  • Congress can check the courts by changing the size or jurisdiction of lower federal courts, proposing constitutional amendments to override a ruling, or impeaching judges for misconduct. The president shapes the judiciary through nominations.
  • Ultimately, the Court's power rests heavily on its perceived legitimacy: rulings that diverge sharply from public opinion can provoke significant political backlash, even if they cannot be directly overturned.
  • The federal budget process is itself a cross-branch check: the president proposes a budget (developed by the OMB), and Congress passes a budget resolution and appropriations bills. Mandatory spending (entitlements, interest on the debt) isn't subject to annual appropriations, while discretionary spending (defense, education) is — giving Congress's "power of the purse" its sharpest edge over the other two branches.

2.10 Comparison: How Each Branch Checks the Others

Every power one branch holds is matched by a check some other branch holds over it — the engine of "ambition counteracting ambition" from Federalist No. 51.

Exam Tip

When a prompt describes an action by one branch, immediately ask: which other branch(es) can respond, and how? Framing your answer around this checks-and-balances matrix is the fastest way to earn points on Unit 2 free-response questions.

Landmark Cases & Precedents: Powers in Action

Federalist No. 70 (1788)
Hamilton's argument for a single, energetic executive as essential to good government.
Federalist No. 78 (1788)
Hamilton's case for an independent judiciary secured by lifetime tenure.
Marbury v. Madison (1803)
Established the principle of judicial review itself.
Baker v. Carr (1962)
Expanded the Court's willingness to rule on "political questions" like redistricting.
War Powers Resolution (1973)
Congress's attempt to limit the president's ability to deploy troops without authorization.
Executive Privilege
The president's claimed right to withhold information from Congress or the courts — not absolute.

Key Vocabulary

Bicameral
A legislature divided into two chambers, as Congress is into the House and Senate.
Gerrymandering
Drawing district lines to benefit a particular party or incumbent.
Filibuster
A Senate tactic of extended debate used to delay or block a vote on a bill.
Executive Order
A presidential directive with the force of law that does not require congressional approval.
Going Public
A strategy of appealing directly to citizens to pressure Congress to act.
Bureaucracy
The agencies and departments that implement and enforce federal laws and policies.
Judicial Review
The power of courts to declare laws or executive actions unconstitutional.
Writ of Certiorari
An order by which the Supreme Court agrees to review a lower court's decision.
Mandatory / Discretionary Spending
Spending required by existing law vs. spending set through annual appropriations.
