Unit 2 Notes

Networks of Exchange

c. 1200–1450 CE ~8–10% of exam 6 topics + comparison

Big Picture

Unit 2 zooms out from individual states to the networks that connected them. Three major trade systems — the Silk Roads, the Indian Ocean network, and the trans-Saharan routes — moved goods, religions, technologies, and even diseases across vast distances. The Mongols are the thread tying it all together: their conquests reopened and protected the Silk Roads, accelerating exchange across all of Eurasia.

2.1 The Silk Roads

  • The Silk Roads were a network of overland trade routes linking East Asia to the Mediterranean, passing through Central Asia, Persia, and the Middle East.
  • Trade was indirect — goods passed through many regional middlemen rather than a single merchant traveling the whole route. Because transport was slow and expensive, goods tended to be low-bulk, high-value luxury items: silk, porcelain, and paper moved west; horses, wool textiles, and precious metals moved east.
  • Caravanserai — fortified roadside inns spaced along the routes — provided merchants with food, water, lodging, and security, making long-distance overland travel feasible.
  • The routes were also a conduit for religion: Buddhist monks and merchants carried Buddhism from India into Central Asia and China along these same roads.
  • Although the Silk Roads existed for centuries before this period, they saw a major revival in the 1200s–1300s once the Mongols brought the entire route under unified control (see 2.2).

2.2 The Mongol Empire & the Making of the Modern World

  • Genghis Khan united the nomadic Mongol and Turkic tribes of the steppe (c. 1206) by organizing the army into units of ten based on loyalty to the khan rather than to clan — breaking traditional tribal divisions.
  • Military advantages: highly mobile horseback cavalry, skilled mounted archers using composite bows, siege techniques adopted from conquered peoples, and a reputation for brutality toward cities that resisted — which often convinced others to surrender without a fight.
  • After Genghis Khan's death, the empire split into four khanates: the Yuan Dynasty (China), the Golden Horde (Russia/Eastern Europe), the Chagatai Khanate (Central Asia), and the Ilkhanate (Persia/Middle East).
  • The Pax Mongolica ("Mongol Peace") describes the relative stability across Eurasia under Mongol control — with bandits suppressed and routes unified under one authority, trade along the Silk Roads boomed.
  • The Yam was a relay system of horse-mounted messengers and supply stations that allowed information and goods to move quickly across the empire's huge distances.
  • Mongol rulers were generally religiously tolerant and pragmatic — they employed administrators of many faiths and ethnicities (Persians, Chinese, and even some Europeans) based on usefulness rather than background.
  • The same connectivity that spread goods and ideas also spread disease: Mongol trade routes carried the bubonic plague westward in the 1300s (see 2.6).

2.3 Exchange in the Indian Ocean

  • The Indian Ocean trade network was the largest and most lucrative of the period, linking East Africa, Arabia, Persia, India, Southeast Asia, and China.
  • Its predictability came from monsoon winds — seasonal wind patterns that reverse direction with the seasons, letting sailors plan voyages out and back within a single year.
  • Shipbuilding innovations made long sea voyages possible: dhows (Arab/Indian vessels with triangular sails good for sailing against the wind) and Chinese junks (large ships, some with watertight compartments that limited flooding damage).
  • Navigational tools — the magnetic compass and the astrolabe (for measuring star positions) — let sailors travel further from sight of land.
  • Major port cities grew wealthy as exchange hubs: Calicut (India), Malacca (Malay Peninsula, controlling the strait between the Indian Ocean and South China Sea), Hormuz (Persian Gulf), and the Swahili Coast city-states of East Africa.
  • Goods traded: spices (pepper, cloves, nutmeg) from Southeast Asia, cotton textiles from India, porcelain from China, and gold and ivory from East Africa.
  • Religious diffusion followed the trade routes — Islam spread into coastal South and Southeast Asia and East Africa largely through Muslim merchant communities settling in port cities.
  • Zheng He's voyages (1405–1433) — massive Ming Dynasty "treasure fleets" that sailed across the Indian Ocean as far as East Africa, projecting Chinese naval power and collecting tribute from foreign rulers.

2.4 Trans-Saharan Trade Routes

  • Camel caravans crossed the Sahara Desert, connecting the gold-producing kingdoms of West Africa to North Africa and, from there, the Mediterranean world.
  • Use of the camel saddle allowed merchants to carry heavy loads across arid terrain where wheeled vehicles and most pack animals could not survive — making large-scale desert trade possible.
  • The core exchange was the gold-salt trade: West Africa had abundant gold but little salt (essential for diet and food preservation), while North Africa had salt deposits but wanted gold — each side valued what the other had in surplus.
  • Other goods included enslaved people, ivory, kola nuts, copper, and cloth moving in both directions.
  • This trade was the economic foundation of major West African states — first Ghana, then Mali (which controlled the trade during this period), and later Songhai.
  • Trading cities like Timbuktu, Gao, and Djenné grew wealthy and became centers of Islamic scholarship as well as commerce.
  • Islam spread into West Africa primarily through these merchant networks — typically adopted first by ruling and merchant elites (for both spiritual and practical/diplomatic reasons), then gradually more broadly.

2.5 Cultural Consequences of Connectivity

  • As people and goods moved across networks, so did religions — often well beyond their place of origin. Buddhism continued spreading along the Silk Roads into Central and East Asia, while Islam expanded through both trade (West Africa, Indian Ocean ports) and conquest (Delhi Sultanate, covered in Unit 1).
  • Diaspora communities — merchants who settled permanently in foreign trading cities — created lasting multicultural populations. Examples include Arab and Persian Muslim merchant communities in Chinese ports and across Swahili Coast towns.
  • Artistic and architectural styles blended across cultures as they came into contact: West African mosques (e.g., the Great Mosque of Djenné) combined Islamic religious forms with local building materials and techniques, and Indo-Islamic architecture blended Persian and South Asian styles.
  • Technology and knowledge also diffused along these networks — paper-making technology spread from China westward through the Islamic world and eventually to Europe.

2.6 Environmental Consequences of Connectivity

  • Connectivity had unintended consequences alongside its benefits. The most dramatic was the Black Death (bubonic plague), which traveled along Mongol-controlled trade routes from Central Asia into the Middle East and Europe in the mid-1300s.
  • The plague killed an estimated one-third of Europe's population and caused major losses across Asia and the Middle East as well.
  • The resulting labor shortages in Europe shifted the balance of power between peasants and landlords — surviving workers could demand higher wages, accelerating the decline of serfdom and triggering peasant revolts in some regions.
  • The speed and scale of the plague's spread is itself evidence of just how interconnected Eurasia had become — a disease outbreak in one region could reach the opposite end of the continent within a few years.

2.7 Comparison: The Three Major Trade Networks

Expect to compare these three networks directly — especially their transportation technology and the role empires/states played in enabling them.

Network Key Transport / Technology Major Goods Role of States / Empires
Silk Roads Camel caravans, caravanserai Silk, porcelain, paper (east→west); horses, textiles, metals (west→east) Mongol Pax Mongolica unified & protected routes, sparking a revival
Indian Ocean Dhows & junks, monsoon winds, compass, astrolabe Spices, cotton textiles, porcelain, gold, ivory Port city-states (Malacca, Swahili Coast) & Ming treasure fleets (Zheng He)
Trans-Saharan Camel caravans & camel saddle Gold (south→north) for salt (north→south); also slaves, ivory, copper West African empires (Ghana, Mali, Songhai) controlled & taxed the trade

Exam Tip

A frequent LEQ prompt asks you to compare the causes or effects of two of these networks. A strong answer goes beyond "both moved goods" — focus on a specific similarity or difference, such as: both relied on a single empire/group of empires to provide security (Mongols for the Silk Roads, West African empires for trans-Saharan trade), but the Indian Ocean network depended on predictable monsoon winds rather than political unification — meaning many independent port cities, not one empire, controlled it.

Key Vocabulary

Caravanserai
Roadside inns along the Silk Roads offering rest, supplies, and security to merchant caravans.
Pax Mongolica
The relative peace and stability across Mongol-controlled Eurasia that allowed trade to flourish.
Yam System
Mongol relay network of horse stations enabling rapid long-distance communication and travel.
Khanates
The four regional empires (Yuan, Golden Horde, Chagatai, Ilkhanate) that divided Mongol territory after Genghis Khan's death.
Monsoon Winds
Seasonal winds that reverse direction predictably, enabling round-trip sailing across the Indian Ocean.
Dhow
Arab/Indian sailing vessel with triangular sails, well-suited to Indian Ocean trade.
Junk
Large Chinese ship, sometimes built with watertight compartments to limit flood damage.
Astrolabe
Navigational instrument that measures the position of stars, helping sailors determine location at sea.
Camel Saddle
Innovation enabling camels to carry heavy loads across the Sahara, making trans-Saharan trade viable.
Gold-Salt Trade
Core exchange of the trans-Saharan network: West African gold traded for North African salt.
Diaspora Community
A population living permanently away from its ancestral homeland — common among merchants in trade-hub cities.
Black Death
Bubonic plague pandemic that spread along Eurasian trade routes in the 1300s, killing roughly a third of Europe's population.
