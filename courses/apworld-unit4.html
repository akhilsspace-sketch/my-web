Unit 4 Notes

Transoceanic Interconnections

c. 1450–1750 CE ~12–15% of exam 7 topics + comparison

Big Picture

This unit covers the moment the Eastern and Western Hemispheres became permanently connected. European maritime technology opened the Atlantic, triggering the Columbian Exchange, the rise of maritime empires, and new labor systems — most consequentially, the Atlantic slave trade. Nearly everything here connects back to one driving question: who profits, and who pays the cost, of this new global economy?

4.1 Technological Innovations & European Exploration

  • European long-distance ocean voyages were made possible by combining technologies borrowed from elsewhere: the caravel (a ship combining European square sails with the maneuverable triangular lateen sail used in the Mediterranean and Indian Ocean) could sail effectively against the wind.
  • Navigation tools — the magnetic compass and astrolabe — had spread to Europe from Chinese and Islamic sources (Units 1–2) and were now applied to Atlantic exploration.
  • Portugal pioneered exploration down Africa's coast searching for a sea route to Asian markets that would bypass Silk Road and Indian Ocean middlemen — Vasco da Gama reached India by sea in 1498.
  • Spain sponsored Columbus's 1492 westward voyage seeking an alternate route to Asia; instead, it led to sustained European contact with the Americas.
  • Motivations are often summarized as "God, Gold, and Glory": spreading Christianity, acquiring precious metals and trade goods, and winning prestige for crown and individual explorers.

4.2 The Columbian Exchange

  • The Columbian Exchange was the transfer of plants, animals, diseases, and people between the Eastern and Western Hemispheres after 1492 — one of the most significant ecological events in human history.
  • Americas → Afro-Eurasia: potatoes, maize, tomatoes, cacao, and tobacco. The potato and maize in particular supported major population growth in Europe, Africa, and China.
  • Afro-Eurasia → Americas: wheat, sugarcane, and livestock — horses, cattle, and pigs. Horses transformed the lifestyles and military capabilities of some Indigenous peoples, especially on the North American Plains.
  • Disease was the deadliest transfer: Old World diseases like smallpox and measles, to which Indigenous Americans had no prior immunity, caused catastrophic population collapse — in many regions over 80–90% over the following century. This demographic collapse was a critical factor enabling European conquest and colonization.

4.3 Maritime Empires: Spanish & Portuguese

  • Spanish conquistadors toppled two massive empires with small forces: Cortés against the Aztec Empire (1519–1521) and Pizarro against the Inca Empire (1532) — succeeding through a combination of steel weapons and gunpowder, devastating disease outbreaks, and alliances with local rivals of the empire (e.g., the Tlaxcalans joined Cortés against the Aztecs).
  • Spain organized its American territories into viceroyalties, governed by viceroys appointed directly by the crown.
  • The encomienda system granted colonists the right to demand labor and tribute from Indigenous communities in a given area — nominally in exchange for protection and Christian instruction, but frequently abusive in practice.
  • Portugal initially focused on a trading-post empire — fortified coastal trading stations along Africa and Asia rather than large territorial conquest — with the major exception of Brazil, where the Portuguese developed large-scale sugar plantation agriculture dependent on enslaved labor.

4.4 Maritime Empires: Dutch, English & French

  • Northern European powers entered colonial competition later, often through joint-stock companies — private companies (e.g., the Dutch East India Company, the English East India Company) granted trade monopolies and even military/governing authority by their home governments.
  • Joint-stock companies spread investment risk across many shareholders, enabling expensive, large-scale ventures that no single investor could finance alone.
  • European colonial policy was broadly guided by mercantilism — the idea that colonies existed to benefit the mother country by supplying raw materials and serving as a captive market for finished goods, with the goal of a favorable balance of trade.
  • The Dutch seized key Indian Ocean ports from the Portuguese and dominated the spice trade; the English and French focused more on settler colonies in North America and the Caribbean, frequently fighting each other (and the Dutch) over colonial territory.

4.5 Labor Systems in the Americas

  • The encomienda system was gradually supplemented and replaced by the repartimiento (a rotational labor draft imposed on Indigenous communities, conceptually similar to the Inca mit'a but enforced by Spanish colonizers) and later the hacienda system — large landed estates relying on debt peonage to bind workers to the land.
  • Plantation agriculture — first sugar in Brazil and the Caribbean, later cash crops like tobacco and cotton in North America — demanded huge labor forces.
  • Initial attempts to use Indigenous labor and European indentured servants (poorer Europeans who exchanged a fixed term of labor for passage to the colonies) proved insufficient — due to disease-driven population collapse among Indigenous peoples and limited supply of indentured workers — driving an increasing shift toward enslaved African labor.

4.6 The Atlantic Slave Trade

  • The triangular trade linked three regions in a continuous cycle: manufactured goods from Europe to Africa, enslaved people from Africa to the Americas (the Middle Passage), and raw materials/cash crops from the Americas back to Europe.
  • Over the centuries of the trade, an estimated 12+ million enslaved Africans were forcibly transported to the Americas; the Middle Passage's brutal conditions caused enormous mortality during the voyage itself.
  • African states and merchant elites were often directly involved in supplying captives — frequently obtained through warfare or raiding — in exchange for European manufactured goods, including firearms. European demand drove the scale of the trade, but its operation depended on African intermediaries as well.
  • The slave trade reshaped West and Central African societies unevenly: some states (e.g., Dahomey, Asante) grew powerful and wealthy through participation, while other regions targeted for raiding suffered severe demographic and social disruption.

4.7 Changing Social Hierarchies

  • Colonial societies developed new hierarchies based on race, ethnicity, and birthplace. In Spanish America, the casta system ranked people roughly as: peninsulares (born in Spain) at the top, criollos (Spanish descent, born in the Americas), then various mixed-race categories such as mestizo (European/Indigenous) and mulatto (European/African), with Indigenous peoples and enslaved Africans at the bottom.
  • Position in this hierarchy determined access to political office, land ownership, and social status — and became a lasting feature of colonial Latin American society.
  • Religious syncretism emerged as enslaved Africans and Indigenous peoples blended Christian practice with their own traditions — for example, Vodou in Haiti combines West African religious elements with Catholic saints and symbolism.

4.8 Comparison: Maritime Empires

Compare how each European power structured its overseas empire — conquest vs. trade, settlement vs. trading posts, and which labor system each relied on.

Power Primary Model Key Labor System Economic Focus
Spanish Territorial conquest of existing empires → viceroyalties Encomienda → repartimiento → hacienda (debt peonage) Silver mining (e.g., Potosí) and agriculture
Portuguese Trading-post empire (Africa/Asia) + plantation colony (Brazil) Enslaved African labor on sugar plantations Sugar, plus African & Asian trade goods
Dutch Joint-stock company (VOC) trading empire Limited settlement; reliance on local labor & trade networks Indian Ocean spice trade

Exam Tip

When discussing labor systems, be precise about which system, where, and when — "Europeans used slavery" is too vague. Note the progression in Spanish territories (encomienda → repartimiento → hacienda) and the regional variation within English colonies (indentured servitude was more common early and in northern colonies; enslaved labor became dominant in the Caribbean and southern colonies as plantation agriculture expanded).

Key Vocabulary

Caravel
European ship design combining square and lateen sails, enabling sailing against the wind on long ocean voyages.
Columbian Exchange
The transfer of plants, animals, diseases, and people between the Eastern and Western Hemispheres after 1492.
Conquistador
Spanish soldier-explorer who led campaigns of conquest in the Americas.
Encomienda System
Spanish system granting colonists the right to extract labor/tribute from Indigenous communities.
Viceroyalty
A territorial division of the Spanish Empire governed by a viceroy appointed by the crown.
Joint-Stock Company
Privately owned company that pools investor capital and was often granted trade monopolies and governing power by a state.
Mercantilism
Economic policy holding that colonies exist to enrich the mother country via raw materials and captive markets.
Triangular Trade
Atlantic trade cycle linking Europe, Africa, and the Americas in the exchange of goods and enslaved people.
Middle Passage
The brutal transatlantic voyage during which enslaved Africans were transported to the Americas.
Casta System
Spanish colonial racial hierarchy ranking people by ancestry and birthplace.
Indentured Servitude
A fixed term of labor exchanged for passage to a colony, common among poorer European migrants.
