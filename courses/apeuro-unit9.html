Unit 9 Notes

Cold War and Contemporary Europe

c. 1914–Present ~9% of exam 6 topics + comparison

Big Picture

Unit 9 carries Europe from the ashes of Unit 8's world wars through more than seven decades of division, integration, and ongoing transformation. The continent split into two competing blocs under American and Soviet influence, then — after the largely peaceful revolutions of 1989 — reunified around a project of unprecedented economic and political integration. That project now faces its own open questions: how much sovereignty should nation-states pool, how should Europe respond to migration and economic shocks, and what does Russia's renewed aggression mean for the continent's future?

9.1 Origins & Division of Cold War Europe

  • The wartime cooperation between the Western Allies and the USSR (Unit 8) broke down quickly after 1945, as Soviet forces installed communist governments across the Eastern European territories they occupied — prompting Churchill's warning of an "Iron Curtain" descending across the continent.
  • The United States responded with the Truman Doctrine and the Marshall Plan — massive economic aid intended to rebuild Western Europe and make communism less appealing to war-weakened populations.
  • Germany itself became the Cold War's central symbol, divided into a Western Federal Republic and a Soviet-aligned Democratic Republic, with Berlin — divided but located deep inside East Germany — repeatedly becoming a flashpoint, from the Berlin Blockade and Airlift (1948–49) to the construction of the Berlin Wall (1961).
  • Both sides formalized their alliances militarily — NATO (1949) for the Western bloc and the Warsaw Pact (1955) for the Soviet bloc — institutionalizing the division of Europe into the two competing systems explored in Topic 9.2.

9.2 Two Blocs: Western Integration, the Soviet Sphere & Decolonization

  • Fueled by Marshall Plan aid, Western Europe experienced extraordinary economic growth through the 1950s and 1960s, and its governments built expansive "welfare states" — public healthcare, education, and social insurance systems designed to prevent the economic desperation that had fueled extremism in Unit 8.
  • This recovery also launched the project of European integration: the European Coal and Steel Community (1951) and then the European Economic Community (1957, the "Common Market") aimed to bind France's and Germany's economies so closely together that another war between them would become unthinkable.
  • East of the Iron Curtain, Soviet-style command economies and one-party communist rule were maintained largely through the presence of Soviet military power; after Stalin's death (1953), Khrushchev's "de-Stalinization" relaxed the worst terror, but uprisings against Soviet control — the Hungarian Revolution (1956) and the Prague Spring (1968) — were both crushed by Warsaw Pact forces.
  • Across this period, Western Europe's market economies, rising living standards, and growing Common Market stood in increasingly visible contrast to Eastern Europe's slower growth, persistent shortages, and subordination to Moscow through the Warsaw Pact — a divide that would define the continent until the revolutions of Topic 9.4.
  • Meanwhile, the drain of two world wars, the cost of maintaining empires, and the spread of colonial nationalist movements drove rapid decolonization after 1945 — India and Pakistan gained independence in 1947, France lost Indochina (1954) and Algeria (1962) after bitter wars, and most of Africa gained independence by the late 1950s and 1960s, often leaving difficult legacies of artificial borders and economic dependency. For Europe, decolonization meant the end of overseas empire as a basis for great-power status, reinforcing the turn toward the regional integration described above.

9.3 Society, Culture & Dissent in Cold War Europe

  • Postwar prosperity transformed Western Europe into a mass consumer society — widespread car ownership, televisions, and household appliances — alongside the growth of mass media and a distinct youth culture.
  • The late 1960s brought social upheaval: student protests (most famously in Paris in May 1968) challenged traditional authority, while "second-wave" feminism raised new questions about workplace discrimination, reproductive rights, and gender roles.
  • Religious practice declined sharply across much of Western Europe during this period, part of a broader trend toward secularization.
  • Behind the Iron Curtain, despite tight restrictions on expression, an underground "dissident" culture persisted — circulating banned writings (samizdat) by figures such as Solzhenitsyn and Havel — keeping alternative political ideas alive and laying groundwork for the movements that would help bring down communist rule in Topic 9.4.

9.4 Détente, 1989 & the End of the Cold War

  • Détente (late 1960s–1970s) brought a relaxation of superpower tensions, including arms-control agreements (SALT) and the Helsinki Accords (1975) — which, notably, included human-rights provisions that Eastern European dissidents (Topic 9.3) would later invoke.
  • Tensions revived in the early 1980s following the Soviet invasion of Afghanistan (1979) and renewed arms buildups, but from 1985 Mikhail Gorbachev's reforms — glasnost (openness) and perestroika (restructuring) — signaled a dramatically reduced Soviet willingness to use force to maintain control over Eastern Europe.
  • By the late 1980s, decades of economic stagnation in the Soviet bloc, contrasted with continued Western prosperity, had eroded popular faith in communist rule — and with Gorbachev unwilling to intervene, a wave of largely peaceful Revolutions of 1989 swept away communist governments in Poland, Hungary, East Germany, and Czechoslovakia, and (far more violently) Romania.
  • The fall of the Berlin Wall (November 1989) became the era's defining image, leading directly to German reunification in 1990; the Soviet Union itself dissolved in 1991, as constituent republics including Russia, Ukraine, and the Baltic states became independent — ending both the USSR and the Cold War with a speed almost no one had predicted.

9.5 Post-Communist Transitions & Deepening EU Integration

  • The former Soviet bloc faced a difficult transition from command to market economies (often called "shock therapy") and from one-party rule to multiparty democracy, with mixed results — Russia under Yeltsin in the 1990s endured severe economic dislocation and the rise of powerful "oligarchs," while under Putin (from 2000) the state reasserted greater economic and political control alongside a more assertive foreign policy.
  • The breakup of Yugoslavia (1991–early 2000s) was the most violent post-communist transition, as resurgent ethnic nationalism produced brutal wars in Croatia and Bosnia (including the Srebrenica genocide of 1995) and later Kosovo, eventually requiring NATO military intervention.
  • By contrast, most Central and Eastern European states — including Poland, Hungary, the Czech Republic, and the Baltic states — completed relatively successful transitions and joined both NATO and the European Union during the 2000s.
  • That European Union was itself transformed by the Maastricht Treaty (1992), which turned the old Economic Community into a full European Union with a common currency, coordinated foreign and security policy, and free movement of people across the Schengen Area.
  • The introduction of the euro (1999/2002) created monetary union without full fiscal or political union — a structural tension whose consequences would become painfully clear in Topic 9.6 — even as the EU's eastward expansion in 2004 and 2007 brought most former Soviet-bloc states fully into Western institutions, completing the reorientation begun by the revolutions of Topic 9.4.

9.6 Contemporary Europe: Globalization, Migration & Open Questions

  • Deepening globalization integrated European economies tightly into global markets — leaving them exposed when the 2008 global financial crisis exposed the euro's structural weakness (a shared currency without a shared fiscal policy), triggering severe sovereign debt crises in Greece, Ireland, Portugal, and Spain that required bailouts and painful austerity measures.
  • The resulting economic strain fed a political backlash across the continent, fueling the rise of anti-establishment and Euroskeptic parties that questioned the value of EU membership and integration itself.
  • Europe also became a major destination for migration — driven by continued movement from former colonies (Topic 9.2), free movement within the expanded EU, and the 2015 migration crisis sparked by the Syrian civil war — which intensified debates over integration, multiculturalism, national identity, and the future of open Schengen borders.
  • These migration debates fueled nationalist and populist movements and echoed much older European questions — going back to the religious conflicts of Unit 2 — about how diverse populations can coexist within a shared political community.
  • Brexit (voted 2016, completed 2020) made the United Kingdom the first member state to leave the EU, reflecting these populist currents and raising sharp questions about the limits of integration that connect directly to the comparison in Topic 9.7.
  • Europe's relationship with Russia has grown openly confrontational — the annexation of Crimea (2014) and the full-scale invasion of Ukraine (2022), the largest European war since 1945, have reshaped European security thinking and renewed NATO's relevance, while the ongoing transition to renewable energy (complicated by past dependence on Russian energy) and the unresolved balance between national sovereignty and supranational cooperation remain defining, open questions for Europe's future.

9.7 Comparison: Visions of European Integration

From Maastricht to Brexit, European politics has been shaped by an enduring debate over how far integration should go — a debate that remains unresolved today.

Pro-Integration ("Federalist") Sovereigntist ("Euroskeptic")
View of National Sovereignty Sovereignty should be pooled for shared benefits Sovereignty should remain with the nation-state
Preferred EU Powers Expand common policies (fiscal, foreign, security) Limit EU role to trade and voluntary cooperation
Economic Policy Shared fiscal tools to support the euro National control over budgets and borders
Example Proposals for an EU finance ministry / deeper union Brexit; Euroskeptic and nationalist parties across the EU

Exam Tip

This debate is the natural endpoint of a theme running through the entire course: the tension between centralized authority and local/national autonomy, from absolutism vs. constitutionalism (Unit 3) through nationalism vs. multinational empire (Unit 7) to the EU vs. the nation-state today. Framing a contemporary issue in terms of a course-long theme is a great way to demonstrate sophistication.

Key Vocabulary

Iron Curtain
Churchill's term for the ideological and physical divide between Western and Soviet-controlled Europe.
NATO / Warsaw Pact
Rival military alliances of the Western and Soviet blocs during the Cold War.
Decolonization
The post-1945 process by which European powers gave up their overseas colonies.
Détente
Period of eased tensions between the superpowers, roughly the 1970s.
Revolutions of 1989
Wave of largely peaceful uprisings that ended communist rule across Eastern Europe.
Maastricht Treaty
1992 treaty that created the European Union from the European Economic Community.
Eurozone
The group of EU member states that have adopted the euro as their currency.
Migration Crisis
The surge of migrants and refugees to Europe, especially from Syria around 2015.
Brexit
The United Kingdom's withdrawal from the European Union, completed in 2020.
